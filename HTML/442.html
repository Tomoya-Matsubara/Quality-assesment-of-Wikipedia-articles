Vladim<U+00ED>r Remek (born 26 September 1948) is a Czech politician and diplomat as well as a former cosmonaut and military pilot. He flew aboard Soyuz 28 from 2 to 10 March 1978, becoming the first Czechoslovak in space and the only Czech in space. As the first cosmonaut from a country other than the Soviet Union or the United States, and with the entry of the Czech Republic into the European Union, Remek is considered to be the first astronaut from the European Union. Remek was a member of the European Parliament between 2004 and 2013 and is also the current Czech Ambassador to Russia.

Early life and military career

Remek was born on 26 September 1948 in the city of <U+010C>esk<U+00E9> Bud<U+011B>jovice.1 He spent two years studying at the observatory in Krav<U+00ED> hora, Brno between 1962 and 1964.2 Remek was influenced by his father, Jozef Remek, himself a military pilot.3 Remek was an active member both in the Pioneers and the Czechoslovak Union of Youth. He studied mathematics and physics at middle school in <U+010C><U+00E1>slav where he earned awards in track running the 400-meter, 800-meter, and 1,500-meter events. Remek graduated in 1966 and proceeded to Vy<U+0161><U+0161><U+00ED> Leteck<U+00E9> U<U+010D>ili<U+0161>t<U+011B>, an aviation school in Ko<U+0161>ice, where he trained in an Aero L-29 Delf<U+00ED>n.4 Remek graduated in 1970 and was commissioned as a lieutenant in the Czechoslovak Air Force.5 Remek served as a fighter pilot, flying MiG-21s in the 1st Fighter Air Regiment.6 In the 1970s Remek married his first wife, Czech actress Hana Davidov<U+00E1>. She was the daughter of politician V<U+00E1>clav David.7 From 1972 to 1976, Remek studied at the Gagarin Air Force Academy. Upon his return to Czechoslovakia in 1976, he was promoted to captain and appointed deputy commander of his fighter regiment, after which Remek went back to Russia to train for the Soviet-led space program. Upon return from space in March 1978, Remek spent time in the <U+010C>SLA staff as the deputy director of the Flight Research Institute in Prague.8 In 1986, Remek became the deputy commander of a flight division based in <U+010C><U+00E1>slav. In 1988 he graduated from Voroshilov-Staff Academy of Soviet Air Force and was appointed to his highest command, as deputy of the 2nd Air Defense Division stationed in Moravia.910 Following the Velvet Revolution in 1989, Remek was relegated to a role as Director of the Museum for Aviation and Astronautics in Prague.11 Following his retirement from the air force in 1995, Remek represented Czech firm CZ Strakonice and joint venture CZ<U+2013>Turbo-GAZ in Moscow.121314

Intercosmos program

Remek (then a Captain) joined the Intercosmos program in 1976; his backup was Old<U+0159>ich Pel<U+010D><U+00E1>k the other Czechoslovak cosmonaut selected to participate with the program. During the flight, Remeck experimented with the Kristall furnace on board the capsule.15 The mission, coinciding with the 30th anniversary of the Soviet-backed 1948 Czechoslovak coup d'<U+00E9>tat, and starring Remek, the son of a Czech mother and Slovak father, had propaganda value in stressing Czechoslovak-Soviet cooperation.16 Remek himself has not denied this although he retains pride in his voyage regardless of the circumstances. He became first cosmonaut from a country other than the Soviet Union or the United States, and with the entry of the Czech Republic into the European Union, Remek is considered to be the first astronaut from the European Union.1718 After Remek's March 1978 flight, he was celebrated in his home country with a series of receptions at factories and other civil workplaces. He was also recognized at a ceremony at Prague Castle as a guest of Gust<U+00E1>v Hus<U+00E1>k, then the general secretary of the Communist Party of Czechoslovakia.19 On 16 March, Remek and Aleksei Gubarev, the other member of the crew, were awarded the medal Hero of the Soviet Union. Czechoslovak reaction to Remek's flight included comments about the media's inundation focused on Remek and the fact that he was only able to journey with a Soviet cosmonaut as if Remek needed a minder. One joke went: "Why didn't the Soviets send up two Czechoslovak cosmonauts? Because they would've landed in West Germany." Remek himself joked that his Soviet counterpart would slap Remek's hands off of controls if he touched anything without permission.20 Jean-Loup Chr<U+00E9>tien would experience this same behavior onboard Soyuz TM-7.2122

Political career

Due in part to his previous business contacts in Russia, Remek was appointed to the Czech Embassy in Moscow as a Trade and Economic Counselor.2324 During the 2004 European Parliament election, Remek was a candidate for the Communist Party of Bohemia and Moravia and, being second on the list behind Miloslav Ransdorf, was elected into the European Parliament.25 During his first term (20 July 2004 to 13 July 2009), Remek was a member of the Confederal Group of the European United Left<U+2013>Nordic Green Left in the European Parliament.26 He was a vocal proponent of the EU's Galileo satellite constellation warning that bureaucratic delay could cede opportunity to the BeiDou, a Chinese competitor.27 He was reelected in 2009. When Petr Kol<U+00E1><U+0159> resigned as the Czech Ambassador to Russia in December 2012, the ambassadorship sat empty for a year until the President of the Czech Republic, Milo<U+0161> Zeman, appointed Remek in January 2014.28 The appointment met with controversy as it was against the wishes of Zeman's Foreign Minister, Karel Schwarzenberg.29 Observers have noted Remek has a friendly history with the Russians and although his communist affiliations are a minority in Russia, his appointment represents Zeman's pragmatic and pro-Russia stance.3031

In popular culture

Czech astronomer Anton<U+00ED>n Mrkos discovered an asteroid in September 1978 and named it 2552 Remek after the cosmonaut.32 Remek is featured in a 2009 independent comedy film called Osadne about three residents from Osadn<U+00E9> that seek out Remek at his office in Brussels to help tourism in their town.33

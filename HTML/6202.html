The May Revolution () was a week-long series of events that took place from May 18 to 25, 1810, in Buenos Aires, capital of the Viceroyalty of the R<U+00ED>o de la Plata. This Spanish colony included roughly the territories of present-day Argentina, Bolivia, Paraguay and Uruguay. The result was the removal of Viceroy Baltasar Hidalgo de Cisneros and the establishment of a local government, the Primera Junta (First Junta), on May 25.

The May Revolution was a direct reaction to Spain's Peninsular War. In 1808, King Ferdinand&nbsp;VII of Spain abdicated in favor of Napoleon, who granted the throne to his brother, Joseph Bonaparte. A Supreme Central Junta led resistance to Joseph's government and the French occupation of Spain, but eventually suffered a series of reversals that resulted in the Spanish loss of the northern half of the country. On February 1, 1810, French troops took Seville and gained control of most of Andalusia. The Supreme Junta retreated to Cadiz and dissolved itself, and the Council of Regency of Spain and the Indies replaced it. News of these events arrived in Buenos Aires on May 18, brought by British ships.

Viceroy Cisneros tried to maintain the political status quo, but a group of criollo lawyers and military officials organized an open cabildo (a special meeting of notables of the city) on May 22 to decide the future of the Viceroyalty. Delegates denied recognition to the Council of Regency in Spain and established a junta to govern in place of Cisneros, since the government that had appointed him Viceroy no longer existed. To maintain a sense of continuity, Cisneros was initially appointed president of the Junta. However, this caused much popular unrest, so he resigned under pressure on May 25. The newly formed government, the Primera Junta, included only representatives from Buenos Aires and invited other cities of the Viceroyalty to send delegates to join them. This resulted in the outbreak of war between the regions that accepted the outcome of the events at Buenos Aires and those that did not.

The May Revolution began the Argentine War of Independence, although no formal declaration of independence was issued at the time and the Primera Junta continued to govern in the name of the deposed king, Ferdinand VII. As similar events occurred in many other cities of the continent, the May Revolution is also considered one of the early events of the Spanish American wars of independence. Historians today debate whether the revolutionaries were truly loyal to the Spanish crown or whether the declaration of fidelity to the king was a necessary ruse to conceal the true objective<U+2014>to achieve independence<U+2014>from a population that was not yet ready to accept such a radical change. A formal declaration of independence was finally issued at the Congress of Tucum<U+00E1>n on July 9, 1816.

Causes

International causes

The United States' declaration of independence from Great Britain in 1776 led criollos (Spanish peoples born in the Americas) to believe that revolution and independence from Spain were feasible.12 Between 1775 and 1783, the American patriots of the Thirteen Colonies waged the American Revolutionary War against both the local loyalists and the Kingdom of Great Britain. The fact that Spain aided the colonies in their struggle against Britain weakened the idea that it would be a crime to end one's allegiance to the parent state.3

The ideals of the French Revolution of 1789 spread across Europe and the Americas as well.4 The overthrow and execution of King Louis XVI and Queen Marie Antoinette ended centuries of monarchy and removed the privileges of the nobility. Liberal ideals in the political and economic fields developed and spread through the Atlantic Revolutions across most of the Western world. The concept of the divine right of kings was questioned by the French Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, by the oft-quoted statement that "all men are created equal" in the United States Declaration of Independence and even by the Spanish church.5

However, the spread of such ideas was forbidden in the Spanish territories, as was the sale of related books or their unauthorized possession.6 Spain instituted those bans when they declared war on France after the execution of Louis XVI and retained them after the peace treaty of 1796.7 News of the events of 1789 and copies of the publications of the French Revolution spread around Spain despite efforts to keep them at bay.89 Many enlightened criollos came into contact with liberal authors and their works during their university studies, either in Europe or at the University of Chuquisaca (modern Sucre).10 Books from the United States found their way into the Spanish colonies through Caracas, owing to the proximity of Venezuela to the United States and the West Indies.11

The Industrial Revolution started in Britain, with the use of plateways, canals and steam power. This led to dramatic increases in the productive capabilities of Britain,12 and created a need for new markets to sell its products.13 The Napoleonic Wars with France made this a difficult task, after Napoleon imposed the Continental System, which forbade his allies and conquests to trade with Britain. Thus Britain needed to be able to trade with the Spanish colonies, but could not do so because the colonies were restricted to trade only with their parent state.14 To achieve their economic objectives, Britain initially tried to invade Rio de la Plata and conquer key cities in Spanish America.15 When that failed, they chose to promote the Spanish-American aspirations of emancipation from Spain.1617

The mutiny of Aranjuez in 1808 led King Charles&nbsp;IV of Spain to abdicate in favor of his son, Ferdinand VII.18 Charles IV requested that Napoleon restore him to the throne; instead, Napoleon crowned his own brother, Joseph Bonaparte, as the new Spanish King.1920 These events are known as the Abdications of Bayonne. Joseph's coronation was met with severe resistance in Spain, which started the Peninsular War, and the Supreme Central Junta took power in the name of the absent king.21 This also led to Spain switching allegiances from France to Britain.22 France eventually invaded Sevilla, and a Council of Regency based in Cadiz replaced the disbanded Supreme Central Junta.23

National causes

Spain forbade its American colonies to trade with other nations or foreign colonies, and imposed itself as the only buyer and vendor for their international trade.24 This situation damaged the viceroyalty, as Spain's economy was not powerful enough to produce the huge supply of goods that the numerous colonies would need. This caused economic shortages and recession.2526 The Spanish trade routes favored the ports of Mexico and Lima, to the detriment of Buenos Aires.27 As a result, Buenos Aires smuggled those products that could not be obtained legitimately.28 Most local authorities allowed this smuggling as a lesser evil, even though it was illegal, and it occasionally equalled in volume the legal commerce with Spain.29 Two antagonistic factions emerged: the landowners wanted free trade so they could sell their products abroad, while the merchants, who benefited from the high prices of smuggled imports, opposed free trade because prices would come down.30

The Spanish monarchy appointed their own candidates to most of the political offices in the viceroyalty, usually favoring Spaniards from Europe.31 In most cases, the appointees had little knowledge of or interest in local issues. Consequently, there was a growing rivalry between criollos and peninsulars (those born in Spain). Most criollos thought that peninsulars had undeserved advantages and received preferential treatment in politics and society.32 The lower clergy had a similar sentiment about the higher echelons of the religious hierarchy.33 Events developed at a slower pace than in the United States independence movement.34 This was in part because the clergy controlled the entire educational system in Spanish America, which led the population to hold the same conservative ideas and follow the same customs as in Spain.35

Buenos Aires and Montevideo successfully resisted two British invasions.36 In 1806, a small British army led by William Carr Beresford seized Buenos Aires for a brief time; a Montevidean army led by Santiago de Liniers liberated the city.37 The following year, a larger army seized Montevideo, but was overwhelmed by the forces of Buenos Aires; the invaders capitulated38 and returned Montevideo to the viceroyalty. There was no aid from Spain during either invasion.3940 Liniers organized criollo militias during the preparations for the second invasion, in spite of the prohibition against them.414243 The Patricios Regiment, led by Cornelio Saavedra, was the biggest criollo army. These events gave criollos military power and political influence that they did not have before and, since the victory was achieved without any help from Spain, it boosted criollo confidence in their independent capabilities.4445

The Portuguese royal family left Europe and settled in colonial Brazil in 1808, after their escape from the Napoleonic invasion of Portugal.46 Carlota Joaquina, sister of Ferdinand VII, was the wife of the Portuguese prince regent, but had her own political projects.47 As she avoided the later capture of the Spanish royal family, she attempted to take charge of the viceroyalty as regent.48 This political project, known as Carlotism, sought to prevent a French invasion of the Americas.49 A small secret society of criollos, composed of politicians such as Manuel Belgrano and Juan Jos<U+00E9> Castelli, and military leaders such as Antonio Beruti and Hip<U+00F3>lito Vieytes, supported this project.5051 They considered it an opportunity to get a local government instead of a European one, or a step towards a potential declaration of independence.5253 The project was resisted by Viceroy Liniers, most peninsulars, and some criollos, including Cornelio Saavedra and the lawyers Mariano Moreno and Juan Jos<U+00E9> Paso.5455 They suspected that it concealed Portuguese expansionist ambitions over the region.56 The supporters of Carlota Joaquina intended her to head a constitutional monarchy, whereas she wanted to govern an absolute monarchy; these conflicting goals undermined the project and led to its failure.5758 Britain, which had a strong influence in the politics of the Portuguese Empire, opposed the project as well: they did not want Spain split into several kingdoms, and considered Carlota Joaquina unable to prevent this.59

Prelude

Liniers government

After the British invasion of 1806, Santiago de Liniers successfully reconquered Buenos Aires.60 The population did not allow Rafael de Sobremonte to continue as Viceroy.61 He had escaped to Cordoba with the public treasury while the battle was still in progress.62 A law enacted in 1778 required the treasury to be moved to a safe location in the case of a foreign attack, but Sobremonte was still seen as a coward by the population.63 The Royal Audiencia of Buenos Aires did not allow his return to Buenos Aires and elected Liniers, acclaimed as a popular hero, as an interim Viceroy.64 This was an unprecedented action, the first time that a Spanish viceroy was deposed by local government institutions, and not by the King of Spain himself;65 King Charles IV ratified the appointment at a later time.66 Liniers armed the entire population of Buenos Aires, including criollos and slaves, and defeated a second British invasion attempt in 1807.67

The Liniers administration was popular among criollos, but not among peninsulars such as the merchant Mart<U+00ED>n de <U+00C1>lzaga and the Governor of Montevideo, Francisco Javier de El<U+00ED>o.68 They requested the Spanish authorities appoint a new viceroy.69 In the wake of the outbreak of the Peninsular War, de El<U+00ED>o created the Junta of Montevideo, which would scrutinise all the orders from Buenos Aires and reserve the right to ignore them, but did not openly deny the authority of the Viceroy or declare Montevideo independent.70

Mart<U+00ED>n de <U+00C1>lzaga began a mutiny to remove Liniers.71 On January 1, 1809, an open cabildo (an extraordinary meeting of vecinos, prominent people of the city) chaired by <U+00C1>lzaga demanded the resignation of Liniers and the appointment of a local junta.72 The Spanish militia and a group of people summoned by the meeting gathered to support the rebellion.73 A small number of criollos, notably Mariano Moreno, supported the mutiny,74 but most of them did not.75 They felt that <U+00C1>lzaga wanted to remove the Viceroy to avoid his political authority while keeping the social differences between criollos and peninsulars unchanged.76 The riot was quickly routed when criollo militias led by Cornelio Saavedra surrounded the plaza and dispersed the insurgents.77 As a result of the failed mutiny, the rebel militias were disarmed.78 This included all peninsular militias, and the power of the criollos increased as a result.79 The leaders of the plot, with the exception of Moreno,80 were exiled to Carmen de Patagones.81 Javier de El<U+00ED>o freed them and gave them political asylum at Montevideo.82

Cisneros government

The Supreme Central Junta replaced Liniers with the naval officer Baltasar Hidalgo de Cisneros, a veteran of the Battle of Trafalgar, to end the political turmoil in the R<U+00ED>o de la Plata.83 He arrived in Montevideo in June 1809 for the handover.84 Manuel Belgrano proposed that Liniers should resist on the grounds that he had been confirmed as Viceroy by a King of Spain, whereas Cisneros lacked such legitimacy.85 The criollo militias shared Belgrano's proposal,86 but Liniers handed over the government to Cisneros without resistance.87 Javier de El<U+00ED>o accepted the authority of the new Viceroy, and dissolved the Junta of Montevideo.88 Cisneros rearmed the disbanded peninsular militias, and pardoned those responsible for the mutiny.89 <U+00C1>lzaga was not freed, but his sentence was commuted to house arrest.90

There was concern about events in Spain and about the legitimacy of local governors in Upper Peru as well.91 On May 25, 1809, the Chuquisaca Revolution deposed Ram<U+00F3>n Garc<U+00ED>a de Le<U+00F3>n y Pizarro as Governor of Chuquisaca and replaced him with Juan Antonio Alvarez de Arenales.92 On July 16, the La Paz revolution, led by Colonel Pedro Domingo Murillo, deposed the Governor of La Paz and elected a new junta.93 A swift reaction from the Spanish authorities defeated those rebellions.94 An army of 1,000 men sent from Buenos Aires found no resistance at Chuquisaca, took control of the city and overthrew the Junta.95 Murillo tried to defend La Paz, but his 800 militiamen were completely outnumbered by the more than 5,000 soldiers sent from Lima.96 He and the other leaders were later beheaded, and their heads were exhibited as a deterrent.97 These measures contrasted sharply with the pardon that Mart<U+00ED>n de <U+00C1>lzaga and others had received after a short time in prison, and the resentment of criollos against the peninsulars deepened.98 Juan Jos<U+00E9> Castelli was present at the deliberations of the University of Chuquisaca, where Bernardo Monteagudo developed the Syllogism of Chuquisaca, a legal explanation to justify self-governance. This influenced his ideas during the "May Week".99

On November 25, 1809, Cisneros created the Political Surveillance Court to persecute afrancesados (supporters of Joseph Bonaparte) and independentists.100 However, he rejected economist Jos<U+00E9> Mar<U+00ED>a Romero's proposal to banish a number of people considered dangerous to the Spanish regime, such as Saavedra, Paso, Vieytes, Castelli and Moreno, among others.101 Romero warned Cisneros against spreading news that might be considered subversive. Criollos felt that soon any pretext would be enough to lead to the outbreak of revolution. In April 1810, Cornelio Saavedra advised to his friends: "it's not time yet, let the figs ripen and then we'll eat them".102 He meant that he would not support rushed actions against the Viceroy, but would do so at a strategically favorable moment, such as when Napoleon's forces gained a decisive advantage in their war against Spain.103

May Week

The May Week was the period of time in Buenos Aires which began with the confirmation of the fall of the Supreme Central Junta and ended with the dismissal of Cisneros and the establishment of the Primera Junta.104

On May 14, 1810, the British war schooner HMS Mistletoe arrived at Buenos Aires with European newspapers that reported the dissolution of the Supreme Central Junta the previous January.105 The city of Seville had been invaded by French armies, which were already dominating most of the Iberian Peninsula.106 The newspapers reported that some of the former members of the Junta had taken refuge on the Isla de Le<U+00F3>n in Cadiz.107 This was confirmed in Buenos Aires on May 17, when the British frigate HMS John Paris arrived in Montevideo; the most recent newspapers reported that members of the Supreme Central Junta had been dismissed.108 The Council of Regency of Cadiz was not seen as a successor of the Spanish resistance but as an attempt to restore absolutism in Spain.109 The Supreme Central Junta was seen as sympathetic to the new ideas.110 South American patriots feared both a complete French victory in the peninsula and an absolutist restoration.111 Cisneros monitored the British warships and seized their newspapers, to conceal the news, but a newspaper came into the hands of Belgrano and Castelli.112 They spread the news among other patriots and challenged the legitimacy of the Viceroy, who had been appointed by the fallen junta.113 When Cornelio Saavedra, head of the regiment of Patricians, was informed of this news, he decided that it was finally the ideal time to take action against Cisneros.114 Mart<U+00ED>n Rodr<U+00ED>guez proposed to overthrow the Viceroy by force, but Castelli and Saavedra rejected this idea and proposed the convening of an open cabildo.115

Friday, May 18 and Saturday, May 19

Although Viceroy Cisneros attempted to conceal the news of the Spanish defeat, the rumor had already spread throughout Buenos Aires.116 Most of the population was uneasy; there was high activity at the barracks and in the Plaza, and most shops were closed.117 The "Caf<U+00E9> de Catalanes" and the "Fonda de las Naciones", frequent criollo meeting places, became venues for political discussions and radical proclamations; Francisco Jos<U+00E9> Planes shouted that Cisneros should be hanged in the Plaza as retribution for the execution of the leaders of the ill-fated La Paz revolution.118

The Viceroy, trying to calm the criollos, gave his own version of events in a proclamation.119 He asked for allegiance to King Ferdinand VII, but popular unrest continued to intensify. He was aware of the news, but only said that the situation on the Iberian Peninsula was delicate; he did not confirm the fall of the Junta.120 His proposal was to make a government body that would rule on behalf of Ferdinand VII, together with Viceroy of Peru Jos<U+00E9> Fernando de Abascal y Sousa, Governor of Potos<U+00ED> Francisco de Paula Sanz and President of the Royal Audiencia of Charcas Vicente Nieto.Nicol<U+00E1>s Rodr<U+00ED>guez Pe<U+00F1>a and Mart<U+00ED>n Rodr<U+00ED>guez.121 During these secret meetings, they appointed a representative commission composed of Juan Jos<U+00E9> Castelli and Mart<U+00ED>n Rodr<U+00ED>guez to request that Cisneros convene an open cabildo to decide the future of the Viceroyalty.122

During the night of May 19 there were further discussions at Rodr<U+00ED>guez Pe<U+00F1>a's house.pez31"/> which involved military and civilian leaders.123 They arranged that Belgrano and Saavedra would meet with Juan Jos<U+00E9> de Lezica, the senior alcalde (municipal magistrate), while Castelli would meet with the procurator Juli<U+00E1>n de Leiva, to ask for their support.pez31"/> Saavedra commented to Lezica that he was suspected of betrayal because of his constant requests for cautious and measured steps.124 This comment was designed to pressure Lezica into speeding up the legal system to allow the people to express themselves, or otherwise risk a major rebellion.125 Lezica asked for patience and time to persuade the Viceroy, and leave a massive demonstration as a last resort.126 He argued that if the Viceroy was deposed in that way, it would constitute a rebellion, which would turn the revolutionaries into outlaws.127 Manuel Belgrano gave the following Monday as the deadline to confirm the open cabildo before taking direct action.128 Leiva would later act as a mediator, being both a confidante of Cisneros and a trusted negotiator for the more moderate revolutionaries.129

Sunday, May 20

Lezica informed Cisneros of the request for an open cabildo and the Viceroy consulted Leiva, who spoke in favor of it.130 The Viceroy summoned military commanders to come to the fort at 7 pm,131 to demand military support.132 There were rumors that it could be a trap to capture them and take control of the barracks.133 To prevent this, they took command of the grenadiers that guarded the Fort and seized the keys of all entrances while meeting with the Viceroy.134 Colonel Cornelio Saavedra, head of the Regiment of Patricios, responded on behalf of all the criollo regiments.135 He compared the current international situation with that prevailing at the time of the mutiny of <U+00C1>lzaga over a year earlier, pointed out that Spain was now almost entirely under Napoleonic control and that the undefeated Spanish provinces were very small in comparison with the Americas.136 He rejected the claim of sovereignty of Cadiz over the Americas,137 and concluded that the local armies wanted to look after themselves, rather than following the fate of Spain.138 Finally, he pointed out that the Supreme Central Junta that appointed Cisneros as Viceroy no longer existed, so he rejected Cisneros' legitimacy as Viceroy and denied him the protection of the troops under his command.139

Castelli and Mart<U+00ED>n Rodr<U+00ED>guez moved to the Fort for an interview with Cisneros.140 Juan Florencio Terrada, commander of the Infantry Grenadiers, joined them, because their barracks were located under Cisneros' window, and his presence would not allow the Viceroy to request military aid to take Castelli and Mart<U+00ED>n Rodr<U+00ED>guez prisoners.141 The guards let them pass unannounced, and they found Cisneros playing cards with Brigadier Quintana, prosecutor Caspe and aide Coicolea.142 Castelli and Rodr<U+00ED>guez demanded once again the convening of an open cabildo, and Cisneros reacted angrily, considering their request an outrage.143 Rodr<U+00ED>guez interrupted him and forced him to give a definitive answer.144 After a short private discussion with Caspe, Cisneros reluctantly gave his consent.145

That night, many of the revolutionaries attended a theatre production on the theme of tyranny, called Rome Saved.146 The lead actor was Morante, playing Cicero.147 The police chief requested Morante to feign illness and not appear, so that the play could be replaced with Misanthropy and Repentance by the German novelist and playwright August von Kotzebue.148 Rumors of police censorship spread quickly; Morante ignored the request and performed the play as planned.149 In the fourth act, Morante made a patriotic speech, about the Gaul threat to Rome (the Gauls are ancestors of the French people) and the need for strong leadership to resist the danger.150 This scene lifted the revolutionaries' spirits and led to frenzied applause.151 Juan Jos<U+00E9> Paso stood up and cried out for the freedom of Buenos Aires, and a small fight ensued.152

After the play, the revolutionaries returned to Pe<U+00F1>a's house.153 They learned the result of the meeting with Cisneros, but were unsure as to whether Cisneros intended to keep his word.154 They organized a demonstration for the following day to ensure that the open cabildo would be held as decided.155

Monday, May 21

At 3 pm, the Cabildo began its routine work, but was interrupted by 600 armed men named the Infernal Legion, who occupied the Plaza de la Victoria and loudly demanded the convening of an open cabildo and the resignation of Viceroy Cisneros.156 They carried a portrait of Ferdinand VII and the lapels of their jackets bore a white ribbon that symbolized criollo<U+2013>Spanish unity.157 Domingo French, the mail carrier of the city, and Antonio Beruti, an employee of the treasury, led the rioters.158 It was rumored that Cisneros had been killed, and that Saavedra would take control of the government.159 Saavedra was at the barracks at that moment, concerned about the demonstration.160 He thought the violence should be stopped and that radical measures such as the assassination of Cisneros should be prevented, but he also thought that the troops would mutiny if the demonstrations were suppressed.161 The people in the Plaza did not believe that Cisneros would allow the open cabildo the next day.162 Leiva left the Cabildo, and Belgrano, who was representing the crowd, requested a definitive commitment.163 Leiva explained that everything would go ahead as planned, but the Cabildo needed time to prepare.164 He asked Belgrano to help the Cabildo with the work, as his intervention would be seen by the crowd as a guarantee that their demands would not be ignored.165 The crowd left the main hall but stayed in the Plaza.166 Belgrano protested about the guest list, which consisted of the wealthiest citizens, and thought that if the poor people were left outside there would be further unrest.167 The members of the Cabildo tried to convince him to give his support, but he left.168

Belgrano's departure enraged the crowd, as he did not explain what had happened, and the people feared a betrayal.169 Demands for Cisneros' immediate resignation replaced those for an open cabildo.170 The people finally settled down and dispersed when Saavedra intervened to say that the claims of the Infernal Legion were supported by the military.171

The invitations were distributed among 450 leading citizens and officials in the capital.172 The Cabildo compiled the guest list, and tried to guarantee the result, inviting people that would be likely to support the Viceroy.173 The revolutionaries countered this move with a similar one, so that most people would be against Cisneros instead.174 The printer Agust<U+00ED>n Donado, supporting the revolutionaries, printed nearly 600 invitations instead of the 450 requested, and distributed the surplus among the criollos.175 During the night, Castelli, Rodr<U+00ED>guez, French and Beruti visited all the barracks to harangue the troops and prepare them for the following day.176

Tuesday, May 22

According to the minutes, only about 251 out of the 450 officially invited guests attended the open cabildo.177178 French and Beruti, in command of 600 men armed with knives, shotguns and rifles, controlled access to the square to ensure that the open cabildo had a majority of criollos.179180 All noteworthy religious and civilian people were present, as well as militia commanders and many prominent residents.181 The only notable absence was that of Mart<U+00ED>n de <U+00C1>lzaga, who was still under house arrest.182

A merchant, Jos<U+00E9> Ignacio Rez<U+00E1>bal, attended the open cabildo but, in a letter to the priest Juli<U+00E1>n S. de Ag<U+00FC>ero, said that he had some doubts which were shared by other people close to him.183 He feared that, no matter which party prevailed in the open cabildo, it would take revenge against the other, the Mutiny of <U+00C1>lzaga being a recent precedent.pez5153"/>

The meeting lasted from morning to midnight, including the reading of the proclamation, the debate and the vote.184 There was no secret ballot; votes were heard one at a time and recorded in the minutes.185 The main themes of the debate were the legitimacy of the government and the authority of the Viceroy.186 The principle of retroversion of the sovereignty to the people stated that, in the absence of the legitimate monarch, power returned to the people; they were entitled to form a new government.187 This principle was commonplace in Spanish scholasticism and rationalist philosophy, but had never been applied in case law.188 Its validity divided the assembly into two main groups: one group rejected it and argued that the situation should remain unchanged; this group supported Cisneros as Viceroy. The other group supported change, and considered that they should establish a junta, like the ones established in Spain to replace the Viceroy.189 There was also a third position, taking the middle ground.190 The promoters of change did not recognize the authority of the Council of Regency, and argued that the colonies in America were not consulted in its formation.191 The debate tangentially discussed the rivalry between criollos and peninsulars; the Viceroy supporters felt that the will of peninsulars should prevail over that of criollos.192

One of the speakers for the first position was the bishop of Buenos Aires, Benito Lue y Riega, leader of the local church, who said:

Not only is there no reason to get rid of the Viceroy, but even if no part of Spain remained unsubdued, the Spaniards in America ought to take it back and resume command over it. America should only be ruled by the natives when there is no longer a Spaniard there. If even a single member of the Central Junta of Seville were to land on our shores, we should receive him as the Sovereign.193

Juan Jos<U+00E9> Castelli was the main speaker for the revolutionaries.194 He based his speech on two key ideas: the government's lapsed legitimacy<U+2014>he stated that the Supreme Central Junta was dissolved and had no rights to designate a Regency<U+2014>and the principle of retroversion of sovereignty.195 He spoke after Riega, and replied that the American people should assume control of their government until Ferdinand VII could return to the throne.

Nobody could call the whole nation a criminal, nor the individuals that have aired their political views. If the right of conquest belongs by right to the conquering country, it would be fair for Spain to quit resisting the French and submit to them, by the same principles for which it is expected that the Americans submit themselves to the peoples of Pontevedra. The reason and the rule must be equal for everybody. Here there are no conquerors or conquered; here there are only Spaniards. The Spaniards of Spain have lost their land. The Spaniards of America are trying to save theirs. Let the ones from Spain deal with themselves as they can; do not worry, we American Spaniards know what we want and where we go. So I suggest we vote: that we replace the Viceroy with a new authority that will be subject to the parent state if it is saved from the French, and independent if Spain is finally subjugated.196

Pascual Ruiz Huidobro stated that, since the authority that appointed Cisneros had expired, Cisneros should no longer have a place in the government.197 Huidobro felt that the Cabildo should be in government, as it was the representative of the people.198 Melchor Fern<U+00E1>ndez, Juan Le<U+00F3>n Ferragut and Joaqu<U+00ED>n Grigera supported his vote, among others.199

Attorney Manuel Genaro Villota, representative of the conservative Spanish, said that the city of Buenos Aires had no right to make unilateral decisions about the legitimacy of the Viceroy or the Council of Regency without the participation of other cities of the Viceroyalty.200 He argued that such an action would break the unity of the country and establish as many sovereignties as there were cities.201 His intention was to keep Cisneros in power by delaying any possible action.202 Juan Jos<U+00E9> Paso accepted his first point, but argued that the situation in Europe and the possibility that Napoleon's forces could conquer the American colonies demanded an urgent resolution.203 He then expounded the "argument of the elder sister", reasoning that Buenos Aires should take the initiative and make the changes deemed necessary and appropriate, on the express condition that the other cities would be invited to comment as soon as possible.204 The rhetorical device of the "elder sister", comparable to negotiorum gestio,205 makes an analogy between the relationship of Buenos Aires and other cities of the viceroyalty with a sibling relationship.206

The priest Juan Nepomuceno Sol<U+00E1> then proposed that the Cabildo should receive the provisional command, until the formation of a governing junta made up of representatives from all populations of the Viceroyalty.207 Manuel Alberti, Miguel de Azcu<U+00E9>naga (who would be members of the Primera Junta some days later), Escalada and Argerich (or Aguirre) supported his vote, among others.208

Cornelio Saavedra suggested that the Cabildo should receive the provisional command until the formation of a governing junta in the manner and form that the Cabildo would deem as appropriate.209 He said "...there shall be no doubt that it is the people that create authority or command."210 At the time of the vote, Castelli's position coincided with that of Saavedra.211

Manuel Belgrano stood near a window and, in the event of a problematic development, he would give a signal by waiving a white cloth, upon which the people gathered in the Plaza would force their way into the Cabildo.212 However, there were no problems and this emergency plan was not implemented.213 The historian Vicente Fidel L<U+00F3>pez revealed that his father, Vicente L<U+00F3>pez y Planes, who was present at the event, saw that Mariano Moreno was worried near the end in spite of the majority achieved.214 Moreno told Planes that the Cabildo was about to betray them.215216

Wednesday, May 23

The debate took all day, and the votes were counted very late that night.217 After the presentations, people voted for the continuation of the Viceroy, alone or at the head of a junta, or his dismissal. The ideas explained were divided into a small number of proposals, designated with the names of their main supporters, and the people then voted for one of those proposals. The voting lasted for a long time, and the result was to dismiss the Viceroy by a large majority: 155 votes to 69.218

Manuel Jos<U+00E9> Reyes stated that he found no reason to depose the Viceroy, and that it would be enough to appoint a junta headed by Cisneros.219 His proposal had nearly 30 votes.220 Another 30 votes supported Cisneros, with no change to the political system.221 A small group supported the proposal of Mart<U+00ED>n Jos<U+00E9> de Choteco, who also supported Cisneros.222

There were also many different proposals involving the removal of Cisneros.223 Many of them required the new authorities to be elected by the Cabildo.224 Pascual Ruiz Huidobro proposed that the Cabildo should rule in the interim and appoint a new government, but this proposal made no reference to popular sovereignty or the creation of a junta.225 This proposal received 35 votes, and sought simply to replace Cisneros with Huidobro: Huidobro was the most senior military officer, and thus the natural candidate under current laws to replace the viceroy in the lack of a new appointment from Spain.226 Juan Nepomuceno Sol<U+00E1> proposed a junta composed of delegates from all the provinces of the viceroyalty, while the Cabildo should govern in the interim; this proposal received nearly 20 votes.227 Cornelio Saavedra, whose aformentioned proposal was that the Cabildo should appoint a Junta and rule in the interim, got the largest number of votes.228 A number of other proposals received only a few votes each.229

At dawn on May 23, the Cabildo informed the population that the Viceroy would end his mandate. The highest authority would be transferred temporarily to the Cabildo until the appointment of a governing junta.230 Notices were placed at various points throughout the city, which announced the imminent creation of a junta and the summoning of representatives from the provinces.231 The notices also called for the public to refrain from actions contrary to public policy.232

Thursday, May 24

The Cabildo interpreted the decision of the open cabildo in its own way.233 When it formed the new Junta to govern until the arrival of representatives from other cities, Leiva arranged for former viceroy Cisneros to be appointed president of the Junta and commander of the armed forces.234235 There are many interpretations of his motives for departing from the decision of the open cabildo in this way.236237 Four other members were appointed to the Junta: criollos Cornelio Saavedra and Juan Jos<U+00E9> Castelli, and peninsulars Juan Nepomuceno Sol<U+00E1> and Jos<U+00E9> Santos Inchaurregui.238

Leiva wrote a constitutional code to regulate the actions of the Junta.239 It stipulated that the Junta could not exercise judicial power, which was reserved for the Royal Audiencia of Buenos Aires; that Cisneros could not act without the support of the other members of the Junta; that the Cabildo could dismiss anyone who neglected his duty; that the Cabildo's consent would be required to create new taxes; that the Junta would sanction a general amnesty for those who had aired opinions at the open cabildo; and that the Junta would invite the other cities to send delegates.240 The commanders of the armed forces, including Saavedra and Pedro Andr<U+00E9>s Garc<U+00ED>a, agreed to this code.241 The Junta swore the oath of office that afternoon.242

These developments shocked the revolutionaries.243 Unsure of what to do next, they feared that they would be punished, like the revolutionaries of Chuquisaca and La Paz.244 Moreno abjured relations with the others and shut himself in his home.245 There was a meeting at Rodr<U+00ED>guez Pe<U+00F1>a's house.246 They felt that the Cabildo would not pursue such a plot without the blessing of Saavedra and that Castelli should resign from the Junta.247 Tagle took a different view: he thought that Saavedra may have accepted out of weakness or naivety and that Castelli should stay in the Junta to counter the others' influence on him.248 Meanwhile, a mob led by Domingo French and Antonio Beruti filled the Plaza. The stability of Cisneros in power, albeit in an office other than Viceroy, was seen as an insult to the will of the open cabildo.249 Colonel Mart<U+00ED>n Rodriguez warned that, if the army were to commit support to a government that kept Cisneros, they would soon have to fire on the people, and that they would revolt.250 He said that "everyone without exception" demanded the removal of Cisneros.251

That night, Castelli and Saavedra informed Cisneros of their resignation from the newly formed Junta.252 They explained that the population was on the verge of violent revolution and would remove Cisneros by force if he did not resign as well.253 They warned that they did not have the power to stop that: neither Castelli to stop his friends, nor Saavedra to prevent the Regiment of Patricians from mutiny.254 Cisneros wanted to wait for the following day, but they said that there was no time for further delays, so he finally agreed to resign.255 He sent a resignation letter to the Cabildo for consideration on the following day.256 Chiclana felt encouraged when Saavedra resigned, and started to request signatures for a manifesto about the will of the people.257 Moreno refused any further involvement, but Castelli and Pe<U+00F1>a trusted that he would eventually join them if events unfolded as they expected.258

Friday, May 25

On the morning of May 25, in spite of bad weather,259 a crowd gathered in the Plaza de la Victoria, as did the militia led by Domingo French and Antonio Beruti.260 They demanded the recall of the Junta elected the previous day, the final resignation of Cisneros, and the appointment of a new junta that did not include him.261262 Historian Bartolom<U+00E9> Mitre stated that French and Beruti distributed blue and white ribbons, similar to the modern cockade of Argentina, among those present.263 Later historians doubt it, but consider it possible that the revolutionaries used distinctive marks of some kind for identification.264 It was rumored that the Cabildo might reject Cisneros' resignation.265 Because of delays in issuing an official resolution, the crowd became agitated, clamoring that "the people want to know what is going on!".266

The Cabildo met at 9 am and rejected Cisneros' resignation.267 They considered that the crowd had no legitimate right to influence something that the Cabildo had already decided and implemented.268 They considered that, as the Junta was in command, the demonstration should be suppressed by force, and made the members responsible for any changes to the resolution of the previous day.269 To enforce those orders, they summoned the chief commanders, but these did not obey.270 Many of them, including Saavedra, did not appear.271 Those that did stated that they could not support the government order, and that the commanders would be disobeyed if they ordered the troops to repress the demonstrators.272

The crowd's agitation increased, and they overran the chapter house.273 Leiva and Lezica requested that someone who could act as spokesman for the people should join them inside the hall and explain the people's desires.274 Beruti, Chiclana, French and Grela were allowed to pass.275 Leiva attempted to discourage the rioter Pancho Planes, but he entered the hall as well.276 The Cabildo argued that Buenos Aires had no right to break the political system of the viceroyalty without discussing it with the other provinces; French and Chiclana replied that the call for a Congress had already been considered.277 The Cabildo called the commanders to deliberate with them.278 As had happened several times in the last few days, Romero explained that the soldiers would mutiny if forced to fight against the rioters on behalf of Cisneros.279 The Cabildo still refused to give up, until the noise of the demonstration was heard in the hall.280 They feared that the demonstrators could overrun the building and reach them.281 Mart<U+00ED>n Rodr<U+00ED>guez pointed out that the only way to calm the demonstrators was to accept Cisneros' resignation.282 Leiva agreed, convinced the other members, and the people returned to the Plaza.283 Rodr<U+00ED>guez headed to Azcuenaga's house to meet the other revolutionaries to plan the final stages of the revolution.284 The demonstration overran the Cabildo again, and reached the hall of deliberations.285 Beruti spoke on behalf of the people, and said that the new Junta should be elected by the people and not by the Cabildo.286 He said that, besides the nearly 400 people already gathered, the barracks were full of people who supported them them, and he threatened that they would take control, by force if necessary.287 The Cabildo replied by requesting their demands in writing.288

After a long interval, a document containing 411 signatures was delivered to the Cabildo.289 This paper proposed a new composition for the governing Junta, and a 500-man expedition to assist the provinces. The document<U+2014>still preserved<U+2014>listed most army commanders and many well-known residents, and contained many illegible signatures. French and Beruti signed the document, stating "for me and for six hundred more".290 However, there is no unanimous view among historians about the authorship of the document.291 Meanwhile, the weather improved and the sun broke through the clouds.292 The people in the plaza saw it as a favorable omen for the revolution.293 The Sun of May was created a few years later with reference to this event.294

The Cabildo accepted the document and moved to the balcony to submit it directly to the people for ratification.295 But, because of the late hour and the weather, the number of people in the plaza had declined.296 Leiva ridiculed the claim of the remaining representatives to speak on behalf of the people.297 This wore the patience of the few who were still in the plaza in the rain.298 Beruti did not accept any further delays, and threatened to call people to arms.299 Facing the prospect of further violence, the popular request was read aloud and immediately ratified by those present.300

The Primera Junta was finally established. It was composed by president Cornelio Saavedra, members Manuel Alberti, Miguel de Azcu<U+00E9>naga, Manuel Belgrano, Juan Jos<U+00E9> Castelli, Domingo Matheu and Juan Larrea, and secretaries Juan Jos<U+00E9> Paso and Mariano Moreno. The rules governing it were roughly the same as those issued the day before, with the additional provisions that the Cabildo would watch over the members of the Junta and that the Junta itself would appoint replacements in case of vacancies.301 Saavedra spoke to the crowd, and then moved on to the Fort, among salvos of artillery and the ringing of bells.302 Meanwhile, Cisneros dispatched a post rider to C<U+00F3>rdoba, Argentina, to warn Santiago de Liniers about what had happened in Buenos Aires and to request military action against the Junta.303

Aftermath

Buenos Aires endured the whole Spanish American Wars of independence without being reconquered by royalist armies or successful royalist counter-revolutions.304 However, it faced several internal conflicts.305 The May Revolution lacked a clear leader as other regions of Latin America; the secretary Mariano Moreno led the initial phase of the government, but he was removed shortly afterwards.306

The Council of Regency, the Royal Audiencia of Buenos Aires and the peninsulars opposed the new situation.307 The Royal Audiencia secretly swore allegiance to the Council of Regency a month later and sent communiqu<U+00E9>s to the other cities of the Viceroyalty, to request them to deny recognition to the new government.308 To put an end to these activities, the Junta assembled Cisneros and all the members of the Royal Audiencia on the pretext that their lives were in danger, and sent them into exile aboard the British ship Dart.309 Captain Mark Brigut Larrea was instructed to avoid American ports and deliver all of them directly to the Canary Islands. The Junta then appointed a new Audiencia composed entirely of criollos loyal to the revolution.310311

Every city in the territory of modern Argentina other than C<U+00F3>rdoba endorsed the Primera Junta.312 The cities of the Upper Peru, however, did not take a position, owing to the recent outcomes of the Chuquisaca and La Paz Revolutions. Asunci<U+00F3>n del Paraguay rejected the Junta and swore loyalty to the Council of Regency.313 The Banda Oriental, under Francisco Javier de El<U+00ED>o, remained a royalist stronghold.314

Former Viceroy Santiago de Liniers organized a counter-revolution in C<U+00F3>rdoba, and this became the first military campaign of the independent government.315 Despite the importance of Liniers himself, and his prestige as a popular hero for his role when the British invaded, the population of C<U+00F3>rdoba preferred to support the revolution.316 This reduced the power of the counter-revolutionary army by means of desertions and sabotage.317318 Liniers's troops were quickly defeated by the forces led by Francisco Ortiz de Ocampo.319 Ocampo refused to shoot the captive Liniers; hence the execution ordered by the Junta was carried out by Juan Jos<U+00E9> Castelli.320 After the victory, the Primera Junta sent military expeditions to many other cities, to demand support and the election of representatives to it.321

Montevideo, which had a historical rivalry with Buenos Aires, opposed the Primera Junta and the Council of Regency declared it the new capital of the Viceroyalty, along with Francisco Javier de El<U+00ED>o as the new Viceroy.322 The city was well defended, so it could easily resist an invasion. Peripheral cities in the Banda Oriental acted contrary to Montevideo's will and supported the Buenos Aires Junta.323 Jos<U+00E9> Gervasio Artigas led them, and kept Montevideo under siege.324 The final defeat of the Montevidean royalists was carried out by Carlos Mar<U+00ED>a de Alvear and William Brown.325

The Captaincy General of Chile followed a process analogous to that of the May Revolution, and elected a Government Junta that inaugurated the brief period known as Patria Vieja. The Junta was defeated in 1814 at the Battle of Rancagua, and the subsequent Reconquista of Chile would make it a royalist stronghold once more. The Andes provided an effective natural barrier between the Argentine revolutionaries and Chile, so there was no military confrontation between them until the Crossing of the Andes, led by Jos<U+00E9> de San Mart<U+00ED>n in 1817, a campaign that resulted in the defeat of the Chilean royalists.326

The Primera Junta increased in size when it incorporated the representatives sent by the provinces.327 From then on, the Junta was renamed the Junta Grande.328 It was dissolved shortly after the June 1811 defeat of the Argentine troops at the Battle of Huaqui, and two successive triumvirates exercised executive power over the United Provinces of the R<U+00ED>o de la Plata.329 In 1814, the second triumvirate was replaced by the authority of the Supreme Director.330 Meanwhile, Mart<U+00ED>n Miguel de G<U+00FC>emes contained the royalist armies sent from the Viceroyalty of Peru at Salta, while San Mart<U+00ED>n advanced towards the royalist stronghold of Lima by sea, on a Chilean<U+2013>Argentine campaign. The war for independence gradually shifted towards northern South America.331 From 1814, Argentina descended into civil war.332

Consequences

According to historian F<U+00E9>lix Luna's Breve historia de los Argentinos (), one of the most important societal consequences of the May Revolution was the shift in the way the people and its rulers related.333 Until then, the conception of the common good prevailed: while royal authority was fully respected, if an instruction from the crown of Spain was considered detrimental to the common good of the local population, it was half-met or simply ignored.334 With the revolution, the concept of common good gave way to that of popular sovereignty, as theorized by Moreno, Castelli and Monteagudo, among others.335 This idea held that, in the absence of a legitimate authority, the people had the right to appoint their own leaders.336 Over time, popular sovereignty would give way to the idea of majority rule.337 This maturation of ideas was gradual, taking many decades to crystallize into stable electoral and political systems, but it was what ultimately led to the adoption of the republican system as the form of government for Argentina.338 Domingo Faustino Sarmiento stated similar views in his Facundo, and noted that cities were more receptive to republican ideas, while rural areas were more resistant to them, which led to the surge of caudillos.339

Another consequence, also according to Luna, was the dissolution of the Viceroyalty of the R<U+00ED>o de la Plata into several different units.340 Most of the cities and provinces had distinctive populations, economies, attitudes, contexts, and interests.341 Until the revolution, all of these peoples were held together by the authority of the Spanish government, but with its disappearance, people from Montevideo, Paraguay and the Upper Peru began to distance themselves from Buenos Aires.342 The brief existence of the Viceroyalty of the Rio de la Plata, which had lasted barely 38 years, impeded the consolidation of a patriotic feeling and failed to bring a sense of community to all of the population.343 The new country of Argentina lacked an established concept of national identity capable to unite the population under a common idea of statehood.344 Juan Bautista Alberdi sees the May Revolution as one of the early manifestations of the power struggles between the city of Buenos Aires and the provinces<U+2014>one of the axial conflicts at play in the Argentine civil wars.345 Alberdi wrote in his book "Escritos p<U+00F3>stumos":

The revolution of May 1810 in Buenos Aires, intended to win the independence of Argentina from Spain, also had the consequence of emancipating the province of Buenos Aires from Argentina or, rather, of imposing the authority of this province upon the whole nation emancipated from Spain. That day, Spanish power over the Argentine provinces ended and that of Buenos Aires was established.346

Historical perspectives

Historiographical studies of the May Revolution do not face many doubts or unknown details. Most of the information was properly recorded at the time and was made available to the public by the Primera Junta as patriotic propaganda. Because of this, historical views on the topic differ in their interpretations of the meanings, causes and consequences of the events, rather than in the accuracy of their depiction of the events themselves. The modern version of events does not differ significantly from the contemporary one.347

The first people to write about the May Revolution were participants who wrote memoirs, biographies and diaries.348 However, their works were motivated by purposes other than historiographic ones, such as to explain the reasons for their actions, clean their public images, or express their support or rejection of the public figures and ideas of the time.349 For example, Manuel Moreno wrote the biography of his brother Mariano as propaganda for the revolutions in Europe,350 and Cornelio Saavedra wrote his autobiography at a moment when his image was highly questioned, to justify himself to his sons.351

The first remarkable historiographical school of interpretation of the history of Argentina was founded by members of the 1837 generation, including Bartolom<U+00E9> Mitre.352 Mitre regarded the May Revolution as an iconic expression of political egalitarianism: a conflict between modern freedoms and oppression represented by the Spanish monarchy, and an attempt to establish a national organization on constitutional principles as opposed to the charismatic authority of the caudillos.353 These authors' views were treated as canonical until the end of the 19th century, when the proximity of the centennial encouraged authors to seek new perspectives.354 The newer authors would differ about the relative weight of the causes of the May Revolution and about whose intervention in the events was more decisive, but the main views expressed by Mitre were kept,355 such as to consider the revolution to be the birth of modern Argentina356 and an unavoidable event.357 These authors introduced the idea of popular intervention as another key element.358 By the time of the World Wars, liberal authors attempted to impose an ultimate and unquestionable historical perspective; Ricardo Levene and the Academia Nacional de la Historia were exponents of this tendency, which still kept most perspectives of Mitre.359 Left-wing authors took a revisionist view based on nationalism and anti-imperialism; they minimized the dispute between criollos and peninsulars and portrayed events as a dispute between enlightenment and absolutism.360 However, most of their work was focused on other historical periods.361

The May Revolution was not the product of the actions of a single political party with a clear and defined agenda, but a convergence of sectors with varying interests.362 Thus, there are a number of conflicting perspectives about it, because different authors highlight different aspects.363 Mitre, for example, referred to The Representation of the Landowners (an 1809 economic report by Mariano Moreno) and the role of the merchants to support the view that the May Revolution intended to obtain free trade and economic integration with Europe;364 right-wing revisionists center around Saavedra and the social customs of the time to describe the revolution under conservative principles;365 and left-wing revisionists use the example of Moreno, Castelli and the rioters led by French and Beruti to describe it as a radical revolution.366

Revolutionary purposes

The government created on May 25 pronounced itself loyal to the deposed King of Spain Ferdinand VII, but historians disagree on whether this was sincere or not.367 Since Mitre, many historians think that this professed loyalty was merely a political deception to gain autonomy.368369370371 The Primera Junta did not pledge allegiance to the Council of Regency, which was still in operation, and in 1810 it still seemed unlikely that Napoleon would be defeated and Ferdinand returned to the throne (which finally happened on December 11, 1813, with the Treaty of Valen<U+00E7>ay).372 The purpose of such a deception would have been to gain time to strengthen the position of the patriotic cause and avoid reactions that may have led to a counter-revolution, by making it appear that monarchical authority was still respected and that no revolution had taken place. The ruse is known as the "Mask of Ferdinand VII". It was upheld by the Primera Junta, the Junta Grande and the First Triumvirate. The Assembly of Year XIII was intended to declare independence, but failed to do so because of other political conflicts between its members.373 However, it suppressed mention of Ferdinand VII in official documents.374 Before the declaration of independence of 1816, the supreme directors considered other options, such as to negotiate with Spain or become a British protectorate.375

The change was potentially favorable for Britain, as trade with the cities of the area was facilitated, without the monopoly that Spain had maintained over their colonies for centuries.376 However, Britain's first priority was the war against France in Europe, and they could not appear to support American independence movements or allow the military attention of Spain to be divided onto two different fronts.377 Consequently, they pushed to avoid explicit independence demonstrations.378 This pressure was exerted by Lord Strangford, the British ambassador at the court of Rio de Janeiro; he expressed support for the Junta, but under the condition that "...the behavior is consistent, and that [the] Capital [is] retained on behalf of Mr. Dn. Ferdinand VII and his legitimate successors".379 Later conflicts between Buenos Aires, Montevideo and Artigas led to internal conflicts on the British front, between Strangford and the Portuguese regent John VI of Portugal.380

Juan Bautista Alberdi and later historians such as Norberto Galasso,381 Luis Romero and Jos<U+00E9> Carlos Chiaramonte382 doubted Mitre's interpretation and put forward different ones. Alberdi thought that "the Argentine revolution is a chapter of the Hispanoamerican revolution, as also of the Spanish one, as also of the French and European one".383 They did not consider it a dispute between independentism and colonialism, but instead a dispute between new libertarian ideas and absolutism.384 The intention was not to cut ties with Spain, but to reformulate the relationship; similarly, the American Revolution was not separatist at its initial steps either.385 Thus, it would have the characteristics of a civil war instead.386 Some points that would justify the idea would be the inclusion of Larrea, Matheu, and Belgrano in the Junta and the later appearance of Jos<U+00E9> de San Mart<U+00ED>n: Larrea and Matheu were Spanish, Belgrano studied for many years in Spain,387 and San Mart<U+00ED>n had spent most of his adult life waging war in Spain against the French.388 When San Mart<U+00ED>n talked about enemies, he called them "royalists" or "Goths", but never "Spanish".389

According to those historians, the Spanish revolution against absolutism got mixed up with the Peninsular War.390 When Ferdinand VII stood against his father Charles IV, who was seen as an absolutist king, many Spaniards got the mistaken impression that he sympathized with the new enlightened ideas.391 Thus, the revolutions made in the Americas in the name of Ferdinand VII (such as the May Revolution, the Chuquisaca Revolution, or the one in Chile) would have sought to replace absolutist power with power formulated under the new ideas.392 Even if Spain was at war with France, the ideals of the French Revolution (liberty, equality and fraternity) were still respected.393 Those revolutions pronounced themselves enemies of Napoleon, but did not face any active French military attack; they promoted instead fights between Spanish armies for keeping either the old or new order.394 This situation would change with the final defeat of Napoleon and the return of Ferdinand VII to the throne, as he began the Absolutist Restoration and persecuted the supporters of the new libertarian ideas within Spain. For people in South America, to stay as a part of the Spanish Empire, but with a new relationship with the mother country, was no longer a feasible option: the only remaining options at this point were to return to absolutism or to adopt independentism.395

Legacy

May 25 is a national day in Argentina, known as First Patriotic Government, with the character of a public holiday. The public holiday is set by law 21.329 and is always celebrated on May 25, regardless of the day of the week.396 The Argentina Centennial and the Argentina Bicentennial were celebrated in 1910 and 2010.

May 25 was designated as a patriotic date in 1813, but the Argentine Declaration of Independence suggests July 9 as an alternative national day. At first this added to the conflicts between Buenos Aires and the provinces during the Argentine Civil War, because the date in May related to Buenos Aires and the date of July 9 related to the whole country.397 Thus the unitarian Bernardino Rivadavia canceled the celebration of July 9, and the federalist Juan Manuel de Rosas allowed it again, but maintained the May celebrations.398 By 1880, the federalization of Buenos Aires removed the local connotations and the May Revolution was considered the birth of the nation.399

The date, as well as a generic image of the Buenos Aires Cabildo, are used in different variants to honor the May Revolution. Two of the most notable are the Avenida de Mayo and the Plaza de Mayo in Buenos Aires, near the location of the Cabildo. The May Pyramid was erected in the Plaza a year after the revolution, and was rebuilt in its present form in 1856. Veinticinco de Mayo ("May 25") is the name of several administrative divisions, cities, public spaces and landforms of Argentina. There are departments of this name in the provinces of Chaco, Misiones, San Juan, Rio Negro and Buenos Aires, the latter holding the town of Veinticinco de Mayo. The cities of Rosario (Santa Fe), Jun<U+00ED>n (Buenos Aires) and Resistencia (Chaco) have eponymous squares. King George Island, which is claimed by Argentina, Britain and Chile, as part of the Argentine Antarctica, the British Antarctic Territory and the Chilean Antarctic Territory respectively, is referred to as Isla 25 de Mayo in Argentina.400

A representation of a cabildo is used on Argentine 25-cent coins,401 and an image of the Sun of May appears on the 5-cent coin.402 An image of the Cabildo during the Revolution appeared on the back of the 5-peso banknote of the former peso moneda nacional.403

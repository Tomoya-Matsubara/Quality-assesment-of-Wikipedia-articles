The Norwegian Sea () is a marginal sea in the North Atlantic Ocean, northwest of Norway. It is located between the North Sea (i.e. north of Scotland) and the Greenland Sea and adjoins the North Atlantic Ocean to the west and the Barents Sea to the northeast. In the southwest, it is separated from the Atlantic Ocean by a submarine ridge running between Iceland and the Faroe Islands. To the North, the Jan Mayen Ridge separates it from the Greenland Sea.

Unlike many other seas, most of the bottom of the Norwegian Sea is not part of a continental shelf and therefore lies at a great depth of about two kilometers on average. Rich deposits of oil and natural gas are found under the sea bottom and are being explored commercially, in the areas with the sea depths of up to about one kilometer. The coastal zones are rich in fish that visits the Norwegian Sea from the North Atlantic for spawning. The warm North Atlantic Current ensures relatively stable and high water temperatures, so that contrary to the Arctic seas, the Norwegian Sea is ice free throughout the year.

Extent

The International Hydrographic Organization defines the limits of the Norwegian Sea as follows:1

On the Northeast. A line joining the Southernmost point of West Spitzbergen to North Cape of Bear Island, through this island to Cape Bull and thence on to North Cape in Norway (71<U+00B0>10'N 25<U+00B0>47'E).

On the Southeast. The West coast of Norway between North Cape and Cape Stadt ().

On the South. From a point on the West coast of Norway in Latitude 61<U+00B0>00' North along this parallel to Longitude 0<U+00B0>53' West thence a line to the NE extreme of Fugl<U+00F6> () and on to the East extreme of Gerpir () in Iceland.

On the West. The Southeastern limit of Greenland Sea [A line joining the Southernmost point of West Spitzbergen to the Northern point of Jan Mayen Island, down the West coast of that island to its Southern extreme, thence a Line to the Eastern extreme of Gerpir () in Iceland].

Formation and geography

The Norwegian Sea was formed about 250 million years ago, when the Eurasian plate of Norway and the North American Plate, including Greenland, started to move apart. The existing narrow shelf sea between Norway and Greenland began to widen and deepen.2 The present continental slope in the Norwegian Sea marks the border between Norway and Greenland, as it stood approximately 250 million years ago. In the north it extends east from Svalbard and on the southwest between Britain and the Faroe. This continental slope contains rich fishing grounds and numerous coral reefs. Settling of the shelf after the separation of the continents had resulted in landslides, such as the Storegga Slide about 8,000 years ago that induced a major tsunami.3

The coasts of the Norwegian Sea were shaped during the last Ice Age. So, large, several kilometer tall glaciers pushed into the land forming fjords, removing the crust into the sea and thereby extending the continental slopes. This is particularly clear off the Norwegian coast near the Lofoten Islands.4 The Norwegian continental shelf is between 40 and 200 kilometers wide, and has a different shape from the shelves in the North Sea and Barents Sea. It contains numerous trenches and irregular peaks which usually have an amplitude of less than 100 meters, but it can reach up to 400 meters.5 They are covered with a mixture of gravel, sand and mud, and the trenches are used by fish as a spawning ground.6 Deeper into the sea, there are two deep basins separated by a low ridge (deepest point at 3,000 m) between the V<U+00F8>ring Plateau and Jan Mayen island. The southern basin is larger and deeper, with large areas between 3,500 and 4,000 meters deep. The northern basin is shallower at 3,200<U+2013>3,300 m, but contains many individual sites going down to 3,500 meters.7 Submarine thresholds and continental slopes mark the borders of these basins with the adjacent seas. To the south lies the European continental shelf and the North Sea, to the east is the Eurasian continental shelf with the Barents Sea. To the west, the Scotland-Greenland Ridge separates the Norwegian Sea from the North Atlantic. This ridge is on average only 500 meters deep, only in a few places reaching the depth of 850 meters. To the north lie the Jan Mayen Ridge and Mohns Ridge, which lie at a depth of 2,000 meters, with some trenches reaching the depths of 2,600 meters.8

Hydrology

Four major water masses originating in the Atlantic and Arctic oceans meet in the Norwegian Sea, and the associated currents are of fundamental importance for the global climate. The warm, salty North Atlantic Current flows in from the Atlantic Ocean, and the colder and less saline Norwegian Current originates in the North Sea. The so-called East Iceland Current transports cold water south from the Norwegian Sea towards Iceland and then east, along the Arctic Circle; this current occurs in the middle water layer. Deep water flows into the Norwegian Sea from the Greenland Sea.9 The tides in the sea are semi-diurnal, that is they rise twice a day to the height of about 3.3 meters.10

Surface currents

The hydrology of the upper water layers is largely determined by the flow from the North Atlantic. It reaches the speed of 10 Sv (1 Sv = million m3/s) and its maximum depth is 700 meters at the Lofoten Islands, but normally it is within 500 meters.11 Part of it comes through the Faeroe-Shetland Channel and has a comparatively high salinity of 35.3<U+2030> (parts per thousand). This current originates in the North Atlantic Current and passes along the European continental slope; increased evaporation due to the warm European climate results in the elevated salinity. Another part passes through the Greenland-Scotland-trench between the Faroe Islands and Iceland; this water has a mean salinity between 35 and 35.2<U+2030>.12 The flow shows strong seasonal variations and can be twice higher in winter than in summer.13 While at the Faeroe-Shetland Channel it has a temperature of about 9.5 <U+00B0>C, it cools down to about 5 <U+00B0>C at Svalbard and releases this energy (about 250 terawatt) to the environment.1415

The current flowing from the North Sea originates in the Baltic Sea and thus collects most of the drainage from northern Europe; this contribution is however relatively small.16 The temperature and salinity of this current shows strong seasonal and annual fluctuations. Long-term measurements within the top 50 meters near the coast result in a maximum temperature of 11.2 <U+00B0>C at the 63<U+00B0> N parallel in September and a minimum of 3.9 <U+00B0>C at North Cape in March. The salinity varies between 34.3 and 34.6<U+2030> and is the lowest in spring owing to the inflow of the melted snow by the rivers.17 The largest rivers discharging into the sea are Namsen, Ranelva and Vefsna. They are all relatively short, but have a high discharge rate owing to their steep mountainous nature.18

Some part of warm surface water flows directly, within the West Spitsbergen Current, from the Atlantic Ocean, off the Greenland Sea, to the Arctic Ocean. This current has a speed of 3<U+2013>5 Sv and has a large impact on the climate.19 Other surface water (~1 Sv) flows along the Norwegian coast in the direction of the Barents Sea. This water may cool in the Norwegian Sea enough do submerge into the deeper layers; there it displaces water which flows back into the North Atlantic.20

Arctic water from the East Iceland Current is mostly found in the southwestern part of the sea, near Greenland. Its properties also show significant annual fluctuations in its properties, with the long-term average temperature below 3 <U+00B0>C and salinity of 34.7<U+2013>34.9<U+2030>.21 The fraction of this water on the sea surface depends on the strength of the current, which in turn depends on the pressure difference between the Icelandic Low and Azores High <U+2013> the larger the difference, the stronger the current.22

Deep-sea currents

The Norwegian Sea is connected with the Greenland Sea and the Arctic Ocean by the 2,600-meter deep Fram Strait.23 The Norwegian Sea Deep Water (NSDW) occurs at depths exceeding 2,000 meters; this homogeneous layer with a salinity of 34.91<U+2030> experiences little exchange with the adjacent seas. Its temperature is below 0 <U+00B0>C and drops to <U+2212>1 <U+00B0>C at the ocean floor.24 Compared with the deep waters of the surrounding seas, NSDW has more nutrients, but less oxygen and is relatively old.25

The weak deep-water exchange with the Atlantic Ocean is due to the small depth of the relatively flat Greenland-Scotland Ridge between Scotland and Greenland, an offshoot of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. Only four areas of the Greenland-Scotland Ridge are deeper than 500 meters: the Faroe Bank Channel (about 850 meters), some parts of the Iceland-Faroe Ridge (about 600 meters), the Wyville-Thomson Ridge (620 meters) and areas between Greenland and Denmark Strait (850 meters) <U+2013> this is much shallower than the Norwegian Sea.2627 Cold deep water flows into the Atlantic through various channels: about 1.9 Sv through the Faroe Bank channel, 1.1 Sv through the Iceland-Faroe channel and 0.1 Sv via the Wyville-Thomson Ridge.28 The turbulence that occurs when the deep water falls behind the Greenland-Scotland Ridge into the deep Atlantic basin mixes the adjacent water layers and forms the North Atlantic Deep Water, one of two major deep-sea currents providing the deep ocean with oxygen.29

Climate

The thermohaline circulation affects the climate in the Norwegian Sea, and the regional climate can significantly deviate from average. There is also a difference of about 10 <U+00B0>C between the sea and the coastline. Temperatures rose between 1920 and 1960,30 and the frequency of storms decreased in this period. The storminess was relatively high between 1880 and 1910, decreased significantly in 1910<U+2013>1960, and then recovered to the original level.31

In contrast to the Greenland Sea and Arctic seas, the Norwegian Sea is ice-free year round, owing to its warm currents.32 The convection between the relatively warm water and cold air in the winter plays an important role in the Arctic climate.33 The 10-degree July isotherm (air temperature) runs through the northern boundary of the Norwegian Sea and is often taken as the southern boundary of the Arctic.34 The Norwegian Sea in winter generally has the lowest air pressure in the entire Arctic and is a region where most Icelandic Low depressions form.35 The water temperature in most parts of the sea is 2<U+2013>7 <U+00B0>C in February and 8<U+2013>12 <U+00B0>C in August.36

Flora and fauna

The Norwegian Sea as a transition zone between boreal and Arctic conditions, and thus contains flora and fauna characteristic to both climatic regions.37 The southern limit of many Arctic species runs through North Cape, Iceland and the center of the Norwegian Sea, while the northern limit of boreal species lies near the borders of the Greenland Sea with the Norwegian Sea and Barents Sea, i.e. these areas overlap. Some species like the scallop Chlamys islandica and capelin tend to occupy this area between the Atlantic and Arctic Oceans.38

Plankton and sea bottom organisms

Most of the aquatic life in the Norwegian Sea is concentrated in the upper layers. Estimates for the entire North Atlantic are that only 2% of biomass is produced at depths below 1,000 meters and only 1.2% occur near the sea floor.39

The blooming of the phytoplankton is dominated by the chlorophyll and peaks around 20 May. The major phytoplankton forms are diatoms, in particular the genus Thalassiosira and Chaetoceros. After the spring bloom the haptophytes of the genus Phaecocystis pouchetti become dominant.40

Zooplankton is mostly represented by the copepods Calanus finmarchicus and Calanus hyperboreus where the former occurs about four times more often than the latter and is mostly found in the Atlantic streams, whereas C. hyperboreus dominates the Arctic waters;41 they are the main diet of most marine predators.42 The most important krill species are Meganyctiphanes norvegica, Thyssanoessa inermis and Thyssanoessa longicaudata.43 In contrast to the Greenland Sea, there is a significant presence of calcareous plankton (Coccolithophore and Globigerinida) in the Norwegian Sea.44

Shrimp of the species Pandalus borealis play an important role in the diet of fish, particularly cod and blue whiting, and mostly occur at the depths between 200 and 300 meters. A special feature of the Norwegian Sea are extensive coral reefs of Lophelia pertusa, which provide shelter to various fish species. Although these corals are widespread in many peripheral areas of the North Atlantic, they never reach such amounts and concentrations as at the Norwegian continental slopes. However, they are at risk due to increasing trawling, which mechanically destroys the coral reefs.45

Fish

The Norwegian coastal waters are the most important spawning ground of the herring populations of the North Atlantic, and the hatching occurs in March. The eggs float to the surface and are washed off the coast by the northward current. Whereas a small herring population remains in the fjords and along the northern Norwegian coast, the majority spends the summer in the Barents Sea, where it feeds on the rich plankton. Upon reaching puberty, herring returns to the Norwegian Sea.46 The herring stock varies greatly between the years. It increased in the 1920s owing to the milder climate an then collapsed in the following decades until 1970; the decrease was however at least partly affected by overfishing.47 The biomass of young hatched herring declined from 11 million tonnes in 1956 to almost zero in 197048 that affected the ecosystem not only of the Norwegian Sea but also of the Barents Sea.49

Enforcement of environmental and fishing regulations resulted in partial recovery of the herring populations since 1987.5051 This recovery was accompanied by a decline of capelin and cod stocks. While the capelin benefited from the reduced fishing, the temperature rise in the 1980s and competition for food with the herring resulted in a near disappearance of young capelin from the Norwegian Sea.52 Meanwhile, the elderly capelin population was quickly fished out. This also reduced the population of cod <U+2013> a major predator of capelin <U+2013> as the herring was still too small in numbers to replace the capelin in the cod's diet.5354

Blue whiting (Micromesistius poutassou) has benefited from the decline of the herring and capelin stocks as it assumed the role of major predator of plankton. The blue whiting spawns near the British Isles. The sea currents carry their eggs to the Norwegian Sea, and the adults also swim there in order to benefit from the food supply. The young spend the summer and the winter until February in the Norwegian coastal waters and then return to the warmer waters west of Scotland.55 The Norwegian Arctic cod is mostly occurs in the Barents Sea and at the Svalbard Archipelago. In the rest of the Norwegian Sea, it is found only during the reproduction season, at the Lofoten Islands,56 whereas Pollachius virens and haddock spawn in the coastal waters.57 Mackerel is an important commercial fish. The coral reefs are populated by different species of the genus Sebastes.58

Mammals and birds

thumb |Armhook squid Gonatus fabricii Significant numbers of minke, humpback, sei and orca whales are present in the Norwegian Sea,59 and white-beaked dolphin occurs in the coastal waters.<ref name="Dolphins138>Margaret Klinowska (Ed.): White-Beaked Dolphins in Dolphins, Porpoises and Whales of the World: The IUCN Red Data Book, IUCN, 1991 ISBN 2-88032-936-1, p. 138 Orcas and some other whales visit the sea in the summer months for feeding; their population is closely related to the herring stocks, and they follow the herring schools within the sea.60 With a total population of about 110,000, minke whales are by far the most common whales in the sea. They are hunted by Norway and Iceland with a quota of about 1,000 per year in Norway. In contrast to the past, their meat rather than fat and oil is primarily consumed nowadays.61

Bowhead whale used to be a major plankton predator, but it almost disappeared from the Norwegian Sea after intense whaling in the 19th century,62 and was temporarily extinct in the entire North Atlantic. Similarly, the blue whale used to form large groups between Jan Mayen and Spitsbergen, but is hardly present nowadays.63 Observations of northern bottlenose whale in the Norwegian Sea are rare.64 Other large animals of the sea are hooded and harp seals and squid.65

Important waterfowl species of the Norwegian Sea are puffin, kittiwake and guillemot. Puffins and guillemots also suffered from the collapse of the herring population, especially the puffins on the Lofoten Islands. The latter hardly had an alternative to herring and their population declined about twice between 1969 and 1987.66

Human activities

Norway, Iceland and Denmark/Faroe Islands share the territorial waters of the Norwegian Sea, with the largest part belonging to the former country. Norway claimed twelve-mile limit as territorial waters since 2004 and an exclusive economic zone of 200 miles since 1976. Consequently, due to the Norwegian islands of Svalbard and Jan Mayen, the southeast, northeast and northwest edge of the sea occur within Norway. The southwest border is shared between Iceland and Denmark/Faroe Islands.67

The largest damage to the Norwegian Sea was caused by extensive fishing, whaling and pollution. The British nuclear complex Sellafield is one of the greatest polluters, discharging radioactive waste into the sea. Other contamination is mostly by oil and toxic substances,68 but also from the great number of ships sunk during the world wars.69 The environmental protection of the Norwegian Sea is mainly regulated by the OSPAR Convention.70

Fishing and whaling

Fishing near Lofoten archipelago has been practiced for hundreds years. The coastal waters of remote Lofoten islands are one of the richest fishing areas in Europe as most of the Atlantic cod swims in the winter to the coastal waters of Lofoten to spawn. So in the 19th century, dried cod was one of Norway's main areas of exports and by far the most important industry in northern Norway. Strong sea currents, maelstroms, and especially frequent storms made fishing a dangerous occupation: several hundred men died on the "Fatal Monday" in March 1821, 300 of them being from a single parish, and about a hundred boats with their crews were lost within a short time in April 1875.71

Whaling was also important for the Norwegian Sea. In the early 1600s, the Englishman Stephen Bennet started hunting walrus at the Bear Island. In May 1607 the Muscovy Company, while looking for the Northwest Passage and exploring the sea, discovered the large populations of walrus and whales in the Norwegian Sea and started hunting them in 1610 near Spitsbergen.72 Later in the 17th century, Dutch ships started hunting bowhead whales near Jan Mayen island; the bowhead population between Svalbard and Jan Mayen was than about 25,000 individuals.73 Britain and Netherlands were then joined by Germans, Danish and Norway.74 Between 1615 and 1820, the waters between Jan Mayen, Svalbard, Bear Island and Greenland between the Norwegian Greenland and Barents seas were the most productive whaling area in the world. However, extensive hunting wiped off whales from that region by the early 20th century.75

Sea monsters and maelstroms

For many centuries, the Norwegian Sea was regarded as the edge of the known world. The disappearance of ships there, due to the natural disasters, induced legends of the monsters (kraken) which halt and sink ships. As late as in 1845, the Encyclop<U+00E6>dia metropolitana contained a multi-page review by Erik Pontoppidan (1698<U+2013>1764) on ship-sinking sea monsters of half a mile in size.76 Many legends might be based on the work Historia de gentibus septentrionalibus of 1539 by Olaus Magnus where he described the kraken and maelstroms of the Norwegian Sea.77 The kraken also appeared in Alfred Tennyson's poem of the same, in Herman Melville's Moby Dick and in Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea by Jules Verne.

Between the Lofoten islands of Moskenes<U+00F8>ya and V<U+00E6>r<U+00F8>y, at the tiny Mosken island, lies the Moskenstraumen <U+2013> a system of tidal eddies and a whirlpool called a maelstrom. With the speed of the order of 15 km/h (the value strongly varies between the sources), it is one of the strongest maelstroms in the world. It was described in the 13th century in the Old Norse poems Edda and remained an attractive subject for painters and writers, including Edgar Allan Poe, Walter Moers and Jules Verne. The term maelstrom originates from the combination of Dutch words malen (to grind) and strom (stream); it was introduced into English language by Poe in his story "A Descent into the Maelstr<U+00F6>m" (1841) describing the Moskenstraumen.78 The Moskenstraumen is created as a result of a combination of several factors, including the tides, position of the Lofoten and the underwater topography; unlike most others whirls, it is located in the open sea rather than in a channel or bay. With the diameter of 40<U+2013>50 meters, it can endanger small fishing vessels even in modern time, which might be attracted by the abundant cod feeding on the microorganisms sucked by the whirl.79

Exploration

The fish-rich coastal waters of northern Norway were known for ages and were attracting skilled sailors from Iceland and Greenland. Thus most settlements in Iceland and Greenland were on the west coasts of the islands, which were also warmer due to the Atlantic currents. The first reasonably reliable map of northern Europe, the Carta marina of 1539 represents the Norwegian Sea as coastal waters and shows nothing north of North Cape. The Norwegian Sea off the coast regions appeared on the maps in the 17th century as an important part of the then-to-find Northern Sea Route and a rich whaling ground.80

Jan Mayen island was discovered in 1607 and become an important base of Dutch whalers. The Dutchman Willem Barents discovered Bear Island and Svalbard,81 which was then used by Russian whalers called pomors. The islands on the edge of the Norwegian Sea have been rapidly divided between nations. During the peaks of whaling, some 300 ships with 12,000 crew members were yearly visiting Svalbard.82

The first depth measurements of the Norwegian Sea were performed in 1773 by Constantine Phipps aboard HMS Racehorse, as a part of his North Pole expedition.83 Systematic oceanographic research in the Norwegian Sea started in the late 19th century, when declines in the yields of cod and herring off the Lofoten prompted the Norwegian government to investigate the matter.84 The zoologist Georg Sars and meteorologist Henrik Mohn persuaded the government in 1874 to send out a scientific expedition, and between 1876 to 1878 they explored much of the sea aboard V<U+00F8>ringen.85 The obtained data allowed Mohn to establish the first dynamic model of ocean currents, which incorporated winds, pressure differences, sea water temperature and salinity and agreed well with the later measurements.86

Navigation

Until the 20th century, the coasts of the Norwegian Sea were sparsely populated and therefore shipping in the sea was mostly focused on fishing, whaling and occasional coastal transportations. Since the late 19th century, the Norwegian Coastal Express sea line was established connecting the more densely populated south with the north of Norway by at least one trip a day. The importance of shipping in the Norwegian Sea increased with the expansion of the Russian and Soviet navies in the Barents Sea and development of international routes to the Atlantic through the Baltic Sea, Kattegat, Skagerrak and North Sea.

The Norwegian Sea is ice free and provides a direct route from the Atlantic to the Russian ports in the Arctic (Murmansk, Archangel, Kandalaksha) which are directly linked to the central Russia. This route was extensively used for supplies during World War II <U+2013> from 811 US ships, 720 reached Russian ports bringing some 4 million tonnes of cargo which included about 5,000 tanks and 7,000 aircraft. The Allies lost 18 convoys and 89 merchant ships on this route.87 The major operations of the German Navy against the convoys included PQ 17 in July 1942, the Battle of the Barents Sea in December 1942 and the Battle of the North Cape in December 1943 and were carried out around the border between Norwegian Sea and Barents Sea, near the North Cape.88

Navigation across the Norwegian Sea declined after the World War II and intensified only in the 1960s<U+2013>70s with the expansion of the Soviet Northern Fleet that was reflected in major joint naval exercises of the Soviet Northern Baltic fleets in the Norwegian Sea. The sea was the gateway for the Soviet Navy to the Atlantic Ocean and thus to the United States, and a major Soviet port of Murmansk was just behind the border of the Norwegian and Barents Sea.89 The countermeasures by the NATO countries resulted in a significant naval presence in the Norwegian Sea and intense cat-and-mouse games between Soviet and NATO aircraft, ships and especially submarines.90 A relic of the Cold War in the Norwegian Sea, the Soviet nuclear submarine K-278 Komsomolets, sank in 1989 southwest of Bear Island, at the border of the Norwegian and Barents seas, with radioactive material onboard which poses potential danger to flora and fauna.91

The Norwegian Sea is part of the Northern Sea Route for ships from European ports to Asia. The travel distance from Rotterdam to Tokyo is 21,100 km via Suez Canal and only 14,100 km through the Norwegian Sea. Sea ice is a common problem in the Arctic seas, but ice-free condition along the entire northern route was observed in the end of August 2008.92 Russia is planning to expand its offshore oil production in the Arctic that should increase the traffic of tankers through the Norwegian Sea to markets in Europe and America; it is expected that the number of oil shipments through the northern Norwegian Sea will increase from 166 in 2002 to 615 in 2015.93

Oil and gas

The most important products of the Norwegian Sea are no longer fish, but are oil and especially gas found under the Norwegian Sea.94 Norway started undersea oil production in 1993 followed by development of the gas field Huldra in 2001.95 The large depth and harsh waters of the Norwegian Sea pose significant technical challenges for offshore drilling.96 Whereas drilling at depths exceeding 500 meters is conducted since 1995, only a few deep gas fields are explored commercially. The most important current project is Ormen Lange (depth 800-1,100 m) where gas production started in 2007. With the reserves of 1.4 cubic feet, it is the major Norwegian gas field. It is connected to the Langeled pipeline, currently the world's longest underwater pipeline, and thus to a major European gas pipeline network.9798 Several other gas fields are being developed. A particular challenge is the Kristin field where the temperature is as high as 170 <U+00B0>C and the gas pressure exceeds 900 bar (900 times the normal pressure).99

The Sejm of the Estates1) or Estates of Galicja2 (3) were the parliament in the first half of the 19th century Galicja region in Austrian Empire. The body existed from 1775 to 1845. In the history of the Polish parliament, it succeeded the general sejm and local sejmik on the territories of the Austrian partition. The Estates were disbanded following the Krak<U+00F3>w Uprising of 1846. In 1861 they was succeeded by the Country Sejm ().

History

Postulate Sejm

Parts of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth Lesser Poland territories were included in the Austrian partition as early as the First Partition of Poland in 1772.Austrian Empire in 1775 allowed the Polish nobility (szlachta) of the newly acquired territories to continue their tradition of holding a local parliament-like meetings, and gave the permission for an advisory body, the Postulate Sejm (), to be held every year.4 The implementation of the Postulate Sejm was delayed, and eventually it held only four sessions, all in Lviv (Lw<U+00F3>w): in 1782, 1784, 1786 and 1788.ski1919"/> Polish legal scholar Stanis<U+0142>aw Grodziski noted that while the Sejm, on the surface, continued the Polish parliamentary tradtion, the real goal of this powerless body, as intended by the Austrian court, was to drive a wedge between the various groups of Polish nobility, reducing their unity and influence.5 The Postulate Sejm was composed of wealthy nobility and two deputies from the city of Lviv.druch1982-241"/> The 1788 session was the last one; part of the Austrian-held Commonwealth territories were briefly included in the Duchy of Warsaw following the Polish-Austrian War in 1806, and the populace represented at the Sejm of the Duchy of Warsaw, but this was short lived, as the territories in question were restored to Austria following the Duchy's occupation and the terms of the Congress of Vienna in 1815.Estates of Galicia

Composition and organization

The number of the members was not fixed; it varied from over 200 (in 1782 and 1817( to as low as 41 (in 1834); in most years the Sejm had averaged 60 to 80 deputies.druch1982-243"/> They were composed of clergy, nobility (either titled or above a certain, relatively high, income level), two deputies from the city of Lviv representing the burgers and later, chancellor of the University of Lw<U+00F3>w.druch1982-242"/> The members were not elective, holding the mandates due to their offices, titles or wealth.druch1982-242"/>

The Estates met for a few days each year, with some exceptions: they were not convened in the years 1831 and 1832 (time of the November Uprising in the neighboring Polish statelet, the Congress Kingdom).druch1982-243"/>

The Estates had no dedicated location to meet.druch1982-247"/> While in session, they met at the former monastery buildings, owned by the University of Lw<U+00F3>w.Competences

Just like the preceeding Postulate Sejm, the Estates had very limited competences: they met to hear the Emperor decrees, distribute tax assessments, grant Galician titles of nobility to individuals who already held similar titles elsewhere in the Austrian Empire, prepare petitions and appeals to be heard by the Emperor.Henryk Schmitt, wrote that the role of the Estates was to listen to the government decisions, and file petitions, which often waited for the royal reply for several years; he thus notes that the Estates were powerless, a "comedy", their only purpose being to fulfill the Congress of Vienna requirement of having some form of "national representation" on the lands of the Austrian partition.6

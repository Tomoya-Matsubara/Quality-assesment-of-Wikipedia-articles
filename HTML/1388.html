Marko Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107> (, ; Â <U+2013> 17 May 1395) was the de jure Serbian king from 1371 to 1395, while he was the de facto ruler of a territory in western Macedonia centered on the town of Prilep. He is known as Prince Marko (, Kraljevi<U+0107> Marko, ) and King Marko (Bulgarian: <U+041A><U+0440><U+0430><U+043B><U+0438> <U+041C><U+0430><U+0440><U+043A><U+043E>; ) in South Slavic oral tradition, in which he has become a major character during the Ottoman occupation of the Balkans. Marko's father, King Vuka<U+0161>in, was the co-ruler alongside Serbian Tsar Stefan Uro<U+0161> V, whose reign was marked by the weakening of the central authority and the gradual disintegration of the Serbian Empire. Vuka<U+0161>in's personal holdings included lands in western Macedonia, Kosovo and Metohija. In 1370 or 1371, he crowned Marko "young king"; this title included the possibility that Marko succeed the childless Uro<U+0161> on the Serbian throne.

On 26 September 1371, Vuka<U+0161>in's forces were defeated in the Battle of Maritsa and he lost his life. About two months later, Tsar Uro<U+0161> died. This formally made Marko the king of the Serbian land; however, great Serbian noblemen, who had become effectively independent from the central authority, did not even consider to recognise him as their supreme ruler. At an uncertain date after 1371, he became an Ottoman vassal. By 1377, significant parts of the territory he inherited from Vuka<U+0161>in were seized by other noblemen. King Marko in reality came to be a regional lord who ruled over a relatively small territory in western Macedonia. He funded the construction of the Monastery of Saint Demetrius near Skopje, better known as Marko's Monastery, finished in 1376. Marko died on 17 May 1395, fighting on the Ottoman side against the Wallachians in the Battle of Rovine.

Although he was a ruler of modest historical significance, Marko became a major character in South Slavic oral tradition. He is venerated as a national hero by the Serbs, Macedonians and Bulgarians, remembered in Balkan folklore as a fearless and powerful protector of the weak, who fought against injustice and confronted the Turks during the early years of Ottoman occupation.

Life

Until 1371

Marko was born around 1335 as the first son of Vuka<U+0161>in Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107> and his wife, Alena.1 The patronymic "Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107>" derives from Mrnjava, described by 17th-century Ragusan historian Mavro Orbin as a minor nobleman from Zachlumia (in present-day Herzegovina and southern Dalmatia).2 According to Orbin, Mrnjava's sons were born in Livno in western Bosnia,3 where he could have moved to after Zachlumia was annexed from Serbia by Bosnia in 1326.4 The Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107> family may have later supported Serbian Emperor (tsar) Stefan Du<U+0161>an in his preparations to invade Bosnia, as did other Zachlumian nobles, and fearing punishment, emigrated to the Serbian Empire before the war started.56 These preparations possibly began two years ahead of the invasion,7 which took place in 1350. From that year comes the earliest written reference to Marko's father Vuka<U+0161>in, denoting him as Du<U+0161>an's appointed <U+017E>upan (district governor) of Prilep,89 which had been acquired by Serbia from Byzantium in 1334 together with other parts of Macedonia.10 In 1355, the Emperor suddenly died of a stroke at the age of about 47.11

Du<U+0161>an was succeeded by his 19-year-old son Uro<U+0161>, who apparently regarded Marko Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107> as a man of trust. The new Emperor appointed him the head of the embassy he sent to Ragusa (now Dubrovnik, Croatia) at the end of July 1361, to negotiate peace between the Empire and the Ragusan Republic during the hostilities that started earlier that year. The peace was not concluded on this occasion, but Marko successfully negotiated the release of Serbian merchants from Prizren detained by the Ragusans. He was also allowed to withdraw the silver his family had deposited in the city. The account of that embassy in a Ragusan document contains the earliest known undisputed reference to Marko Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107>.12 An inscription written in 1356 on a wall of a church in the Macedonian region of Tikve<U+0161>, mentions a Nikola and a Marko as governors in that region, but the identity of this Marko is disputed.13

Du<U+0161>an's death was followed by the stirring of separatist activity in the Serbian Empire. The south-western territories, including Epirus, Thessaly, and lands in southern Albania, seceded by 1357.14 The core of the state remained loyal to the new Emperor Uro<U+0161>. It consisted of three main regions: the western lands, including Zeta and Travunia with the upper Drina Valley; the central Serbian lands; and Macedonia.15 Nevertheless, local noblemen asserted more and more independence from Uro<U+0161>' authority even in the part of the state that remained Serbian. Uro<U+0161> was weak and unable to counteract these separatist tendencies, becoming an inferior power in his own domain.16 Serbian lords also fought each other over territories and influence.17 Vuka<U+0161>in Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107> was a skilful politician, and gradually assumed the main role in the Empire.18 In August or September 1365, Uro<U+0161> crowned him king, making him his co-ruler. By 1370, Marko's potential patrimony increased as Vuka<U+0161>in expanded his personal holdings from Prilep further into Macedonia, Kosovo and Metohija, acquiring Prizren, Pristina, Novo Brdo, Skopje, and Ohrid.19 In a charter he issued on 5Â April 1370, Vuka<U+0161>in mentioned his wife Queen Alena, and his sons Marko and Andrija<U+0161>, signing himself as "Lord of the Serb and Greek Lands, and of the Western Provinces" ().20 In late 1370 or early 1371, Vuka<U+0161>in crowned Marko Young King.21 This title had been given to heirs presumptive of Serbian kings to secure their position as successors to the throne. Since Uro<U+0161> was childless, Marko could thus become his successor, starting a new<U+2014>Vuka<U+0161>in's<U+2014>dynasty of Serbian sovereigns.22 This would mean the end of the two-centuries-long reign of the Nemanji<U+0107> dynasty. Most of the other Serbian lords were not happy with this situation, which strengthened their aspirations towards independence from the central authority.23

Vuka<U+0161>in wanted to obtain a well-connected spouse for his eldest son Marko. A princess of the Croatian House of <U+0160>ubi<U+0107> from Dalmatia, was sent by her father Grgur to the court of their relative Tvrtko I, the ban of Bosnia, to be raised and suitably married by Tvrtko's mother Jelena. The latter was the daughter of George II <U+0160>ubi<U+0107>, whose maternal grandfather was Serbian King Dragutin Nemanji<U+0107>.24 As the ban and his mother approved of Vuka<U+0161>in's idea to marry the <U+0160>ubi<U+0107> princess to Marko, the wedding was about to be held.2526 In April 1370, however, Pope Urban V sent a letter to Tvrtko in which he forbade him to give the Catholic lady in marriage to the "son of His Magnificence, the King of Serbia, a schismatic" (filio magnifici viri Regis Rascie scismatico).27 The pope also wrote about this impending "offence to the Christian faith" to King Louis I of Hungary, the nominal overlord of the ban,28 and that marriage never happened.29 Marko subsequently married Jelena, the daughter of Radoslav Hlapen, the lord of Veria and Edessa, the major Serbian nobleman in southern Macedonia.30

In the spring of 1371, Marko participated in the preparations for a campaign against Nikola Altomanovi<U+0107>, the major lord in the west of the Empire.31 The campaign was planned jointly by King Vuka<U+0161>in and <U+0110>ura<U+0111> I Bal<U+0161>i<U+0107>, the lord of Zeta, who was married to Olivera, the king's daughter. In July of that year, Vuka<U+0161>in and Marko camped with their army outside Scutari, on Bal<U+0161>i<U+0107>'s territory, ready to make an incursion towards Onogo<U+0161>t in Altomanovi<U+0107>'s land. The attack never happened, as the Ottomans threatened the land of Despot Jovan Uglje<U+0161>a, the lord of Serres, Vuka<U+0161>in's younger brother who ruled in eastern Macedonia. The forces of the Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107>s were quickly directed eastward.32 Having in vain looked for allies, the two brothers finally entered with their own troops into the territory controlled by the Ottomans. At the Battle of Maritsa on 26Â September 1371, the Turks annihilated the Serbian army.33 The bodies of Vuka<U+0161>in and Jovan Uglje<U+0161>a were never found. The place where the battle was fought, near the village of Ormenio in the east of present-day Greece, has ever since been called S<U+0131>rp S<U+0131>nd<U+0131><U+011F><U+0131> ("Serbian Rout" in Turkish). The outcome of this battle had serious consequences for the entire region because it opened up the Balkans to the Turks.34

After 1371

When his father died, "young king" Marko legally became a king and the co-ruler of Emperor Uro<U+0161>. The end of the Nemanji<U+0107> dynasty came soon afterwards, when Uro<U+0161> died on 2 or 4Â December 1371, which formally made Marko the sovereign of the Serbian state.35 Serbian lords, however, did not even consider to recognise him as their supreme ruler,36 and the separatism within the state increased even more.37 After the demise of the two brothers and the destruction of their armies, the Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107> family was left without any real power.38 Lords surrounding Marko exploited the opportunity and seized significant parts of his patrimony. By 1372, <U+0110>ura<U+0111> I Bal<U+0161>i<U+0107> took Prizren and Pe<U+0107>, and Prince Lazar Hrebeljanovi<U+0107> took Pristina.39 By 1377 Vuk Brankovi<U+0107> acquired Skopje, and Albanian magnate Andrea Gropa became practically independent in Ohrid. The latter possibly remained a vassal to Marko as he had been to Vuka<U+0161>in.40 Gropa's son-in-law was Marko's relative Ostoja Rajakovi<U+0107> of the clan of Ugar<U+010D>i<U+0107> from Travunia. He was one of the Serbian nobles from Zachlumia and Travunia (adjacent principalities in present-day Herzegovina) who had received lands in the newly conquered parts of Macedonia during Emperor Du<U+0161>an's reign.41 The only significant town that Marko kept was Prilep, from which his father's rise had started. King Marko then became a petty prince who ruled over a relatively small territory in western Macedonia, bordered in the north by the <U+0160>ar mountains and Skopje, in the east by the Vardar and the Crna Reka rivers, and in the west by Ohrid. The southern limits of his territory are uncertain.42 Marko was not the sole ruler even in this little domain, as he shared it with his younger brother Andrija<U+0161>, who had his own land in it.43 Their mother, Queen Alena, became a nun after Vuka<U+0161>in's death, taking the monastic name Jelisaveta, but she was the co-ruler with Andrija<U+0161> for some time after 1371. The youngest brother, Dmitar, lived on the territory controlled by Andrija<U+0161>. There was yet another brother named Ivani<U+0161>, about whom very little is known.44 The exact date when Marko became an Ottoman vassal is uncertain, but it probably did not happen immediately after the Battle of Maritsa.45

At some point, Marko separated from his spouse Jelena and lived with Todora, the wife of a man named Grgur. Jelena returned to her father Radoslav Hlapen in Veria. Marko later sought to reconcile with her, but to get his wedded wife back, he first had to send Todora to Hlapen. As Marko's domain was bordered to the south by Hlapen's land, this reconciliation may have been motivated by the fact that Marko did not want an enemy in the south, after all the territorial losses that he had in the north.46 That this marital episode is known is due to scribe Dobre, a subject of Marko's. Dobre transcribed a liturgical book for the church in the village of Kalu<U+0111>erec, and when he finished the job, he wrote an inscription in the book which begins as follows:47

Glory to God the Finisher for ever and ever, amen, amen, amen. This book was written in Pore<U+010D>je, in the village called Kalu<U+0111>erec, in the days of the pious King Marko, when he handed over Todora the wife of Grgur to Hlapen, and took back his first-wedded wife Jelena, Hlapen's daughter.

Marko's fortress was situated on a hill to the north of present-day Prilep. Its remains, partially well-preserved, are called Markovi Kuli ("Marko's towers"). Beneath the fortress lies a village named Varo<U+0161><U+2014>the site of medieval Prilep. The village contains the Monastery of Archangel Michael renovated by Marko and Vuka<U+0161>in, whose portraits are depicted on the walls of the monastery's church.48 Marko was the ktetor of the Church of Saint Sunday in Prizren, which was finished in 1371, just before the Battle of Maritsa. In the inscription above the church's entrance, he is titled "young king".49

The Monastery of St. Demetrius, popularly known as Marko's Monastery, is situated at the village of Markova Su<U+0161>ica near Skopje. Its construction lasted from c.Â 1345 to 1376 or 1377. Kings Marko and Vuka<U+0161>in, its ktetors, are portrayed above the southern entrance of the monastery's church.50 Marko is represented as an austere-looking man in purple clothes, wearing a crown adorned with strings of pearls. With his left hand he holds a scroll, the text on which begins with the words: "I, in the Christ God the pious King Marko, built and inscribed this divine templeÂ ..." In his right hand he holds a big horn that symbolizes the horn of oil with which the Old Testament kings were anointed at their enthronement (as described, e.g., in 1 Samuel 16:13). According to an interpretation, Marko is shown here as the king chosen and anointed by God to lead his people in the times of crisis after the Battle of Maritsa.51

Marko minted his own money, as did his father and other Serbian nobles of the time.52 His silver coins weighed 1.11Â grams,53 and were produced in three types. In two of them, the obverse contained this text in five lines: "In the Christ God, the pious King Marko".54 The reverse depicted Christ seated on a throne in the first type, and Christ seated in a mandorla in the second. In the third type, the reverse represented Christ in a mandorla, and the obverse contained the text in four lines: "Pious King Marko".55 With this simple title Marko referred to himself also in the aforementioned church inscription. He did not include any territorial designation in his title, probably in tacit acknowledgement of his limited sway.56 His brother Andrija<U+0161> also minted his own money; still the money supply on the territory ruled by the Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107> brothers mostly consisted of the coins that had been struck by King Vuka<U+0161>in and Tsar Uro<U+0161>.57 It is estimated that about 150 pieces of Marko's coins are kept today in various numismatic collections.58

By 1379, Prince Lazar Hrebeljanovic, the lord of Pomoravlje, emerged as the first and most powerful among Serbian nobles.5960 In his signatures, he titled himself as the Autokrator of all the Serbs (); nevertheless, he was not powerful enough to unite all Serbian lands under his authority. The Bal<U+0161>i<U+0107>, Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107>, Konstantin Draga<U+0161> (maternally a Nemanji<U+0107>), Vuk Brankovi<U+0107>, and Radoslav Hlapen, ruled in their respective domains without consulting with Lazar.61 Another king besides Marko advanced on the political scene: in 1377, the Metropolitan of Mile<U+0161>eva crowned Tvrtko I, maternally related to the Nemanji<U+0107> dynasty, King of the Serbs and of Bosnia. He had previously taken some western parts of the former Serbian Empire.62

On 15Â June 1389, Serbian forces led by Prince Lazar, Vuk Brankovi<U+0107>, and Tvrtko's nobleman Vlatko Vukovi<U+0107> of Zachlumia, confronted the Ottoman army led by Sultan Murad I at the Battle of Kosovo, the most famous battle in Serbia's medieval history.63 With the bulk of both armies being wiped out, both Lazar and Murad lost their lives and the battle concluded in a stalemate. However, in the wake of it the Serbs were left with too few men to effectively defend their lands, while the Turks had many more troops in the east. Consequently, the Serbian principalities that were not already Ottoman vassals, one after the other became so in the following years.64

In 1394, a group of Ottoman vassals in the Balkans decided to renounce their vassalage.65 Marko was not one of them, but his younger brothers Andrija<U+0161> and Dmitar refused to remain under Turkish dominance. In the spring of 1394, they left their homeland and emigrated to the Kingdom of Hungary, entering into the service of King Sigismund. They travelled via Ragusa, where they withdrew two thirds of their late father's deposit of 96.73Â kilograms of silver, the remaining third being left for Marko. Andrija<U+0161> and Dmitar were the first Serbian nobles who emigrated to Hungary: the northward migration of the Serbs would continue throughout the Ottoman occupation.66

In 1395, the Turks assailed Wallachia to punish its ruler Mircea I for his incursions into their territory.67 Three Serb vassals fought on the Ottoman side: King Marko, Lord Konstantin Draga<U+0161>, and Despot Stefan Lazarevi<U+0107>, the son and heir of Prince Lazar. The Battle of Rovine took place on 17Â May 1395, and was won by the Wallachians. King Marko and Konstantin Draga<U+0161> were killed in it. After their deaths, the Turks annexed their lands, and combined them into a single Ottoman province centred in Kyustendil.68 Thirty-six years after the Battle of Rovine, Konstantin the Philosopher wrote the Biography of Despot Stefan Lazarevi<U+0107>. In this book, Konstantin recorded an account of what Marko said to Draga<U+0161> on the eve of the battle: "I pray the Lord to help the Christians, no matter if I will be the first to die in this war."69

In folk poetry

Serbian epic poetry

Marko Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107> is the most popular hero of Serbian epic poetry,70 in which he is referred to as Kraljevi<U+0107> Marko; with the word kraljevi<U+0107> meaning "prince",71 or "king's son". This informal title was often attached to the names of King Vuka<U+0161>in's sons in contemporary sources. It was also used post-positively as a surname: Marko Kraljevi<U+0107>. The title/surname was adopted by Serbian oral tradition, and became an integral part of the hero's name.72 Poems about Kraljevi<U+0107> Marko are not sequels that continue the same story line<U+2014>the only thing that binds them into a single poetic cycle is the hero himself.73 His adventures are narrated with the goal of illuminating his character and personality.74 The epic Marko was endowed with a life of 300Â years, and other prominent heroes from the 14th to the 16thÂ centuries appear sometimes as his companions,75 including Milo<U+0161> Obili<U+0107>, Relja Krilatica, Vuk the Fiery Dragon, and Sibinjanin Janko with his nephew Banovi<U+0107> Sekula.76 The poems contain few historical facts about Marko Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107>, notably his connection to the epoch of the disintegration of the Serbian Empire, and his vassalage to the Ottomans.77 They were composed by anonymous Serbian folk bards during the Ottoman occupation of their land. American Slavicist George Rapall Noyes characterized them as "combining tragic pathos with almost ribald comedy in a fashion worthy of an Elizabethan playwright."78

Serbian epic poetry accords with the historical fact of King Vuka<U+0161>in being Marko's father. It asserts that his mother was Jevrosima, the sister of voivode Mom<U+010D>ilo, the lord of the Pirlitor Fortress in Mount Durmitor (in Old Herzegovina). Mom<U+010D>ilo is described as a man of immense size and strength, possessing magical attributes: a winged horse and a sabre with eyes. Vuka<U+0161>in murdered him with the help of the voivode's young wife Vidosava, despite Jevrosima's self-sacrificing attempt to save her brother. Instead of marrying Vidosava, as was the initial plan, Vuka<U+0161>in killed the treacherous woman. He took Jevrosima from Pirlitor to his capital city of Scutari and married her, as the dying Mom<U+010D>ilo had actually advised him to do. She bore him two sons, Marko and Andrija<U+0161>, and the poem recounting these events concludes with the statement that Marko took after his uncle Mom<U+010D>ilo.79 This epic character corresponds historically to the Bulgarian brigand and mercenary Momchil, who was for some time in the service of Serbian Tsar Du<U+0161>an; he later became a despot and died in 1345 in the Battle of Peritheorion.80 According to another account, Marko and Andrija<U+0161> were born by a vila (Slavic mountain nymph), whom Vuka<U+0161>in wedded after he caught her by a lake and took off her wings so that she could not fly away.81 As Marko matured he developed a strong individuality, and Vuka<U+0161>in once declared that he had no control over his son, who went wherever he wanted, drank and brawled. Marko grew up into an extraordinarily large and strong man, with a rather terrifying appearance which was at the same time somewhat comical. He wore a wolf-skin cap pulled low over his dark eyes; his massive black moustache was as large as a six-months-old lamb; his cloak was a shaggy wolf-pelt. A Damascus sabre swung at his girdle and a spear was slung across his back. Marko's six-flanged mace weighed 66Â okas (85Â kilograms), which he hung at the left side of his saddle, balancing it with a well-filled wineskin attached to the saddle's right side. His grip was such that he could squeeze drops of water out of a piece of dry cornel wood. He defeated a succession of the greatest champions, fighting triumphantly against overwhelming odds.8283

The hero's inseparable companion and friend was his piebald wonder-horse <U+0160>arac, who could talk. When Marko drank he always gave <U+0160>arac an equal share of the wine.84 The horse could leap three spear-lengths high and four spear-lengths forward, which enabled Marko to pursue and capture the dangerous and elusive vila called Ravijojla. She then became his blood sister, promising to aid him if he should ever be in evil straits. When Ravijojla helped him to kill the monstrous, three-hearted Musa Kesed<U+017E>ija, who almost defeated him, Marko grieved because he had slain a better man than himself.8586 Marko is portrayed as a protector of the weak and helpless, a fighter against Turkish bullies and against injustice in general. He acted as an ideal bearer of patriarchal and natural norms of life: amidst a Turkish military camp, he beheaded the Turk who dishonourably killed his father; he abolished the marriage tax by killing the tyrant who imposed it on the people of Kosovo; he saved the sultan's daughter from an unwanted marriage, after she entreated him as her blood brother to help her; he rescued three Serbian voivodes, his blood brothers, from a dungeon; he helped animals in distress. He is shown as a rescuer and benefactor of people, and a promoter of life; "Prince Marko is remembered like a fair day in the year," as is stated in a poem.87

A striking characteristic of Marko's was his devotion to his mother Jevrosima, for whom he cherished a limitless reverence and love. He constantly sought her advice and obeyed it even when it contradicted his own impulses and desires. She lived with Marko at his mansion in Prilep, shining as his lodestar that led him toward the good and away from the evil, along the path of moral improvement and Christian virtues.88 Marko's honesty and high moral courage are conspicuous in the poem in which he happened to be the only person who knew the will of the late Tsar Du<U+0161>an regarding his heir. Marko refused to bear false witness in favour of the pretenders<U+2014>his own father and uncles<U+2014>and spoke out the truth that Du<U+0161>an had appointed his son Uro<U+0161> heir to the Serbian throne. This almost cost him life as Vuka<U+0161>in tried to kill him.89

Marko is also represented as a loyal vassal to the Ottoman sultan, fighting to protect the potentate and his empire from dangerous outlaws. When summoned by the sultan, he participated in Turkish military campaigns.90 Yet even in this relationship, the hero's strong personality and sense of dignity were expressed. More than once the sultan actually showed anxiety towards his burly, wayward vassal,91 and the interviews between Marko and his imperial master usually ended in this way:

Marko's fealty was skilfully combined with the suggestion that the nominal servant was in reality greater than his lord. Serbian bards thus reversed the roles and turned the tables on their conquerors. This dual aspect of Marko could be a reason why he became a national hero of the Serbs: for them he grew into "the proud symbol expressive of the unbroken spirit that lived on in spite of disaster and defeat,"94 as stated by David Halyburton Low, translator of Serbian epic poems.

In fights, Marko used not only his strength and prowess but also cunning and trickery. Despite all his extraordinary qualities, he was not depicted as an abstract superhero or a god, but as a mortal man. There were opponents in the presence of whom his courage wavered and those who surpassed him in strength; there were times when his spirit quailed. He had his evil moments, when he acted capricious, short-tempered, or even with cruelty, but these were few in number. The prevailing traits of the hero's nature were honesty, self-sacrificing loyalty, and the fundamental goodness.95

With his comically stylized appearance and behaviour, and his wry remarks at opponents' expense, Marko is regarded as the most humorous character in Serbian epic poetry.96 When a Moor smote him with a mace, the hero spoke to the attacker laughingly, "O valiant black Moor! Are you jesting or smiting in earnest?"97 Jevrosima once advised her son to cease from his bloody adventures and to plough fields. He obeyed, but in his grimly humorous way,98 ploughing the sultan's highway instead of fields. There came a group of Turkish Janissaries, who transported three packs of gold. They shouted at him to stop ploughing the highway, to which he responded by warning them to keep off the furrows. Marko quickly wearied of the exchange of words:

Marko, aged 300Â years, rode on 160-years-old <U+0160>arac by the seashore towards Mount Urvina, when a vila told him that he was going to die. Marko then stooped over a well and saw no reflection of his head from the water, hydromancy thus confirming the vila's words. He killed <U+0160>arac lest Turks capture and use him for menial labor, and gave his beloved companion an elaborate burial. He broke his sword and spear and threw his mace far out into the sea, before lying down to die. The hero's body was found seven days later by Vaso, the abbot of the Monastery of Hilandar on Mount Athos, and his deacon Isaija. Abbot Vaso transported Marko to Mount Athos and buried him at the monastery, leaving no sign of his grave.101

Epic poetry of Bulgaria and Macedonia

"Krali Marko" has been one of the most popular characters in Bulgarian folklore for centuries.102 Bulgarian epic tales in general and those about Marko in particular seem to originate from the southwestern part of the Bulgarian ethnic area,103 much of it on the territory of the present-day Republic of Macedonia. Therefore, the same tales are also seen as part of the ethnic heritage of the present-day Macedonian nation.

According to local legends, Marko's mother was Evrosiya (<U+0415><U+0432><U+0440><U+043E><U+0441><U+0438><U+044F>), sister of the Bulgarian voivoda Momchil, who ruled territories in the Rhodope Mountains. At the birth of Marko, three narecnitsi (fate-fairies) appeared and foretold that he would become a hero and replace his father, the king Vuka<U+0161>in. When his father heard this he threw his son in a basket in the river to get rid of him. But a samodiva (also called samovila) named Vila found Marko and brought him up, becoming his foster mother. Because Marko suckled the samodiva's milk, he acquired supernatural powers. He is portrayed as a Bulgarian fighter for freedom against the Turks. He has a winged horse, called Sharkolia (meaning "dappled") and a stepsisterÂ <U+2014> the samodiva Gyura. The Bulgarian legends incorporate important fragments of pagan mythology and beliefs, even though the Marko epos itself was created as late as the 14<U+2013>18th century. Among Bulgarian epic songs, songs from the cycle about Krali Marko are particularly common and occupy a central place in it.104105 Some prominent Bulgarian folklorists who collected stories about Marko were teacher Trayko Kitanchev (in the region of Resen in Western Macedonia), and Marko Cepenkov from Prilep (in different areas in the region).106

In legends

South Slavic legends about Kraljevi<U+0107> Marko or Krali Marko are mostly based on mythological motifs that are much older than the historical Marko Mrnjav<U+010D>evi<U+0107>. There are differences between the hero's image in the legends and that in the folk poems. In some areas he was imagined as a giant who walked stepping on hilltops, his head knocking the clouds. It was also narrated that he helped God in shaping the Earth in ancient times, and created the river gorge of Demir Kapija ("Iron Gate") with a stroke of his sabre. Thus he drained the sea that covered the regions of Bitola, Mariovo, and Tikve<U+0161> in Macedonia, which enabled people to inhabit them. After the Earth was shaped, he took to arrogantly showing off his strength. God took it away from him by leaving a bag as heavy as the Earth on a road: when Marko tried to lift it, he lost his gigantic strength and became an ordinary man.107

Legends also have it that the hero acquired his strength after he was suckled by a vila. King Vuka<U+0161>in threw his little son Marko into a river, because he did not resemble him, but the boy was saved by a cowherd who adopted him as a son, and the vila suckled him. By other accounts, Marko was a shepherd or a cowherd who found a vila's children lost in a mountain, and made a shade for them against the scorching sun, or gave them water. As a reward, the vila suckled him three times, after which he was able to lift and throw a huge boulder. In an Istrian version of this story, he made a shade for two snakes instead of the children. In a Bulgarian version, each of the three draughts of milk that he suckled from the vila's breast, turned into a snake.108

He was regularly associated with huge solitary boulders and indentations in rocks. The boulders were said to be thrown by Marko from a hill, the indentations being his footprints or those of his horse.109 He was also connected with other geographic objects, such as hills, glens, cliffs, caves, rivers, brooks, and groves, which he created or did something memorable at. They were often named after him, so there are many toponyms from Istria in the west to Bulgaria in the east that are derived from the hero's name.110 In stories from Bulgaria and Macedonia, Marko had a sister who was as strong as he, and competed with him in throwing boulders.111

Marko's wonder-horse was a gift from a vila by some legends, while a Serbian story gives the following account. He was looking for a horse that could bear him. To test a steed, he would grab him by the tail and sling him over his shoulder. Noticing a leprous piebald foal owned by some carters, he grabbed him by the tail, but could not move him at all. Marko bought and cured the foal, naming him <U+0160>arac (after <U+0161>ara "dapple"). He grew up into an enormously powerful horse, becoming the hero's inseparable companion.112 A legend from Macedonia has it that Marko, on a vila's advice, captured a sick horse in a mountain and cured him. The patches on his skin that had been covered with crusts grew white hairs, so the hero's horse became piebald.113

According to folk traditions the hero never died, but lives on in a cave, in a den covered with moss, or in an unknown land.114 A Serbian legend recounts that Marko once fought in a battle in which so many men were killed that the fighters and their horses ended up swimming in blood. He lifted up his hands towards heaven and said, "O God, what am I going to do now!" God took pity on Marko transporting him and <U+0160>arac into a cave, where the hero stuck his sabre into the rock and fell asleep. There is some moss in the cave, which <U+0160>arac eats bit by bit, while the sabre slowly comes out of the rock. When it falls down after it completely emerges, and the horse eats all of the moss, Marko will awake and reappear in the world.115 Some people allegedly saw him after they descended into a deep pit, where he lived in a large house in front of which his horse was also seen. Others saw him in a faraway land, dwelling in a cave. According to a tradition from Macedonia, Marko drank of "eagle's water" which made him immortal, and he now accompanies Prophet Elijah in heaven.116

In modern culture

In the 19th century, Marko was the subject of multiple dramatizations. In 1831, the Hungarian drama Prince Marko was shown in Budim, possibly written by Istv<U+00E1>n Balog 117 and in 1838, the Hungarian drama Prince Marko <U+2013> Great Serbian Hero by Celesztin Perg<U+0151> was shown in Arad.118 In 1848, Jovan Sterija Popovi<U+0107> wrote the tragedy The Dream of Prince Marko which has the legend of sleeping Marko as its central motif. Petar Preradovi<U+0107> wrote the drama Kraljevi<U+0107> Marko which glorifies the strength of the South Slavs. In 1863, Francesco Dall'Ongaro presented his Italian drama Resurrection of Prince Marko.119

Of all the epic or historical figures of Serbian history, Marko is considered to have given the most inspiration to visual artists:120 a monograph on the subject lists 87 authors.121 The oldest known depictions of Marko are 14th century frescoes from Marko's Monastery and Prilep.122123 From 18th century a drawing of Marko on parchment is preserved in the <U+010C>ajni<U+010D>e Gospel, reminiscent of the style of ste<U+0107>ci reliefs,124 while Vuk Karad<U+017E>i<U+0107> wrote that in his childhood (late 18th century) he saw a painting of Marko carrying an ox on his back.125

In the 19th century, lithographs of Marko were made by Anastas Jovanovi<U+0107>,126 Ferdo KikerecMina Karad<U+017E>i<U+0107>,127 Novak Radoni<U+0107>,128 <U+0110>ura Jak<U+0161>i<U+0107>;129 20th century Nade<U+017E>da Petrovi<U+0107>,130 Mirko Ra<U+010D>ki,131 Uro<U+0161> Predi<U+0107>,132 Paja Jovanovi<U+0107>.133 A well-known sculpture of Marko riding his <U+0160>arac by Ivan Me<U+0161>trovi<U+0107> was reproduced on a Yugoslav banknote and stamp.134 A number of modern illustrators have drawn Marko, as well. They include Alexander Key, Aleksandar Klas, Zuko D<U+017E>umhur, Vasa Pomori<U+0161>ac, Bane Kerac and many others.135

Some common motifs are present in works of multiple authors. These are: Marko and Ravijojla; Marko with his mother; Marko and <U+0160>arac; Marko shooting an arrow; Marko plows the roads; Fight between Marko and Musa; and death of Marko.136 Also, several artists have tried to reconstruct a realistic portrait of Marko on the basis of his frescoes.137

In 1924, the Prilep Brewery introduced a light beer called Krali Marko.138

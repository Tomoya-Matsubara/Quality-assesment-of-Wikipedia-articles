Patrick (named Dagmar by the Norwegian Weather Service1 and Tapani in Finland)

Storm Patrick swept over Norway on Christmas Day with severe damage in central coastal areas, continuing over the Scandinavian peninsular towards the Baltic Sea and Gulf of Finland.

Meteorological history

Formed as secondary low to Cato, affecting central Norway,2 Sweden3 and Finland.4

Impact

Norway

Patrick (Dagmar) arrived in Norway as a southwesterly storm, with windspeeds estimated to be 30 m/s on the coast. Powerful winds occurred in Sogn og Fjordane , M<U+00F8>re og Romsdal and Tr<U+00F8>ndelag during the night of December 24 and early morning of December 25. Extreme high storm surge in Finnmark estimated to be 50-80 cm over normal sea levels. In Norway comparison was made with the New Year's Day Storm of 1992, however this storm was not as strong5 Patrick (Dagmar) is believed to be the third strongest storm to hit Norway in 50 years.6 7 8 A large landslide on January 1, 2012 close to the Norwegian city of Trondheim has been attributed to the warm weather and large amounts of rain the system brought to the area, which resulted in 50 people being evacuated.910 A F2 tornado was reported in Hellesylt, Norway.

Sweden

In Sweden the storm left 60,000 homes without electricity11 The Swedish transport authority suspended all train traffic north of G<U+00E4>vle at 8pm on Christmas day.12

Finland

Patrick (Tapani) was dubbed the worst storm in Finland in 10 years. 13 Thousands of customers were left without electricity in Southern Finland.14 The storm was a rare event in Finland and gave the warmest christmas period in half a century.15 An old man is reported to have died after being hit by a falling tree.16

Estonia

Patrick left 100,000 homes without power in Estonia, and triggered 600 rescue operations. Eesti Energia reassigned it's customer services personnel to answer emergency calls.17 Patrick also brought record high temperatures to the country for December. 18 19 Flooding was reported in the streets of major cities.20 21

Russia

The Saint Petersburg Dam gates were closed to protect the city, preventing 15 ships from entering the port.22 The storm tore metal sheeting from roofs and many trees were brought down.23

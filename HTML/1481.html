Lewis Robert "Hack" Wilson (April 26, 1900 <U+2013> November 23, 1948) was an American professional baseball player who played 12 seasons in Major League Baseball (MLB) for the New York Giants, Chicago Cubs, Brooklyn Dodgers and Philadelphia Phillies.1 Despite his diminutive stature, he was one of the most accomplished power hitters in the game during the late 1920s and early 1930s.2 His 1930 season with the Cubs is widely considered one of the most memorable individual single-season hitting performances in baseball history. HIghlights included 56 home runs (the National League record for 68 years) and 191 runs batted in, a mark yet to be surpassed. As one sportswriter of the day remarked, "For a brief span of a few years, this hammered down little strongman actually rivaled the mighty [Babe] Ruth."

While Wilson's combativeness and excessive alcohol consumption made him one of the most colorful sports personalities of his era,3 his drinking and fighting undoubtedly contributed to a premature end to his athletic career and, ultimately, his premature demise.45 He was inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1979.6

Baseball career

Early life and minor leagues

Lewis Robert Wilson was born April 26, 1900 in the Pennsylvania steel mill town of Ellwood City, north of Pittsburgh.7 His mother, Jennie Kaughn, 16, was an unemployed drifter from Philadelphia; his father, Robert Wilson, 24, was a steel worker. His parents never married; both were heavy drinkers, and in 1907 his mother died of appendicitis at the age of 24.

In 1916 Lewis left school to take a job at a locomotive factory, swinging a sledge hammer for four dollars a week.8 Although only five feet six inches tall, he weighed 195 pounds with an 18-inch neck, and feet that fit into size-five-and-one-half shoes. One sportswriter later observed that he was "built along the lines of a beer keg, and was not wholly unfamiliar with its contents."9 While his unusual physique was considered an oddity at the time,10 his large head, tiny feet, short legs and broad, flat face are now recognized as hallmarks of the Fetal Alcohol Syndrome.1112

In 1921 Wilson moved to Martinsburg, West Virginia to join the Martinsburg Mountaineers of the Class "D" Blue Ridge League.1314 After breaking his leg while sliding into home plate during his first professional game,15 he was moved from the catcher's position to the outfield.16 In 1923, playing for the "B" division Portsmouth Truckers, he led the Virginia League in hitting with a .388 batting average.1718 Late in the season, New York Giants manager John McGraw purchased his contract from Portsmouth for $10,500.19

New York Giants

Wilson made his major league debut with the Giants on September 29, 1923, and became the starting left fielder the following season after Billy Southworth fell into a hitting slump.20

Multiple stories exist to explain the origin of his nickname: By one account, a New York newspaper held a nicknaming contest; the winning entry was "Hack" because Wilson reminded many fans of another stocky athlete, the popular wrestler Georg Hackenschmidt.21 In another version, McGraw is said to have remarked that Wilson's physique was reminiscent of a "hack" (slang for taxicab in that era). Giants teammate Bill Cunningham claimed that the nickname was based on Wilson's resemblance to Hack Miller, a catcher with the Chicago Cubs. The New York Times printed the first documented usage of "Hack" on June 10, 1924.

By mid-July Wilson was ranked second in the National League (NL) in hitting.22 He ended the season with a .295 average along with 10 home runs and 57 runs batted in (RBIs) as the Giants clinched the NL pennant.23 In the 1924 World Series he averaged only .233 as the Giants lost to the Washington Senators in seven games.24

Early in the 1925 season Wilson hit the longest home run on record at Ebbets Field against the Brooklyn Robins,25 but fell into a slump in May, and was replaced in left field by Irish Meusel.26 On July 2 he hit two home runs in one inning, tying Ken Williams's major league record set in 1922,27 but his hitting slump continued. In August McGraw told reporters that he had "...made the mistake of rushing [Wilson] along," and sent him to the Giants' minor league affiliate, the Toledo Mud Hens of the American Association. At season's end a front office oversight left him unprotected on the Toledo roster, and the last-place Chicago Cubs acquired him on waivers.28 "They let go the best outfielder I ever played alongside," said Giants right fielder Ross Youngs, "and they're going to regret it."

Glory years with the Cubs

Wilson regained his form as the Cubs' center fielder in 1926 and soon became a favorite of Chicago fans.29 On May 24 he hit the center field scoreboard with one of the longest home runs in Wrigley Field history as the Cubs came from behind to defeat the Boston Braves.30 Later that evening he made news again when he was arrested during a police raid of a Prohibition-era speakeasy while trying to escape through the rear window.31 He ended the season with a league-leading 21 home runs along with 36 doubles, 109 RBIs, a .321 batting average, and a .406 on-base percentage.32 The Cubs improved to fourth place, and Wilson ended the year ranked fifth in voting for the NL's Most Valuable Player (MVP) Award.33

Another strong performance followed in 1927 as Wilson once again led the league in home runs. Although the Cubs were in first place heading into the final month of the season, the team faltered and again finished fourth.34 Wilson posted a .318 average with 30 home runs and 129 RBIs,35 and led NL outfielders with 400 putouts.36 He led the NL in home runs for a third consecutive year in 1928 with 31, along with 120 RBIs and a .313 average as the Cubs improved to third place.37

Wilson had a combative streak and frequently initiated fights with opposing players and fans. On June 22, 1928 a near-riot broke out in the ninth inning at Wrigley Field against the St. Louis Cardinals when Wilson jumped into the box seats to attack a heckling fan.38 An estimated 5,000 spectators swarmed the field before police could separate the combatants and restore order.39 The fan sued Wilson for $20,000, but a jury ruled in his favor.40 The following year he took offense at a remark by Cincinnati Reds pitcher Ray Kolp, and upon reaching first base after hitting a single he charged into the Reds dugout, punching Kolp several times before they could be separated.41 Later that evening at the train station, Wilson exchanged words and blows with Cincinnati player Pete Donohue.42 In late 1929 he signed a contract to fight Art Shires of the Chicago White Sox in a boxing match,43 but reneged after Shires lost a fight to George Trafton of the Chicago Bears in December. There was nothing to gain, he said, by fighting a defeated boxer.44

In 1929, Wilson hit .345 with 39 home runs and a league-record 159 RBIs.4546 He and new teammate Rogers Hornsby (who also contributed 39 home runs) led the Cubs to their first NL pennant in eleven years. In the World Series against Connie Mack's Philadelphia Athletics, Wilson's .471 hitting performance was eclipsed by two fielding errors at Shibe Park. Though trailing the Series two games to one, the Cubs were leading by a score of 8<U+2013>0 in the fourth game when the Athletics mounted a 10-run rally in the seventh inning. Wilson lost two fly balls in the sun; the second, with two runners on base, led to an inside-the-park home run by Mule Haas as the Athletics won 10<U+2013>8.4748 After the game, Cubs manager Joe McCarthy reportedly told a boy asking for a souvenir baseball, "Come back tomorrow and stand behind Wilson, and you'll be able to pick up all the balls you want!"4950 The Athletics won again the next day to take the Series in five games.51

1930: The peak

Wilson's 1930 season, aided by a lively ball wound with special Australian wool, is considered one of the best single-season hitting performances in baseball history.5253 By the middle of July he had accumulated 82 RBIs. In August he hit 13 home runs and 53 RBIs, and by September 15 he had reached 176 RBIs, breaking Lou Gehrig's major league record established three years earlier.54 He ended the season with 190, along with an NL-record 56 home runs, .356 batting average, .454 on-base percentage, and league-leading .723 slugging percentage.55 He was unofficially voted the NL's most "useful" player by the Baseball Writers Association of America (which did not inaugurate its official MVP award until 1931).56

In 1999 the Commissioner of Baseball officially increased Wilson's 1930 RBI total to 191 after a box score analysis by baseball historian Jerome Holtzman revealed that Charlie Grimm had been mistakenly credited with an RBI actually driven home by Wilson.57 191 RBIs remains one of baseball's most enduring records; only Gehrig (184) and Hank Greenberg (183) ever came close, and there have been no serious challenges in the last 75 years. (The best recent effort was 165 by Manny Ramirez in 1999.58) 56 home runs stood as the NL record until the 1998 season, when it was broken by Sammy Sosa (66) and Mark McGwire (70).59

Decline

Wilson's success in the 1930 season served only to fuel his drinking habits, and in 1931 he reported to spring training 20 pounds overweight.60 In addition, the NL responded to the prodigious offensive statistics of the previous year (the only season ever in which the league as a whole batted over .300) by introducing a heavier ball with raised stitching to allow pitchers to gain a better grip and throw sharper curveballs.61 Wilson complained that the new Cubs manager, former teammate Rogers Hornsby, did not allow him to "swing away" as much as Joe McCarthy had.62 He went into a protracted hitting slump and was benched in late May.63 By late August Cubs owner William Wrigley, Jr. publicly expressed his desire to trade him.64 On September 6 he was suspended without pay for the remainder of the season after a fight with reporters aboard a train in Cincinnati.65 He was hitting only .261 with 13 home runs at the time.66

During the 1931-1932 off-season the Cubs traded Wilson along with Bud Teachout to the St. Louis Cardinals for Burleigh Grimes.67 In early 1932 the Cardinals traded him to the Brooklyn Dodgers for minor league player Bob Parham.68 Wilson hit .297 with 23 home runs and 123 RBIs for Brooklyn in 1932, and began 1933 with a ninth-inning game-winning pinch-hit inside-the-park grand slam home run at Ebbets Field<U+2014>the first pinch-hit grand slam in Dodger history, and only the third inside-the-park pinch-hit grand slam in MLB history.69 However, by season's end his offensive totals had dropped substantially and he was hitting .262 when the Dodgers released him mid-season in 1934.7071 The Philadelphia Phillies signed him immediately, but after just two hits in 20 at bats, he was released again a month later.72 After a final season with the Albany Senators of the Class "A" New York-Pennsylvania League, Wilson retired at the age of 35.73

Career statistics

In a 12-year major league career, Wilson played in 1,348 games and accumulated 1,461 hits in 4,760 at bats for a .307 career batting average and a .395 on-base percentage. He hit 244 home runs and batted in 1,063 runs, led the NL in home runs four times, and surpassed 100 RBIs six times. Defensively, he finished his career with a .965 fielding percentage.74

Life after baseball

Wilson returned to Martinsburg where he opened a pool hall, but encountered financial problems due to a failed sporting goods business venture, and then a divorce.75 By 1938 he was working as a bartender near Brooklyn's Ebbets Field where he sang for drinks, but had to quit when customers became too abusive.76 A night club venture in suburban Chicago was another financial failure.77 In 1944 he took a job as a good will ambassador for a professional basketball team in Washington D.C., where he lamented that fans remembered his two dropped fly balls in the 1929 World Series far more vividly than his 56 home runs and 191 RBIs in 1930.78 Unable to find work in professional baseball, he moved to Baltimore where he worked as a tool checker in an airplane manufacturing plant and later as a laborer for the City of Baltimore. When municipal authorities realized who he was, he was made the manager of a Baltimore public swimming pool.79

On October 4, 1948 Wilson was discovered unconscious after a fall in his home.80 Though the accident did not appear serious at first, pneumonia and other complications developed and he died of internal hemorrhaging on November 23, 1948 at the age of 48.81 Wilson died penniless and his son refused to claim his remains. NL President Ford Frick finally sent money to cover his funeral expenses.8283 In marked contrast to Babe Ruth's funeral, which had been attended by thousands just three months earlier, only a few hundred people were present for Wilson's services.84 He was buried in Rosedale Cemetery in the town where he made his professional playing debut, Martinsburg, West Virginia.85 Ten months later Joe McCarthy organized a second, more complete memorial service, attended by Kiki Cuyler, Charlie Grimm, Nick Altrock and other players from the Cubs and the Martinsburg team (by then renamed the Blue Sox). A granite tombstone was unveiled, with the inscription, "One of Baseball's Immortals, Lewis R. (Hack) Wilson, Rests Here."

One week before his death, Wilson gave an interview to CBS Radio which was reprinted in Chicago newspapers. In 1949 Charlie Grimm, the Cubs' new manager, posted a framed excerpt from that interview in the Cubs clubhouse, where it remains. It reads, in part:

"Talent isn't enough. You need common sense and good advice. If anyone tries to tell you different, tell them the story of Hack Wilson. ... Kids in and out of baseball who think because they have talent they have the world by the tail. It isn't so. Kids, don't be too big to accept advice. Don't let what happened to me happen to you."

In 1979 Wilson was inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame by the Veterans Committee.86 A Martinsburg street is named Hack Wilson Way in his honor,8788 and the access road to a large city park within his home town, Ellwood City, Pennsylvania, is known as Hack Wilson Drive.89

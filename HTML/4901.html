Hagop Vahram <U+00C7>er<U+00E7>iyan was a professor of mathematics, geography, and calligraphy at the Robert College of Istanbul, known for designing the signature of Mustafa Kemal Atat<U+00FC>rk, the first president of Turkey.

Life

Of Armenian descent, Hagop Vahram <U+00C7>er<U+00E7>iyan was a Turkish educator.1 He travelled to the United States to study the Palmer Method, which by that time had gained widespread popularity.2 He was known for teaching this method during his career as a professor.rk'<U+00FC>n imzas<U+0131>n<U+0131>n <U+00F6>yk<U+00FC>s<U+00FC>" /> He returned to Istanbul and became a professor of mathematics, geography, and calligraphy at the Robert College in Istanbul. Over his 55-year career at the Robert College, <U+00C7>er<U+00E7>iyan taught over 25,000 students, among them the future Prime Minister Bulent Ecevit, Foreign Ministers Selim Sarper and Rifat Turgut Menemencio<U+011F>lu, and Cabinet Minister Kasim Gulek.rk'<U+00FC>n imzas<U+0131>n<U+0131>n <U+00F6>yk<U+00FC>s<U+00FC>" />3

Atat<U+00FC>rk's signature

During the initial years of the Turkish Republic and under the reforms of Atat<U+00FC>rk, a Latin-based alphabet was introduced to replace the Perso-Turkic script then in use. As part of the reforms, Turkish citizens were also required to take up a last name under the Surname Law in 1934. Prior to this, Turkey's Christian and Jewish citizens were already using surnames, but Muslims did not use surnames. Muslims were generally referred by their social or professional titles such as "Pasha", "Hoca", "Bey", "Han<U+0131>m", "Efendi", or their names were complemented with that of their father.rk'<U+00FC>n imzas<U+0131>n<U+0131>n <U+00F6>yk<U+00FC>s<U+00FC>" /> Mustafa Kemal himself was required to take up a surname, and the Turkish Parliament gave him the surname Atat<U+00FC>rk, meaning father of the Turks.rk'<U+00FC>n imzas<U+0131>n<U+0131>n <U+00F6>yk<U+00FC>s<U+00FC>" /> Many members of parliament, including some of <U+00C7>er<U+00E7>iyan's former students, suggested that Atat<U+00FC>rk needed a new signature for his name. On a November morning in 1934, members of parliament presented the proposal to <U+00C7>er<U+00E7>iyan, who accepted the task.rk'<U+00FC>n imzas<U+0131>n<U+0131>n <U+00F6>yk<U+00FC>s<U+00FC>" /> That night, five model signatures were prepared and the following morning, police officers arrived to collect them.rk'<U+00FC>n imzas<U+0131>n<U+0131>n <U+00F6>yk<U+00FC>s<U+00FC>" /> Atat<U+00FC>rk personally selected the one of "K. Atat<U+00FC>rk" from these five model signatures.4

Legacy

According to Hagop <U+00C7>er<U+00E7>iyan's son Dikran <U+00C7>er<U+00E7>iyan, after the death of Atat<U+00FC>rk in 1938, his father's contribution to Atat<U+00FC>rk's signature risked being forgotten: "Some tried to introduce others as the creator of the signature. There were efforts to forget my father. But the truth always come[s] to the surface".References

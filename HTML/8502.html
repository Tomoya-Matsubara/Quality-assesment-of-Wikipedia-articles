Mezzamorphis is the second studio album by the Christian rock band Delirious?. It was released in 1999 to a largely positive critical reception, representing a large step towards a more electronic and mainstream sound for the band. However, despite the critical acclaim, many former Delirious? fans disliked the album, claiming that the band had sold out to the secular industry.

Mezzamorphis spawned two UK singles, "See the Star" and "It's OK", both of which landed in the top 20 of the UK Singles Charts, whilst the album itself peaked at number 25 on the UK Album Charts, giving Delirious? their second top 30 album in a row. The album has since gone silver in the UK.

Recording and production

Following their breakthrough 1997 album King of Fools, Delirious? started recording Mezzamorphis in February 1998, and continued to record throughout that year, interspersed with summer tours throughout the US and UK. The band began recording with roughly half the songs already written, and continued writing as they recorded.1 Most of the recording took place in an old school in West Sussex. In the words of Martin Smith, "We turned a classroom into a control room, and there were some wooden rooms where students used to dine, in which we set up a drum kit with different mike positions. We did the bulk of it there for the first six months in between touring. In the end, we went to a smaller studio in Ford just to finish it off."2 The band used movable drapes to experiment with different drum sounds and reverbs; most of the drums on the album are thus natural and unprocessed.3

Formerly known for a sound similar to that of U2,456 Delirious? aimed for a more dense and electronic sound, taking inspiration from Radiohead.789 CCM producer Tedd T was brought in to add aspects of electronica to the production.10 Recording was finished at a small studio near Littlehampton, putting the final touches on vocals.11

The album was mixed by Jack Joseph Puig12 in Los Angeles early in 1999. In the words of guitarist Stu G, "Martin and I went out to LA in January for three weeks and sat down with him and explained to him what we were doing and where we were coming from, what we were trying to accomplish with the music. He was very perceptive. He definitely took our tracks and made them into something we couldn't have done ourselves."13 Puig's mixing of the album deepened the bass sound and brought it more of a "club" feel.14

Content

As with the previous album, King of Fools, Mezzamorphis is mostly midtempo; notable exceptions include "Bliss"15 and "Gravity".16 The new electronic direction taken by the band incorporated a theremin, played by bassist Jon Thatcher; additionally, Stu G used Revox tape delays to create keyboard-like guitar effects.17 Classical instruments were also included, including a Salvation Army brass band on "It's OK",18 an upright bass (also played by Thatcher) on "Kiss Your Feet", and a string quartet throughout the album.

While Mezzamorphis is not strictly a concept album, more than one professional reviewer has pointed out that there is a general theme of getting to heaven running through the whole work.1920 The band have confirmed this,21 and have also pointed out a theme of change.22 "Heaven", inspired by the troubles in Northern Ireland, ties the two themes of heaven and change together; according to Smith, "we have to come to realize that we come from the dust, yet we believe one day we'll meet Jesus. In the meantime, we're caught between two worlds. Heaven is my home."23

The first track, "Mezzanine Floor", is the change-oriented centrepiece of the album, written about the musical growth of the band.24 According to Stu G, "we feel that we're at a point where we've left where we were, but we're not yet where we believe we're going to end up. So, we're on the way, in the middle, on the mezzanine floor."25 "Metamorphis", written solely by Stu G, further explores this theme of change. The name of the album is a mixture of the names of these two tracks (see Metamorphosis and Mezzanine).2627

Another strong theme from the album is peer pressure, particularly due to the band's part-Christian-part-mainstream status. The song "Gravity" refers to the opposing influences of Heaven and the secular world pulling the band in opposite directions, whilst "Metamorphis" further explores "the fact that out in the world, although everybody wants to be their own selves, we're pulled this way and that. Everyone you meet wants you to be something or another. The song asks God to help us to be our own true selves, as God sees us, not the way folk pressure us to be what they want us to be." "Bliss" deals with accusations of selling out; in the words of Stu G, "we know exactly where we want to go, we want to take our music to the world and have it recognized as being as good as anything that's out there. But we're not going to compromise or back away from our integrity as Christians."28

Promotion, reception, and response

The promotional UK tour featured American rock band Switchfoot as support.29 Delirious? also performed several songs live at Glastonbury Festival.3031

The first single, "See The Star", was released in the UK in 1999, selling over 5,000 copies in the first day and 12,000 copies in the first week. This was enough to push it to number sixteen in the UK Singles Charts and number two in the indies32 - the highest chart position ever held by a Delirious? single. A year later, the band released "It's OK", which entered the singles charts at number eighteen and the indie charts at number three.33 The song was featured an episode of the US television series Get Real.34 "Gravity" was planned to be released as a single in the UK,35 but was only released as a radio single in the US, due to Radio One's refusal to playlist the band.36

Mezzamorphis was released on 12 April 1999 in the United Kingdom. Despite the extensive publicity campaign, the album debuted at number 25 in the UK Album Charts and number 2 in the indies,37 lower than the band's first album, King of Fools; however, it was the second consecutive Top 30 release for the band. The album also reached number 2 in the CCM charts in America and number 137 in the Billboard 200 (number 5 in the Heatseekers) following its 8 June release.38

Critical response was generally positive in both the secular and UK Christian music media. Rock Sound magazine praised "the band's talent for writing instant rock/pop songs"39 and stated, "it shouldn't be long before Delirious? claim their rightful place as one of Britain's brightest new bands",40 whilst Q magazine named them "the hottest thing in Christian rock"41 and predicted, "Mezzamorphis will be the album that makes them".42 The UK Christian music magazine Cross Rhythms awarded the album a full ten-square rating, calling the album an "absolute classic",43 whilst Jesus Freak Hideout stated, "the musical growth is evident... and appreciated".44 The album also drew comparisons to U2's anthemic qualities4546 and was praised for its modern production.4748

In the United States, the Virgin marketed version omitted three songs that were included on the Sparrow version. Each of these contained explicit faith references.49 Additionally, the album caused some controversy in the US Christian music scene50 due to the lyric "she's as pretty as hell" in the song "It's OK".51 The band had left the song off promotional copies of the album, and Sparrow Records had considered leaving the song off the album;52 several Christian retailers dropped the album amongst the complaints.53 Smith wrote a response to the controversy on the band's website;54 however, many fans continued to believe that the band had left Christian rock for the secular industry. The band also received criticism for the lack of the word "Jesus" on the album.5556

In geometry, the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma and the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman<U+2013>Starr theorem study the Minkowski sums of many sets in a vector space, addressing the question, "how close is this sum to being convex?".2 The Shapley<U+2013>Folkman<U+2013>Starr results suggest that when the number of sets being summed exceeds the dimension of the vector space, then their Minkowski sum is approximately convex.3 The propositions of Shapley, Folkman, and Starr give an upper bound on the Euclidean distance of the Minkowski sum from the its convex hull (the smallest convex set that contains the Minkowski sum); this distance is zero exactly when the sum is convex. Their bound on the distance depends on the dimension D and on the shapes of the summand-sets, but not on the number of summand-sets N, Because it is determined by the shapes of a subcollection of only D summand-sets, the bound on the distance from the average sumset to its convex hull decreases to zero as the number of summands increases to infinity, for summand-sets of uniformly bounded size.4

Proved by Lloyd Shapley and Jon Folkman, the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma was first published by the economist, Ross M. Starr, who was investigating the existence of economic equilibria when some consumer preferences need not be convex.5 In his paper, Starr proved that a "convexified" economy has general equilibria that are closely approximated by "quasi-equilbria" of the original economy. The results of Shapley, Folkman, and Starr have applications in optimization and probability theory, in addition to their applications in economics and geometry.6

Preliminaries

The Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma depends upon the following definitions and results from convex geometry.

Real vector spaces

A real vector space of two dimensions may be given a Cartesian coordinate system in which every point is identified by a list of two real numbers, called "coordinates", which are conventionally denoted by x and y. Two points in the Cartesian plane can be added coordinate-wise

(x1, y1) + (x2, y2) = (x1+x2, y1+y2);

further, a point can be multiplied by each real number <U+03BB> coordinate-wise

<U+03BB> (x, y) = (<U+03BB>x, <U+03BB>y).

More generally, any real vector space of (finite) dimension D can be viewed as the set of all possible lists of D real numbers  } together with two operations: vector addition and multiplication by a real number. For finite-dimensional vector spaces, the operations of vector addition and real-number multiplication can each be defined coordinate-wise, following the example of the Cartesian plane.

Convex sets

In a real vector space, a set is defined to be convex if, or each pair of its points, every point on the line segment that joins them is covered by the set. For example, a solid cube is convex; however, anything that is hollow or dented, for example, a crescent shape, is non-convex. Vacuously, the empty set is convex.

More formally, a set Q is convex if, for all points v0 and v1 in Q and for every real number <U+03BB> in the unit interval [0,1], the point

(1 − <U+03BB>) v0 + <U+03BB>v1

is a member of Q.

By mathematical induction, a set Q is convex if and only if every convex combination of members of Q also belong to Q. By definition, a convex combination of an indexed subset {v0, v1, . . . , vD} of a vector space is any weighted average  for some indexed set of non-negative real numbers {<U+03BB>d} satisfying the equation  = 1.

The definition of a convex set implies that the intersection of two convex sets is a convex set. More generally, the intersection of a family of convex sets is a convex set.

Convex hull

For every subset Q of a real vector space, its is the unique minimal convex set that contains Q. It is defined to be the intersection of all the convex sets that cover Q. The convex hull of a set can be equivalently defined to be the set of all convex combinations of points in Q.

Convex hulls of Minkowski sums

Minkowski addition behaves well with respect to "convexification" <U+2014> the operation of taking convex hulls. Specifically, for all subsets Q1 and Q2 of a real vector space, the convex hull of their Minkowski sum is the Minkowski sum of their convex hulls. That is,

Conv( Q1 + Q2 ) = Conv( Q1 ) + Conv( Q2 ).

This result holds more generally. For each finite collection of non-empty sets,

Conv(  <U+2211> Qn  ) = <U+2211> Conv( Qn ).

In mathematical terminology, the operations of convexification and of Minkowski summation commute with each other.78

Proof and computations

The original proof of the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma established only the existence of the representation, but did not provide an algorithm for computing the representation. In 1981, Starr published an iterative method for computing a representation of a given sum-point; however, his computational proof provides a weaker bound than do the original results.16

Applications

The Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma has applications in economics, in optimization theory, and in probability.

Mathematical economics

In the era before Starr's paper, non-convex sets seemed to stump economists from proving that that, supply and demand could be "balanced" with several consumers and goods <U+2014> so that a general economic equilibrium could exist simultaneously for all consumers and for all the markets in all the goods.1718

Convex sets and economic equilibria

Improving Arrow and Debreu's model of an economy, Lionel W. McKenzie proved the existence of general equilibria by using Brouwer's theorem on the fixed points of a continuous function from a compact, convex set into itself. In McKenzie's approach to the Arrow<U+2013>Debreu model, convexity is essential, because such fixed-point theorems are inapplicable to non-convex sets.19 For example, the rotation of the unit circle by 90 degrees lacks fixed points, although this rotation is a continuous transformation of a compact set into itself; although compact, the unit circle is non-convex. In contrast, the same rotation applied to the convex hull of the unit circle leaves the point (0,0) fixed. Thus, non-convexity seemed to prevent economists from proving the existence of an equilibrium (with Brouwer's fixed-point theorem).20

Preceding papers

A sequence of papers in the Journal of Political Economy (JPE) discussed the approximate convexity of sums of non-convex sets from 1959 to 1961; the contributors were F. M. Bator, M. J. Farrell, T. C. Koopmans, and T. J. Rothenberg . These earlier and non-mathematical papers lacked the precise mathematical definitions, propositions, and proofs of later papers in mathematical economics.21 These JPE-papers stimulated a paper by Shapley and Shubik, which (like Starr) considered "convexified" consumer preference and introduced a notion of an "approximate equilibria".22 The JPE-papers and the Shapley<U+2013>Shubik paper influenced another notion of "quasi-equilibria", due to Aumann. Starr's convexification of preferences

Starr began his research while he was an undergraduate at Stanford University. He had enrolled in the (graduate) advanced mathematical-economics course of Kenneth J. Arrow, who provided Starr with an extensive and annotated reading-list.23 The Shapley<U+2013>Folkman results are named after Lloyd Shapley and Jon Folkman, who proved both the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma and a weaker version of the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman<U+2013>Starr theorem in an unpublished report, "Starr's problem" (1966), which was cited by .24

In his paper, Starr studied the general equilibria of the artificial economy in which non-convex preferences were replaced by their convex hulls. Starr was investigating the existence of economic equilibria when some consumer preferences need not be convex.25 Applying the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma, proved that the "convexified" economy has general equilibria that are closely approximated by some "quasi-equilbrium" of the original economy. Using his corollary, Starr derived a bound on the distance from a "quasi-equilbrium" to an equilibrium of a "convexified" economy, when the number of agents exceeds the dimension of the goods.26 With his 1969 paper, Starr extended the scope of the Arrow<U+2013>Debreu theory of general equilibrium beyond convex sets:

Thus, in the aggregate, the discrepancy between an allocation in the fictitious economy generated by [taking the convex hulls of all of the consumption and production sets] and some allocation in the real economy is bounded in a way that is independent of the number of economic agents. Therefore, the average agent experiences a deviation from intended actions that vanishes in significance as the number of agents goes to infinity.27

Being stated and often proved in economics textbooks, the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma and Starr's corollary require only linear algebra and Euclidean geometry. The approximate convexity of aggregates of non-convex sets have been studied with more advanced mathematics, particularly using measure theory and integration theory.34

The Shapley<U+2013>Folkman<U+2013>Starr results establish that non-convexities are compatible with approximate equilibria in markets with many consumers; these results also apply to production economies with many small firms. However, non-convexities arise in economic applications where the number of economic agents is relatively small, also; for markets with few agents, the aggregation-results of Shapley, Folkman, and Starr are inapplicable. Non-convexities remain important in oligopolies dominated by producers with increasing returns to&nbsp;scale, especially "natural monopolies" dominated by one producer.35 Non-convex sets arise also with externalities,36 with exchanges of information,37 and with incomplete markets,3839 the last being associated with stock markets.40 Such applications have continued to motivate economists to study non-convex sets.41 Chapter 8 "Applications to economics", especially Section 8.5.3 "Enter nonconvexity" (and the remainder of the chapter), particularly page 495:

Probability and measure theory

Convex sets may be studied with probability theory. Each point in the convex hull of a (non-empty) compact set is the expected value of a simple random vector that takes its values among the subset of extreme points, by the Krein<U+2013>Milman theorem. This correspondence between convex sets and simple random vectors implies that the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman<U+2013>Starr results are useful in probability theory and measure theory.44 In the other direction, probability theory provides tools to examine convex sets generally and the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman<U+2013>Starr results specifically.45 The Shapley<U+2013>Folkman<U+2013>Starr results have been widely used in the probabilistic theory of random sets,46 for example, to prove a law of large numbers,4748 a central limit theorem,4950 and a large-deviations principle.51 These probabilistic limit theorems dispense with the assumption that all the random sets be convex, because their proofs use the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman<U+2013>Starr results.

The Shapley<U+2013>Folkman<U+2013>Starr results have been used to refine the Brunn<U+2013>Minkowski inequality in the measure theoretic geometry of convex bodies.52 In the theory of vector measures, the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma has been used to prove Lyapunov's theorem, which states that the range of a (non-atomic) vector measure is closed and convex.53 In fact, Lyapunov's theorem has been viewed as a continuous analogue of the Shapley<U+2013>Folkman lemma.

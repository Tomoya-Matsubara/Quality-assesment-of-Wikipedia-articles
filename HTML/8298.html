[[Image:Goya_Y_no_hai_remedio.jpg|thumb|right|Goya, And it can't be helped (Y no hay remedio) from The Disasters of War (Los desastres de la guerra)]], c.1810<U+2013>1814, roughly contemporary with The Third of May. The Third of May 1808 (or El tres de mayo de 1808 en Madrid; Los fusilamientos de la monta<U+00F1>a del Pr<U+00ED>ncipe P<U+00ED>o,1 or Los fusilamientos del tres de mayo) is a painting completed in 1814 by the Spanish master Francisco Goya, in the Museo del Prado, Madrid. Along with its companion piece of the same size, The Second of May 1808 (also known as The Charge of the Mamelukes), it was commissioned by the provisional government of Spain at Goya's suggestion to commemorate Spanish resistance to Napoleon<U+2019>s armies during the occupation of 1808.

For its content, presentation and emotional force, the painting is widely regarded as groundbreaking, and an archetypal image of the horrors of war. Although Goya drew from a wide range of high and popular art sources when composing the work, in many respects The Third of May 1808 represents a clear break from established conventions. In its variance from both the traditional depictions of war and the traditions of Christian art the painting had no distinct precedent, and is acknowledged as one of the first paintings of the modern era.2 According to the art historian Kenneth Clark, The Third of May 1808 is "the first great picture which can be called revolutionary in every sense of the word, in style, in subject, and in intentionÂ ..."3

The Third of May 1808 has inspired a number of later major works, including those of <U+00C9>douard Manet, who based a series of five paintings on Goya's work, and Pablo Picasso, who directly referenced the painting in his Massacre in Korea and echoed it in his own masterpiece Guernica.

Background

On February 18, 1799, Napoleon I of France declared himself First Consul of the French Republic, and proclaimed himself Emperor of Europe later that year. Politically and strategically, Spain was important to French interests, as it controlled access to the Mediterranean. The reigning Spanish sovereign, Carlos IV, was regarded as a lightweight internationally. Even in his own court he was seen as a "half-wit king who renounces cares of state for the satisfaction of hunting", and a cuckold unable to control his energetic wife. Napoleon took advantage of the weak king by suggesting the two nations conquer and divide Portugal, with France and Spain each taking a third of the spoils, with the final third going to the Spanish Prime Minister Manuel de Godoy, along with the title of Prince of the Algarve. Godoy was seduced, and accepted the French offer. However, Godoy failed to grasp Napoleon's true intentions, and was unaware that his new ally and co-sovereign, the former King's son Ferdinand VII of Spain, was using the invasion merely as a device to seize the Spanish parliament and throne. Ferdinand intended not only that Godoy be killed during the takeover, but also intended to sacrifice the lives of his own parents during the impending power struggle.4

Under the guise of reinforcements, twenty three thousand French troops entered Spain unopposed in November 1807.5 Even when French intentions became clear the following February, the occupying forces found little resistance aside from isolated disconnected actions in areas, including Saragossa.6 Napoleon<U+2019>s principal commander, Marshall Joachim Murat, believed that Spain would benefit from more progressive and competent rulers than the Bourbons, while Napoleon's brother Joseph Bonaparte was intended to be made King.7 After Napoleon convinced Ferdinand to return Spanish rule to Carlos IV, Carlos was left with no choice but to abdicate on March 19, 1808, in favour of Joseph Bonaparte.

Although the Spanish people had accepted foreign monarchs in the past, they were deeply resentful of the new French rule. On May 2, 1808, provoked by news of the planned removal to France of the last members of the Spanish Royal family, the people of Madrid rebelled in the Dos de Mayo Uprising. The rebel fighters consisted largely of "ex-prisoners, vagabonds, and transients", according to contemporary accounts.89 Goya commemorated the uprising in his The Second of May which depicts a cavalry charge against the rebels in the Puerta del Sol, in the center of Madrid, the site of several hours of fierce combat.10 His The Third of May, much the better known of the pair, illustrates French reprisals on the following day, when hundreds of Spaniards were rounded up and shot. Civilian Spanish opposition remained important during the five years of the ensuing Peninsular War. In the original guerrilla war, from which the term derives,11 irregular Spanish forces considerably aided the Spanish and British armies led by the 1st Duke of Wellington, who first landed in Portugal in August 1808. By the time of the painting's conception, the public imagination had made the rioters symbols of heroism and patriotism.12

Like other Spanish liberals, Goya was placed in a difficult personal position by the French invasion. He had supported the initial aims of the French Revolution, and hoped for a similar development in Spain. Goya's 1798 portrait of the French ambassador turned Commandant Ferdinand Guillemardet betrays a personal admiration.1314 Yet while maintaining his position as court painter, for which an oath of loyalty to Joseph was necessary, Goya had by nature an instinctive dislike of authority.15 He witnessed the subjugation of his countrymen by the French troops.16 During these years he painted little, although the experiences of the occupation provided inspiration for drawings that would form the basis for his prints in the The Disasters of War (Los desastres de la guerra).17

In February 1814, after the final expulsion of the French, Goya approached the provisional government with a request to "perpetuate by means of his brush the most notable and heroic actions of our glorious insurrection against the Tyrant of Europe".18 His proposal accepted, Goya began work on the canvass. However, it is unknown whether he personally witnessed either the rebellion or the reprisals;19 despite many attempts to place him at the events of either day.20

The painting

Description

[[Image:Goya_No_se_puede_mirar.jpg|thumb|Goya, One cannot look at this, (No se puede mirar), The Disasters of War (Los desastres de la guerra)]], ca.1810<U+2013>1814, a very similar composition. The The Disasters series of etches all date from 1810s, but it is not known which prints pre-date the paintings. However it seems accepted by critics that Goya was freer in expression in the prints, and conformed more to traditional conventions in his paintings.21 The painting is set at night, and centers on two masses of men; one a rigidly poised firing squad, the other a disorganized group of captives held at gun point. Executioners and victims face each other abruptly across a narrow space; according to Clark, "By a stroke of genius [Goya] has contrasted the fierce repetition of the soldiers' attitudes and the steely line of their rifles, with the crumbling irregularity of their target."22 A square lantern is situated on the ground between the two groups, and throws a strong light, creating the effect of a staged scene. However, the painting is not intended by Goya as a formalized decoration, or political tableau.23 The brightest illumination falls on the huddled victims to the left, whose numbers include a monk or friar in prayer.24 To the immediate right and at the center of the canvas, a number of other condemned figures stand next in line to be shot.25 The central figure is the brilliantly lit man kneeling amid the bloodied corpses of those already executed, his arms flung wide in either appeal or defiance.26 His yellow and white clothing reflects the colors of the lantern.

On the right side of the canvas, engulfed in shadow, stands the firing squad, painted as a monolithic unit. Seen nearly from behind, their shakos and bayonets form a relentless and immutable column. Most of the faces of the figures cannot be seen, but the face of the man to the right of the main victim, peeping fearfully towards the soldiers, acts as a repoussoir at the back of the central group. Without distracting from the intensity of the foreground drama, a townscape with a steeple looms in the nocturnal distance.27 Between the hillside and the shakos there is a crowd with torches in the distance, perhaps onlookers, or more soldiers or victims.

One cannot look at this (No se puede mirar), one of the aquatint etchings from the series The Disasters of War (Los desastres de la guerra), is clearly related compositionally and thematically.28 It is unclear if this was produced before or after The Third of May 1808, as the series etching were apparently created throughout the 1810s. In One cannot look at this, the female central figure has her arms outstretched, but pointing down, whilst another figure has his hands clasped in prayer, and several others shield or hide their faces. This time the soldiers are not visible even from behind; only the bayonets of their guns are seen.

The Second and Third of May are thought to have been intended as part of a larger series.29 Written commentary and circumstantial evidence suggest that Goya may have painted four large canvases memorializing the rebellion of May 1808. In his memoirs of the Royal Academy in 1867 Jos<U+00E9> Caveda wrote of four paintings by Goya of The Second of May, and an artist and collector of Goya named Crist<U+00F3>bal F<U+00E9>rriz asserted the presence of two other paintings of the theme, a revolt at the royal palace and a defense of artillery barracks.30 Contemporary prints provide precedence for such a series. The disappearance of two paintings may indicate official displeasure with the subject, that of a popular insurrection.31

Provenance

Despite the work's commemorative value, there are no surviving contemporaneous accounts mentioning the painting, and it is unknown when or where it was first exhibited. This apparent lack of comment may be due to Fernando VII's preference for neoclassical art,32 while popular revolts of any kind were not regarded as suitable subject matter by the restored Bourbons. According to some accounts the painting lay in storage for thirty to forty years before being shown to the public.33 A monument to the fallen in the uprising, also commissioned in 1814 by the provisional government, "was stopped by Ferdinand VII, in whose eyes the senators and heroes of the war of independence found small favour, on account of their reforming tendencies"34 That the painting remained in the possession of the government or monarchy is indicated by its mention in an 1834 Prado inventory;35 much of the royal collection had been transferred to the Museum upon its opening in 1819. Th<U+00E9>ophile Gautier mentioned seeing 'a massacre' by Goya during a visit to the museum in 1845, while a visitor in 1858 noted the same painting. However both accounts refer to the work as depicting the events of the second of May;36 the Dos de Mayo Uprising continues to be the Spanish name for the whole episode. In 1867, Goya's biographer Charles Emile Yriarte considered the painting important enough to suggest it be honored by a special exhibition.37

It was not until 1872 that The Third of May was listed in the Prado's published catalog, under the title Scene of the Third of May 1808.38 The Prado mentions that both Pr<U+00ED>ncipe P<U+00ED>o paintings suffered damage while being transported to Valencia during the Spanish Civil War, apparently the only time they have left Madrid.39

Iconography and invention

The painting met with mixed reactions from art critics and historians when it first became public. Historically artists had tended to depict war in the high style of history painting, and Goya's unheroic description was unusual for the time. An early view by critics was that the painting was flawed on technical grounds; that the perspective is flat, or the victims and executioners are standing too close together to be realistic. Although these observations may be correct, according to the writer Richard Schickel, the fact that Goya did not strive for academic correctness lends to the overall force and impression of the piece.40

The Third of May references a number of earlier works of art, however its power resides not in its adherence to traditional compositional formulas, but in its bluntness.41 Pictorial artifice has here given way to the epic depiction of unvarnished brutality. Even the contemporary Romantic painters, who were also intrigued with subjects of injustice, war and death, composed their paintings with greater artifice. This and their awareness of the conventions of beauty, is evident in Th<U+00E9>odore G<U+00E9>ricault's Raft of the Medusa (1818<U+2013>19) and Eugene Delacroix's 1830 painting Liberty Leading the People.42

The painting is structurally and thematically tied to traditions of martyrdom in Christian art, for example in its use of light and darkness, and its appeal to life juxtaposed against the inevitability of imminent execution,.43 Even works within this tradition that depict violentce, such as those of Jusepe de Ribera, have a harmony in composition, and anticipate the "crown of martyrdom" for the victim.44

In The Third of May, the man with raised arms at the focal point of the composition has often been compared to a crucified Christ,45 and a similar pose is sometimes seen in depictions of Christ's nocturnal Agony in the Garden of Gethsemane.46 Goya's figure displays stigmata-like marks on his right hand,47 while the lantern at the center of the canvass references a traditional attribute of the Roman soldiers who arrested Christ in the garden.48 Not only is he posed as if in crucifixion, but he wears yellow and white, the heraldic colors of the Papacy.49 In The Third of May, however, there is no attempt to find transcendence, and no sense that the sacrifice of life will lead to salvation.

The lantern as a source of illumination in art was widely used by Baroque artists, and perfected by Caravaggio.50 Traditionally a dramatic light source and the resultant chiaroscuro were used as metaphors for the presence of God. Whether manifested by torch or candle light, illumination took on religious connotations. In The Third of May the role of the lantern indicates no such miracle. Rather, it affords light only so that the firing squad may complete its grim work, and provides a stark illumination so that we may bear witness to wanton violence. The traditional role of light in art as a conduit for the spiritual has been inverted.51

The victim is presented by Goya as anonymous as his killers, and his entreaty is delivered not, as in traditional painting, to God, but to an unheeding and impersonal firing squad.52 He is not granted the heroism of individuality, but is merely part of a continuum of victims. Beneath him lies a bloody and disfigured corpse; behind and around him are others who will momentarily share the same fate. Here, for the first time, nobility in individual martyrdom is replaced by futility and irrelevance, the victimization of mass murder, and anonymity seen as a modern condition.53

The progression of time in Goya<U+2019>s painting is a further element without precedent in Western art history.54 In earlier paintings, the death of a subject was typically depicted as a conclusive act, and endowed with the virtue of heroism. The Third of May furnishes no such cathartic message. Instead, there is a continuous procession of the condemned into a formalized mechanization of murder. That which is inevitable is seen in the corpse of the man splayed on the ground in the lower left portion of the work. There is no room left for the sublime; his head and body have been disfigured to a degree that renders resurrection impossible.55 The unfortunate victim is painted in a manner devoid of either aesthetic or spiritual grace. For the rest of the picture Goya has placed our eye level mostly along the central horizontal axis, only here changing the perspectival point of view so that we look down on the mutilated body.56

Finally, there is no attempt by the artist to ameliorate the subject<U+2019>s brutality through technical skill. Method and subject are indivisible. Goya<U+2019>s technique owes less to the artfulness of traditional virtuosity than it does to the intrinsic morbidity of the subject.57 The brushwork could not be described as pleasing, and the colors are restricted to earth tones and black, punctuated by bright flashes of white and the red blood of the victims. The quality of the pigment itself is characteristic of that which Goya would employ in many of his later works; a granular solution producing a matte, sandy finish.58 It is difficult to conceive of admiring the work for painterly flourishes, such is the forcefulness of its horror and its lack of theatricality.59

Sources

The works of art which appear to have been the most likely sources for the composition of The Third of May were drawn from popular imagery, prints and broadsides. Depicitions of firing squads were popular in Spanish political imagery during the Napoleonic War.60 Goya's appropriations suggest that he may have been interested in painting works of heroic scale that would appeal to the general public.61 Miguel Gamborino's 1813 devotional print The Assassination of Five Monks from Valencia is thought to have served as a source for Goya's composition.62 Points of similarity include a victim in a posture of crucifixion, whose white garment sets him apart from his companions; a tonsured monk with clenched hands who kneels to his left; and, an executed corpse lying in the foreground.63 Unlike Gamborino, however, Goya did not include angels in his scene.

The geometry of the composition may be an ironic comment on the French painter Jacques-Louis David's 1784 work Oath of the Horatii. The outstretched arms of David's three Roman Horatii in salute have become, in Goya's work, the rifles of the firing squad; the upraised arms of the Horatiis' father are, in the Goya, the victim's gesture as he faces the riflemen. While David painted his figures' expressions with a neoclassical luster, Goya's reply is fashioned from brutal realism.64 Goya may also have been responding to a painting by Antoine-Jean Gros; the French occupation of Madrid is the subject of Gros's Capitulation of Madrid, The Fourth of December 1808.65

Goya is thought to have been influenced by works of Rubens such as his versions of the Massacre of the Innocents, and The Horrors of War, painted after 1638. That Goya was influenced by Rubens is clear because he also painted a version of Saturn Devouring His Son, c.1819<U+2013>1823 that was influenced by Rubens' 1636 painting of the same name, now in the Prado. Kenneth Clark felt the Second of May an "artistic failure ... perhaps he could not shake off the memory of similar compositions by Rubens"66 Indicating his close interest in Rubens' work, Goya also produced a chalk drawing of the Saturn subject in 1796<U+2013>7 that was even closer in tone to Rubens.

Legacy

The first paraphrasing of The Third of May was <U+00C9>douard Manet's Execution of the Emperor Maximilian, painted in several versions in 1867<U+2013>69. In giving visual form to an event he did not witness, and which was not recorded in photographs,68 Manet apparently relied on the example of Goya's composition. It is possible that he saw Goya's painting at the Prado in 1865, before finishing his Execution for the Salon of 1867,69 and in any case he saw it in the form of a reproductive print that was published in 1867 by an acquaintance of Manet.70 Art critic Arthur Danto made a comparison between Goya's The Third of May and Manet's several versions of the Execution of the Emperor Maximilian:

The Third of May also depicts an execution, an early event in the so-called Peninsular War between France and Spain. Napoleon Bonaparte invaded Spain in 1808, capturing its royal family and replacing them with his brother, Joseph. The French were as unpopular in Spain as they later were in Mexico, and they encountered a fierce insurrection, which ultimately triumphed. The Third of May execution was an indiscriminate killing of civilians by French soldiers in reprisal for a guerrilla attack the previous day. Goya's painting of the massacre, which shows terrified civilians facing a firing squad, was intended to arouse anger and hatred on the part of Spanish viewers. Goya's is a highly romantic picture of a deeply emotional episode.71

The Third of May is cited as an influence on Pablo Picasso's 1937 Guernica, which depicts the aftermath of the Nazi German bombing of Guernica during the Spanish Civil War.72 In 2006, an exhibition at the Prado placed The Third of May in the same room as Guernica and the Execution of the Emperor Maximilian.73 Picasso's Massacre in Korea, painted in 1951 during the Korean War, is an even more direct reference to the composition of The Third of May;74 it seems that the perpetrators of the massacre were intended to be the South Korean army or their Western allies.

Aldous Huxley, who in 1959 published an essay on the etchings of Goya, thought Goya lacked Rubens ability to fill the canvas with an ordered composition. However, Huxley believed that the Tres de Mayo succeeded because, Goya "is speaking in his native language, and he is therefore able to express what he wants to say with maximum force and clarity".75

Kenneth Clark described the character of The Third of May by alluding to its radical departure from history painting and its singular intensity:

With Goya we do not think of the studio or even of the artist at work. We think only of the event. Does this imply that The Third of May is a kind of superior journalism, the record of an incident in which depth of focus is sacrificed to an immediate effect? I am ashamed to say that I once thought so; but the longer I look at this extraordinary picture and at Goya's other works, the more clearly I recognise that I was mistaken."''76

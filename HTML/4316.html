The Ray-Ban Wayfarer is a design of sunglasses manufactured by Ray-Ban since 1952, when their design was a revolutionary break from the metal eyewear of the past. Wayfarers enjoyed early popularity in the 1950s and 1960s, especially after they were worn by Audrey Hepburn in the 1961 movie Breakfast at Tiffany's. Though the sunglasses had faded from the limelight by the 1970s, a lucrative 1982 product placement deal brought Wayfarers to their height of popularity. Since the mid-2000s, the sunglasses have been enjoying a revival. Wayfarers are sometimes cited as the best-selling design of sunglasses in history12 and have been called one of the most enduring fashion icons of the 20th century.3

Design and early popularity

Wayfarers were designed in 1952 by Raymond Stegeman,45 an inventor who procured dozens of patents for Bausch and Lomb, Ray-Ban's parent company.6 The design was a radically new shape, "a mid-century classic to rival Eames chairs and Cadillac tail fins."7 According to design critic Stephen Bayley, the "distinctive trapezoidal frame spoke a non-verbal language that hinted at unstable dangerousness, but one nicely tempered by the sturdy arms which, according to the advertising, gave the frames a 'masculine look.'"8 Wayfarers, which took advantage of new plastics technology,9 marked the transition between a period of eyewear with thin metal frames and an era of plastic eyewear.10

Like Ray-Ban Aviators, Wayfarers were originally marketed as sunglasses for pilots.11 Despite the advertising campaign targeted at men, however, they quickly rose in popularity among Hollywood starlets.12 Kim Novak wore Wayfarers on the French Riviera in 1954,13 and Marilyn Monroe made Wayfarers into a "cult object."14 Audrey Hepburn's wearing of tortoise shell Wayfarers sunglasses the 1961 movie Breakfast at Tiffany's1516 was the design's turning point, "transforming the Wayfarer into an accessory legend."17 (Wayfarers sunglasses have gone through numerous design modifications during their history;18 Hepburn's oversized shades in Breakfast at Tiffany's are an example of the early Wayfarer silhouette.19) During the 1950s and 1960s, celebrities including John Lennon,20 Bob Dylan,21 James Dean,22 John F. Kennedy,23 Roy Orbison,24 and Andy Warhol25 were known for wearing Wayfarers.

1970s slump and 1980s comeback

After Wayfarers' heyday in the 1950s and 1960s, sales declined.26 Though Wayfarers were worn in the 1980 movie The Blues Brothers,27 only 18,000 pairs were sold in 1981,28 and Wayfarers were on the verge of discontinuation.29

The sunglasses' fate was reversed, however, when in 1982 Ray-Ban signed a $50,000-a-year deal with Unique Product Placement of Burbank, California, to place Ray-Bans in movies and television shows.30 (Between 1982 and 1987, Ray-Ban sunglasses appeared in over 60 movies and television shows per year;31 Ray-Ban's product placement efforts have continued through 2007.32) Tom Cruise's wearing of Wayfarers in the 1983 movie Risky Business marked the beginning of a Wayfarers phenomenon; 360,000 pairs were sold that year.33 By 1986, after further appearances in Miami Vice and Moonlighting, sales had reached 1.5 million.34 Wayfarers rose to popularity among musicians, including Johnny Marr,35 Blondie's Debbie Harry,36 Elvis Costello,37 Morrissey,38 Patti Smith,39 and members of U2,40 and among other celebrities such as Jack Nicholson41 and even Anna Wintour.42 Wayfarers are also mentioned in a lyric of Don Henley's 1984 song "The Boys Of Summer," You got that hair slicked back and those wayfarers on, baby. Bret Easton Ellis' fiction often name-dropped references to Wayfarers.43 Ray-Ban's Wayfarer offerings expanded from two models in 1981 to more than 40 models in 1989,44 and Wayfarers were the decade's sunglasses of choice.45

1990s decline and 2001 redesign

In the 1990s, the frames again became unpopular.46 The 1950s revival that fueled the glasses' popularity in the 1980s had lost steam, and Wayfarers were outcompeted by wraparound frames.47 In 2001, the Wayfarer underwent a significant redesign, with the frames made smaller and less angular, and changed from acetate to a lighter injected plastic.48 The changes were intended to update the frames' style during a period of unpopularity and to make them easier to wear (the frames' previous tilt made them impossible to perch on top of one's head, for instance).49 According to an eyewear boutique owner commenting in The Independent in 2007, however, the design was "diluted" and "horrible."50

Mid-2000s comeback

Wayfarers were brought back into fashion in the mid-2000s when celebrities including Chlo<U+00EB> Sevigny and Mary-Kate Olsen began wearing vintage frames.5152 Ray-Ban designers soon noticed that vintage Wayfarers were commanding high prices on eBay,53 and the 2007 re-introduction of the original Wayfarer design aimed to respond to the demand.545556 (As of 2007, Wayfarers were available in Original Wayfarer, New Wayfarer, and Wayfarer Folding styles.57) Ray-Ban's marketing strategy was threefold: a return to the sunglasses' original, rebellious design, an "edgy" advertising campaign and "high-profile PR events", and the use of new media like MySpace to connect with consumers.58 As of October 2007, the Wayfarer was the Luxottica Group's third-best-selling style.59

During the 2000s Wayfarer revival, many sunglasses designs inspired by the original Wayfarers were produced by designers unaffiliated with Ray-Ban. Grey Ant's Grant Krajecki designed a larger, cartoonish version of the glasses "so extreme that [they] are best worn by those with a good sense of humor".60 Sabre Vision's "Poolside" design is a smaller, thinner version that resembles "a cross between old-school Oakleys and the pair worn by Tom Cruise in 'Risky Business'".61 Other Wayfarer-inspired sunglasses included Oliver Peoples' "Hollis", REM Eyewear's "Converse", and various designs in Juicy Couture, Hugo Boss, Kate Spade, and Marc Jacobs' 2008 lines.62

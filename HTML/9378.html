Congregation is the third studio album by American alternative rock band The Afghan Whigs, released January 31, 1992, on Sub Pop. After touring for two years in support of their 1990 album Up in It, the band received $15,000 advance from Sub Pop to record a follow-up album. It was primarily recorded at Bear Creek Studios in Woodinville and Buzz's Kitchen in Los Angeles during July to August 1991. The band's frontman Greg Dulli produced the album entirely with audio engineer Ross Ian Stein. The album's production and release were hindered by Sub Pop's financial difficulties, which were eventually resolved by the massive commercial success of Nirvana's 1991 album Nevermind.

Expanding on the aggressive sound of the band's previous work, Congregation has more refined musical details and slower songs such as ballads. Its music also incorporates R&B, soul, and funk influences. The album's cover depicts a nude black woman holding a white baby in her arms and alludes to the band's African-American music roots. The songs have themes of pleasure and guilt, and deal with topics such as dysfunctional relationships and sexual deviancy. Conceived during the burgeoning grunge scene, the album has been viewed by music writers as stylistically distinct from other Sub Pop and grunge music.

Upon its release, Congregation was very well received by music critics, who praised its musical quality, stylistic elements, and emotional content. It was promoted with two singles, "Turn On the Water" and "Conjure Me", and The Afghan Whigs' extensive touring in the United States and Europe. Although it did not chart, the album helped the band attain a cult following and receive offers from major record labels, leading to their signing to Elektra Records. Since its initial reception, Congregation has been viewed by writers as the band's breakthrough album and a significant release in rock music during the 1990s.

Background

After forming in 1986 in Cincinnati, The Afghan Whigs<U+2014>vocalist and rhythm guitarist Greg Dulli, bassist John Curley, lead guitarist Rick McCollum, and drummer Steve Earle<U+2014>released their 1988 debut album Big Top Halloween on an independent record label and gained the attention of Seattle-based label Sub Pop.1 With their second album Up in It (1990) released on the label, the band toured regularly in the United States for two years and occasionally in Europe before recording Congregation.2 Sub Pop gave the band a $15,000 advance to record the album, which Dulli later said was a "then-unheard-of" and "bloated" amount.3

The album's cover shows a nude black woman sitting on a blanket with a white baby held in her arms.4 It serves as a comical reference to the band's African-American music roots.5 Along with a collective interest in classic rock, band members had other individual tastes in music, including McCollum's interest in free jazz, experimental, and Indian music, and Dulli's love of hip hop, soul,6 and funk, particularly Motown artists and Prince.7

Recording

The Afghan Whigs began recording the album in July 1991.9 They held recording sessions for Congregation at Bear Creek Studios in Woodinville, Washington and Buzz's Kitchen in Los Angeles, California.10 Dulli wrote most of its songs and produced the album with audio engineer Ross Ian Stein.11 Session musicians such as Miss Ruby Belle, Shecky Stein, and Lance Ellison also contributed; Belle sung lead vocals on "Her Against Me", Stein played piano on "Conjure Me", and Ellison played piano and sung lead vocals on "The Temple" with John Curley and Rick McCollum.12 Singer-songwriter Shawn Smith contributed backing vocals to "This Is My Confession" and "Dedicate It".13 After the rest of the band finished recording their parts, Dulli stayed in Los Angeles to record his vocals, overdub the recorded tracks, and mix the album.14 However, the album's production was delayed due to Sub Pop's financial difficulties, and Dulli got another job in Los Angeles to pay for the studio.15

Although the label eventually settled the studio's bills and the album's sessions ended in August 1991,16 the band was unsure if Sub Pop had enough money to release any more albums, let alone Congregation.17 After the massive commercial success of Nirvana's 1991 album Nevermind,18 Sub Pop recovered financially by receiving royalties from the album after the band left them for a major label.19 Dulli later said that while he was "stranded in L.A. ... Nirvana saved the whole fuckin' label".20 He recalled thanking the band's frontman Kurt Cobain upon the album's completion, saying that "I went to Nirvana's show at the Palace in L.A. and hugged Kurt and said, 'Good one, bro. Congratulations. And thank you<U+2014>now I can go home.'"21

After the album's sessions were done, Dulli was inspired to write and record an additional track on the day of Miles Davis' death,22 September 28, 1991.23 He received two messages on his answering machine from music executive David Katznelson, the first providing directions to a cookout and the second informing Dulli that Davis had passed, saying that "Miles is dead. Don't forget the alcohol."24 Working at Ultrasuede in Cincinnati,25 Dulli incorporated Katznelson's line as a lyric and, with a guitar riff from Rick McCollum, hastily recorded "Miles Iz Ded", which was included as a hidden track on the album.26 Congregation was mastered by Jack Skinner at K Disc Mastering in Hollywood.27 Dulli subsequently took a drive in his car and listened to the album while observing his scenery, a practice he continued with future albums.28

Composition

Music and style

The album expands on aggressive sound of the band's previous work with refined musical details and occasionally slower songs.2930 In The Rough Guide to Rock (2003), music journalist Alex Ogg writes that The Afghan Whigs were "managing to balance volume with subtlety" amid the burgeoning grunge music scene.31 The album's music incorporates heavy counterpoint rhythms, indie rock harmonics,32 wah-wah and slide guitars,33 and staccato riffs with funk, metal, and blues influences.3435 Chicago Tribune writer Brad Webber compares its melodies to those of "later H<U+00FC>sker D<U+00FC>".36 Option perceives influences from "ex-indie guitar bands" such as Dinosaur Jr. and Nirvana in the songs' "catch-iness and in the arrangements' juxtaposition of frenzy and laziness."37 David Sprague of the Trouser Press comments that the band's instrumentation "contriv[es] panoramic images through judicious use of wah-wah guitar ... and the tribal drumbeats with which Steven Earle invokes an air of ritualistic surrender."38

The album's rock sound is complemented by a predominant soul influence.39 Piero Scaruffi calls the album "a calmer and catchier collection" than Up in It and notes elements of R&B and soul ballads.40 Christophe Basterra of Magic characterizes the music as "The Four Tops appropriating 'Search and Destroy' by Iggy & the Stooges".41 The slow-burning ballad "Let Me Lie to You" incorporates soul and grunge musical styles.42 Dulli wrote the song in an attempt to deviate from the aesthetic of Sub Pop, whom he felt discouraged its acts from recording slow songs.43 He cites writing the song as the moment he began having faith in himself as a songwriter.44 Songs such as "Miles Iz Ded",45 "Conjure Me", and "Turn On the Water" incorporate funk influences and wah-wah guitar.4647 Pitchfork Media's Stephen M. Deusner cites the latter song as an example of how the band "integrate[d] African-American sounds and influences into their white rock", writing that it "uses Isaac Hayes' wakka-chikka guitars as a punk accessory, and its jumpy guitar riffs instill these songs with a sense of motion that suggests amped-up r&b."48 "Tonight" features bluesy acoustic guitar.49

Lyrical themes

The songs discuss dysfunctional intimate relationships and sexual deviancy,5051 and feature themes of pleasure and guilt.52 They are narrated from the perspective of a broken sinner and lothario with sinister and narcissistic tones.5354 Dulli's lyrics incorporate haunting refrains and express angst and melodrama.555657 His baritone vocals are moaningly husky and feature falsetto wails.5859 Daniel Fidler of Spin compares Dulli's vocals on the album to those of "a young Paul Westerberg in heat."60 David Sprague of the Trouser Press calls Congregation a "strangely flamboyant" album that showcases "Dulli's metamorphosis from everypunk wallflower to rakish scoundrel with a heart of glass."61 Jason Ankeny of Allmusic calls Dulli "a truly magnetic presence" and writes of his performance on the album, "by turns predator ('Tonight') and prey ('I'm Her Slave'), he's the guy your parents always warned you about, delivering each syllable of his remarkable lyrics with equal measures of innuendo and venom."62 Stephanie Benson of Rhapsody comments that his lyrics are "full of stifling dissatisfaction and boozy philosophy".63

"I'm Her Slave" is a heroin anecdote with lyrics narrated by a subjugate lover.64 Music critic Greg Kot cites "Turn On the Water" as an example of when "the twisted narrator is the victim" and "cast adrift" in Dulli's lyrics.65 Inspired by a paranoid breakup,66 "Conjure Me" is told from the perspective of an aggressive predator and obscure object of desire.6768 On "Kiss the Floor", the narrator recounts stealing a girl's virginity and avoiding her brothers.69 "This Is My Confession" has a theme of absolution.70 The lyrics depicts it as an empty sexual experience: "Shove my head against the door, crawl inside and kiss the floor / Waiting for the sun again, drink it, smoke it, stick it in."71 "The Temple" is a cover of the song of the same name from the 1970 rock opera Jesus Christ Superstar.72 Dulli became a fan of the rock opera as a child when his babysitter played it.73 "Let Me Lie to You" has lyrics expressing passive cruelty and subtle manipulation.74 "Tonight" depicts a peaceful night as the backdrop for the narrator's corrupt one-track mind: "Follow me down to the bushes, dear / No one will know, we'll disappear / I'll hold your hand, we'll never tell / Our private little trip to hell".75 The album's hidden track "Miles Iz Ded" is about seduction and alcohol,76 with a last call sensibility and despairing tone.7778

Releasen and promotion

The album was released by Sub Pop on January 31, 1992,79 and distributed by Caroline Records.80 It was issed on LP and CD formats.81 Congregation was promoted with two singles. "Turn On the Water" was released in January as a 7-inch single with "Miles Iz Ded" on its B-side.82 It was also released as a CD single coupled with the tracks "Chalk Outline" and "Miles Iz Ded".83 On February 25, "Turn On the Water" was released as a cassette single.84 "Conjure Me" was released in May as a 7-inch single backed with a cover of The Supremes' 1965 song "My World Is Empty Without You".85 A music video for the single was filmed, featuring a topless woman caressing Dulli as he lip syncs the song,86 and received rotation on MTV.87 Congregation was reissued in September 1998.88 It was released in the United Kingdom on November 22, 1999, through PIAS Recordings.89

The Afghan Whigs promoted the album with an appearance on MTV and an international tour with dates in major American cities and Europe.90 During their shows, the band interspersed R&B songs in their set list, including an interpolation of The Spinners' 1972 song "I'll Be Around" in "Turn On the Water".91 Their extensive touring for the album gained the band a larger audience.92

Reception

Although it did not chart,93 Congregation received very positive reviews from music critics upon its release.94 Michael Azerrad of Rolling Stone called it "superb".95 Daniel Fidler of Spin called the album "a clean move toward musical excellence" and commented that it "shows the band bursting its Sub Pop chains with catchy, heavy guitar riffs, downright lovable song arrangements, melodies that jerk your emotions back and forth, and yes, endearing, raspy vocals that wail, moan, scream, and shout".96 Brad Webber of the Chicago Tribune described the album as "a more accessible brand of music than normally associated with the Seattle-Minneapolis axis of grunge guitar groups."97 He found The Afghan Whigs to be "on rock's cutting edge" and commented that the band "stirs a torrent of emotions on neatly paced songs".98

J. D. Considine of Musician characterized the band as "clever enough to find hooks in the sort of gnarled riffs and guitar noise Fugazi plays for atmosphere", as well as "canny enough to avoid most of the mannerisms that make much Amerindie rock seem so cliched."99 Considine concluded in his review, "The best reason to join this Congregation is that the songs are catchy enough to hold their own against the best work of bands like Dinosaur Jr. or the Replacements."100 In a mixed review, Option commended the album's "sheer listenability", "clear recording and dynamic performances", but criticized Dulli's subject matter and panned the band's cover of "The Temple".101 Melody Maker ranked the album number 25 on its year-end best albums for 1992.102

With the album's critical acclaim and their steady touring,103 the band attained a cult following and was courted by several major labels who wanted to sign them.104105 Frustrated with the distribution limitation of a small record label,106 they signed to Elektra Records after their final release for Sub Pop, an EP of soul cover songs called Uptown Avondale (1992).107 Their major label debut Gentlemen was released in 1993 to critical acclaim and mainstream exposure.108

Legacy

Along with Gentlemen, Congregation is generally considered by music writers to be part of The Afghan Whigs' peak era.109110111 Dulli cites it as "the record where we came into our own".112 Melody Maker dubbed it "the finest rock LP of the decade" and commented that it is "nothing less than rock raping pop, a ferocious deflowering of Motown's romantic ideal".113 With Congregation, Stephanie Benson of Rhapsody found the band to be "crucial to the birth of '90s alternative rock."114 Allmusic editor Jason Ankeny viewed the album as the band's artistic breakthrough and "ticket to the big leagues", calling it "an incendiary and insidious set which bridges the gap between the noisy aggression of the band's early releases and the soulful swagger of their later work."115 Ankeny also cited it as "the grunge era's most overlooked masterpiece" and an indication of the band's musical growth, writing that "while still unmistakably a member of the Sub Pop stable, there's a greater maturity and depth to their sinewy sound, with a newfound grasp of mood and nuance".116

Yahoo! Music's Chris Norris called it a "disjointed set, which finds the band suspended between its punky roots and a more voluptuous later style."117 The Spin Alternative Record Guide (1995) noted "a creative musical blend" on the album, but concluded that "the results feel like dress rehearsals for Gentlemen, with one partial exception, 'Conjure Me,' and one absolute triumph: a surging final track ... that might be the Afghan Whigs' strongest recorded performance."118 In The New Rolling Stone Album Guide (2004), Rolling Stone journalist Joe Gross gave the album three-and-a-half out of five stars and viewed it as a "quantum leap" over the band's previous work, commenting that it "shows that they ditched grunge for soul because they were no damn good at the former and ladies dig the latter."119 In 2002, Italian music magazine Il Mucchio Selvaggio included the album in its list of 100 Best Albums by Decade.120 Polish webzine Screenagers ranked it number 79 on its 2004 list of the Top 100 Albums of the '90s.121 Italian music journalist Eddy Cil<U+00EC>a and Federico Guglielmi included Congregation in their 2010 book on essential rock albums.Track listing

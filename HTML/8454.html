(pronounced ) is the Irish name for the island of Ireland and the sovereign state of the same name.

Etymology

The modern Irish <U+00C9>ire evolved from the Old Irish word <U+00C9>riu, which was the name of a Gaelic goddess. <U+00C9>riu is generally believed to have been the matron goddess of Ireland, a goddess of sovereignty, or simply a goddess of the land. The origin of <U+00C9>riu has been traced to the Proto-Celtic reconstruction *<U+03A6><U+012B>werjon (nominative singular <U+03A6><U+012B>werj<U+014D>).1 This suggests a descent from the Proto-Indo-European reconstruction *piHwerjon, likely related to the adjectival stem *piHwer- (cf. Sanskrit p<U+012B>van, p<U+012B>var<U+012B> and p<U+012B>vara meaning "fat, full, abounding"). This would suggest a meaning of "abundant land".

This Proto-Celtic form became <U+012A>weri<U+016B> or <U+012A>veri<U+016B> in Proto-Goidelic.2 It is highly likely that explorers borrowed and modified this term. During his exploration of northwest Europe (circa 320 BCE), Pytheas of Massilia called the island Ierne (written ). In his book Geographia (circa 150 CE), Claudius Ptolemaeus called the island Iouernia (written ). Based on these historical accounts, the Roman Empire called the island Hibernia.

Thus, the evolution of the word would follow as such:

  • Proto-Celtic *<U+03A6><U+012B>werjon (nominative singular <U+03A6><U+012B>werj<U+014D>)
    • Proto-Goidelic *<U+012A>weri<U+016B> or *<U+012A>veri<U+016B>
      • Old Irish <U+00C9>riu
        • Modern Irish <U+00C9>ire

Largely discredited etymologies

Other explanations for the etymology of <U+00C9>ire are:

  • Derived from a root word Ara (also spelt Arya, Aire or Aera) meaning noble, as in 'Aryan'. Among the very many poetic names for the island of Ireland was M<U+00E1>g Ealga meaning plain of the nobles.
  • Ar or Ir in the Irish language also meant land, and according to old manuscripts was the name given to the lands of the mythological Celtic tribe of Go<U+00ED>del Glas who travelled from Scythia across Greece and eventually to Ireland.

Difference between <U+00C9>ire and Erin

While <U+00C9>ire is simply the name for Ireland in the Irish language, and sometimes used in English, Erin is a common poetic name for Ireland in English, as in Erin go bragh. The distinction between the two is one of the difference between cases of nouns in Irish. <U+00C9>ire is the nominative case, the case that (in the modern Gaelic languages) is used for nouns that are the subject of a sentence i.e. the noun that is doing something as well as the direct object of a sentence. Erin derives from <U+00C9>irinn (pronounced ), the Irish dative case of <U+00C9>ire, which has replaced the nominative case in D<U+00E9>ise Irish (and some non-standard sub-dialects elsewhere), in Scottish Gaelic (where the usual word for Ireland is ) and Manx Gaelic, where the word is spelled Nerin, with the initial n- is probably in origin a fossilisation of the preposition in/an "in" (cf. Irish in <U+00C9>irinn, Scottish an <U+00C8>irinn/ann an <U+00C8>irinn "in Ireland"). The genitive case <U+00C9>ireann is used in the Gaelic forms of the titles of companies and institutions in Ireland e.g. Iarnr<U+00F3>d <U+00C9>ireann (Irish Rail), D<U+00E1>il <U+00C9>ireann (Irish Parliament) or Poblacht na h<U+00C9>ireann (The Republic of Ireland).

<U+00C9>ire as a state name

Article 4 of the Irish constitution adopted in 1937 provides that: "The name of the state is <U+00C9>ire, or, in the English language, Ireland."3 The Constitution's English-language preamble also described the population as "We, the people of <U+00C9>ire". The Republic of Ireland Act enacted in 1948 makes clear that the "Republic of Ireland" is a description and not a name of the state. Ireland (in English) and <U+00C9>ire (in Irish) remain its two official names. Article 8 states that both Irish and English are the official languages of the state with Irish designated as the "national" and "first official" language. "<U+00C9>ire" has to some extent passed out of everyday conversation and literature.

The name "<U+00C9>ire" has been used on Irish postage stamps since 1922; on all Irish coinage (including Irish euro coins); and together with "Ireland" on passports and other official state documents issued since 1937. "<U+00C9>ire" is used on the Official Seal of the President of Ireland. Before the 1937 Constitution, "Saorst<U+00E1>t <U+00C9>ireann" (the Irish name of the Irish Free State) was generally used.

During the Emergency (as World War II was known), Irish ships had "EIRE" and the Irish tricolour painted large on their sides and deck, to identify them as neutrals.

From 1938 to 1962 the international plate on Irish cars was marked "EIR", short for <U+00C9>ire, until statutory instrument no. 269 of 1961 allowed "IRL".4 In 1922-1938 it was "SE", and from 1962 "IRL" has been adopted. Irish politician Bernard Commons TD suggested to the D<U+00E1>il in 1950 that the government examine "the tourist identification plate bearing the letters EIR" "with a view to the adoption of identification letters more readily associated with this country by foreigners".5 "EIR" is also shown in other legislation such as the car insurance statutory instrument no. 383 of 1952 and no. 82 of 1958.67

Under the 1947 Convention Irish-registered aircraft have carried a registration mark starting "EI" for <U+00C9>ire.

From January 2007, the Irish government nameplates at meetings of the European Union have borne both <U+00C9>ire and Ireland, following the adoption of Irish as a working language of the European Union.

Use of Eire in Britain

In 1938 the British government provided in the Eire (Confirmation of Agreements) Act 1938 that British legislation could henceforth refer to the Irish Free State as "Eire" (but not as "<U+00C9>ire"). The 1938 Act was repealed in 1981, and in the meantime Eire became a rare but accepted spelling in British English, but has now passed out of general use.

Other uses

<U+00C9>ire has also been incorporated into the names of Irish commercial and social entities, such as "eircom plc" (formerly "Telecom <U+00C9>ireann") and its former mobile phone network, and Eircell 8 In 2006 the Irish electricity network was devolved to EirGrid. The company "BetEire Flow" (eFlow), named as a pun on "better", is a French consortium running the electronic tolling system at the West-Link bridge west of Dublin.9 According to the Dublin Companies Registration Office in 2008, over 500 company names incorporate the word <U+00C9>ire in some form.10

Sometimes the incorporation is humorous or ironic, such as the pop group named "Scary<U+00C9>ire",11 or Cormac <U+00D3> Gr<U+00E1>da's "<U+00C9>irvana" paper in 2007 on the Celtic Tiger economy.12

History

The Lottie Moon House, which is located at 220 East High Street in Oxford, Ohio was completed in 1831. It was not named Lottie Moon House, but rather adopted the name after being inhabited by the Moon family in 1839. The nineteenth century architecture is a simple design enhanced by a one story wooden porch that spans most of the entire front of the home. The front of the home is symmetrical which adds to its simple beauty. 1

The Lottie Moon House is part of the historic neighborhood that surrounds Miami University. The home was built for residence, but after Lottie Moon became famous for being a Confederate Spy, the home was deemed of historic importance, and it was donated to Miami University to be apart of their campus.

Lottie Moon

Cynthia Charlotte Moon, fondly known as Lottie, was a young girl when her family moved into the residency at 220 East High Street. Lottie became famous as a Confederate Spy. She rode the battle lines in the South in President Abraham Lincoln's personal carriage. Lottie was disguised as "Lady Hull", a rheumatic English invalid. She pretended to be asleep while President Lincoln and Secretary of War, Edwin M. Stanton, discussed upcoming strategies. 2

The two Union men believed that they were taking Lady Hull to the South for a warm springs treatment for her sickness. Little did they know they were delivering the infamous Confederate Spy, Lottie Moon, into the expectant hands of Jefferson Davis.

Stanton offered $10,000 for the capture of Lottie Moon after realizing Lady Hull was Lottie Moon. 3

Past Residents

The Moon family was the first and most famous family to reside at 220 East High Street. Robert Moon, Lottie's father, moved his family into the home in 1839, and they lived as a family in the home until 1849. In 1849, Robert Moon offered the home as a gift to Lottie upon the announcement of her engagement to James Clark. However, Lottie moved to the South to be closer to the war and better able to relay messages. 4 Robert and his family continued to live in the home until his death in 1856.

Dr. John Hall, president of Miami University from 1855 to 1866, moved into the home with his family. They lived in this home until his resignation from presidency. 5 In 1870, Milo Sawyer, a retired oil cloth manufacturer moved into the home with his wife, Fannie, and their two children. He was a university professor and resided in the home for two years.

Sutton C. Richey bought the home from Milo Sawyer in 1873. Richey was a druggist. His wife, Rella, and their three children lived in the home. Richey had a practice in the McCullough Building located at 20 East High Street from 1859 until 1891. When Mr. Richey died, Rella lived in the home with their daughter, Jeannie, until Rella's son, Samuel Webster Richey, took the home for himself. He passed it on to his son. It was upon the death of Samuel Webster Richey Jr. that the home was donated to Miami University by his sons. 6 Sheffield Jr. and Tom Richey donated the home to Miami University in 1988, and it was recognized as a piece of historic property. The gift was accepted by Provost E. Fred Carlisle who recognized that "Miami University values its Oxford heritage very much. This house demonstrates the richness of that heritage." 7

Details of Lottie Moon House

The home was originally a residency, but now is used as a historic site for the City of Oxford, Ohio. There is a partial basement. The foundation is stone block, and the wall is brick bearing. The exterior material brick is American bond, and the roof type is truncated hip with asphalt shingles. 8

The wooden porch on the front of the house is single story and spans the entire front of the home. The windows are six feet by six feet. There are three on the second story, and two surrounding the front door on the first story.

The house faces the Miami University campus. There is a fence around the premises with a gate topped with a lion's head that was made by Sutton C. Richey, a former owner. 9

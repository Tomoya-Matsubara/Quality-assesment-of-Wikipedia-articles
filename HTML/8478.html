Alexis Bachelot (born John Augustine Bachelot, February 22, 1796 <U+2013> December 5, 1837) was a Roman Catholic priest best known for his tenure as the first Prefect Apostolic of the Sandwich Islands. In that role, he led the first permanent Catholic mission to the Kingdom of Hawaii. Bachelot was raised in France, where he attended the Irish College in Paris, and was ordained a priest in 1820. He led the first Catholic mission to Hawaii, arriving in 1827. Although he had expected the approval of then Hawaiian King Kamehameha&nbsp;II, he learned upon arrival that Kamehameha II had died and a new government that was hostile towards Catholic missionaries had been installed. Bachelot, however, was able to convert and then quietly minister to a small group of Hawaiians for four years before being deported in 1831 on the orders of Ka<U+02BB>ahumanu, the Kuhina Nui (a position similar to queen regent) of Hawaii.

Bachelot then traveled to California, where he served as an assistant minister while pastoring and teaching. In 1837, having learned of Queen Ka<U+02BB>ahumanu's death and King Kamehameha&nbsp;III's willingness to allow Catholic priests on the island, Bachelot returned to Hawaii, intending to continue his missionary work. However, by Bacehlot's arrival, Kamehameha III had again changed his mind and Bachelot was removed from the island and confined to a ship for several months. He was freed only after the French and British navies imposed a naval blockade on the Honolulu harbor. Although he was later able to secure passage on a ship to Micronesia, he died en route and was buried on an islet near Pohnpei. His treatment in Hawaii prompted the government of France to dispatch a frigate to the island; the resulting intervention is known as The French Incident and led to the emancipation of Catholics in Hawaii.

Early life

Jean Augustin Bachelot was born in Saint-Cyr-la-Rosi<U+00E8>re, Orne, France on February 22, 1796.1 In 1806, he left home for Paris, where he enrolled in the Preparatory Seminary of Piepus to pursue priesthood. In 1813, he professed at the Congregation of the Sacred Hearts of Jesus and Mary,2 taking the name Alexis.3 He studied at the Irish College in Paris before being ordained as a priest in 1820. As a priest, he initially served as the College's rector and later led the preparatory seminary at Tours.4

Hawaiian mission

In the early 1820s, Jean Baptiste Rives, a French adviser to the Hawaiian king Kamehameha&nbsp;II, traveled to Europe to attempt to convince European Catholics to organize a mission to Hawaii. Members of the Congregation of the Sacred Hearts of Jesus and Mary were receptive to his idea,5 and in 1825, Pope Leo&nbsp;XII assigned them the task of evangelizing Hawaii.6 Bachelot was appointed the Prefect Apostolic of the Sandwich Islands, and in this role led the first permanent mission to Hawaii.7

The expedition was organized by the influential Monneron family and funded by the government of France. Bachelot was assisted in his new position by fellow priests Patrick Short and Abraham Armand, as well as several lay brothers. The mission sailed from Bordeaux on the La Com<U+00E8>te in November 1826. The missionaries were initially accompanied by a group that planned to explore commercial trading opportunities8 but returned to France after reaching Mexico.9 Unbeknownst to Bachelot, political changes occurred in Hawaii prior to the mission's arrival. King died in 1824 and his younger brother became king.10 Because was young at the time of his ascension, Queen Ka<U+02BB>ahumanu (their stepmother) ruled as Kuhina Nui.11 On the advice of Hiram <U+2014>a Protestant missionary who had converted the Hawaiian royalty four years previously<U+2014>Queen Ka<U+02BB>ahumanu took a hard stance against Catholicism. Rives' influence on the Hawaiian government had faded,12 and he never returned to Hawaii.13

The La Com<U+00E8>te arrived in Honolulu on July 7, 1827. The priests were faced with a situation of dire poverty owing to the absence of Rives' patronage.14 Furthermore, they had promised the La Com<U+00E8>te's captain that Rives would pay for their passage after they arrived in Hawaii, but by the time of their arrival, Rives had already left Hawaii.15 Queen Ka<U+02BB>ahumanu refused to allow the missionaries to stay in Hawaii, suspecting them to be covert agents of the government of France.16 She instructed the La Com<U+00E8>te's captain to take the mission with him when he departed.17 The captain, however, refused to do so because he did not receive payment for their passage, so the party was able to remain.18 The priests began their missionary work, but encountered suspicion from most chiefs. The members of the party had great difficulty defending themselves, as none of them were fluent in English or Hawaiian.19 The group received a favorable response, however, when they met high chief Boki,20 the royal governor of Oahu, and his wife Kuini Liliha. (The couple were Catholic converts and rivals of Queen Ka<U+02BB>ahumanu.) Boki welcomed the party and gave its members permission to stay.21

For several months, Bachelot and his fellow missionaries lived in three small rented structures,22 saying their first mass on the island in a grass hut.23 They later built a chapel on a small plot of land they purchased,24 where the Cathedral of Our Lady of Peace25 was dedicated in 1843.26 After settling on the island, the group avoided drawing attention to themselves and studied the Hawaiian language.27 During their mission's first two years, the group converted 65 Hawaiians28 and ministered to Hawaiians who had already been converted.29 They often held surreptitious night-time meetings with converts who feared persecution.30 The priests' vestments and rituals made their evangelism efforts more effective because they reminded Hawaiians of native religious customs.31

Bachelot introduced two plant species to Hawaii: Prosopis humilis32 and the Bougainvillea.33 Prosopis humilis trees later covered thousands of acres there.34 He had obtained the seeds, which were originally gathered by Catholic missionaries in California, from the Royal Conservatory in Paris.35 Bachelot translated a prayer book into Hawaiian (O Ke A'o Ana Kristiano, "Christian Doctrine", c. 1831),36 authored a catechism in Hawaiian (He <U+014C>lelo Ho'ona'auao, "A Word of Instruction", 1831),37 and wrote an introduction to Hawaiian grammar in French (Notes Gramaticales, "Grammatical Notes", 1834).38

Persecution

By 1827, Protestant Christianity, and in particular Bingham's teachings,39 had become the de facto state religion of the Kingdom of Hawaii.40 Queen Ka<U+02BB>ahumanu persecuted Catholics from 1829 until her death in 1832, by, for example, forbidding Hawaiians from attending masses and instructing Bachelot not to proselytize.41 In December 1831, Bachelot and Short were deported and forced to leave on a ship, the Waverly, bound for North America.42 Though Queen Ka<U+02BB>ahumanu steadfastly opposed his work, Bachelot viewed her as a good person who had been deceived by Protestant missionaries.43

The news of Bachelot and Short's expulsion caused controversy in the United States, where it was viewed as a violation of the rights appertaining to foreigners in the 1826 treaty signed by Commodore Thomas ap Catesby Jones of the United States Navy and King . U.S. Navy Commodore John Downes protested the expulsions while in discussion with chiefs during his 1832 visit to the kingdom.44

California

The Waverly landed at a vacant area near San Pedro, Los Angeles, in January 1832.45 Bachelot and Short traveled to the Mission San Gabriel Arc<U+00E1>ngel,46 where they were welcomed by the Franciscans who staffed the mission.47 Bachelot later became the pastor of a church in Los Angeles,48 served as an assistant minister for the mission,49 and led the mission on an interim basis after its priest was reassigned in 1834.50 He also taught in Los Angeles schools during a teacher shortage.51 He ministered in California until 183752 and became popular with Angelenos.53

In 1833, the Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith reorganized the jurisdiction of Oceania. Hawaii became part of the newly created Apostolic Vicariate of Eastern Oceania, which was split into northern and southern divisions. Bachelot remained as the Prefect Apostolic for the northern division. Bishop Etienne Jerome Rouchouze served as the Vicar Apostolic of Eastern Oceania and oversaw Bachelot's assignment in Hawaii.54

In 1835 and 1836, two representatives of the Catholic Church traveled to Hawaii in an attempt to ascertain whether Bachelot could return.55 Queen Ka<U+02BB>ahumanu had died in 1832, and the following year King began making radical changes to Hawaiian law.56 Columban Murphy, a Catholic lay brother from the United Kingdom, visited King in 1835 and discussed the possibility of Bachelot's return.57 Finding King amenable to the idea, Murphy traveled to California to relay the news. He was unable, however, to locate Bachelot, who was absent from the area at the time. After Bachelot received Murphy's message, Bachelot and Short decided to return to Hawaii.58 The Ayuntamiento in Los Angeles, a municipal council, attempted to dissuade Bachelot59 and asked the Catholic leadership in Santa Barbara to prevent him from leaving,60 but he insisted on departing61 and the Catholic leadership did not prevent him.62

Final years

When Bachelot and Short arrived in Honolulu in May 1837, they spent only 13 days on the island. Notwithstanding the agreement he had signed with French Naval Captain Abel Aubert Dupetit Thouars that allowed French citizens to live on the island,63 King sought to deport the priests. Bachelot and Short were confined to the ship on which they had arrived, the Clementine,64 on .65 However, the Clementine's captain, Jules Dudoit, refused to transport them from Hawaii.66 Dudoit, a British citizen of French descent, met with Charlton and they publicly protested the priests' confinement.67 Their efforts to secure freedom for the priests to live on the island were unsuccessful68 until the British naval vessel HMS Sulphur and the French frigate the La V<U+00E8>nus arrived in Honolulu69 on .70 The ships were commanded respectively by Edward Belcher and Dupetit Thouars, who each tried to convince the authorities to allow the priests to return to the island. After negotiations proved futile, they blockaded the harbor, boarded the Clementine, and brought Bachelot and Short ashore. The La V<U+00E8>nus sent 300 sailors to escort them from the harbor to the French mission. King agreed to allow the priests to stay in Honolulu until they could find a ship to transport them elsewhere, under the condition that they refrained from proselytizing.71

That year, Bachelot, who suffered from a form of rheumatism,72 became very sick.73 By November 1837, he had recovered sufficiently to leave Hawaii. He purchased a ship74 and sailed toward Micronesia, intending to work on a mission.75 Bachelot's health significantly worsened after leaving Hawaii76 and he died at sea on December 5, 1887.77 He was buried on an islet off the coast of Pohnpei. In 1838, a small chapel was built near his grave.78

Owing to the persecution of Bachelot and his fellow priests, the government of France sent the frigate Art<U+00E9>mise to Hawaii in 1839. Its captain, Cyrille Pierre Th<U+00E9>odore Laplace, had been instructed to force the government to stop persecuting Catholics. In response to this show of force, King granted Catholics freedom of religion.79

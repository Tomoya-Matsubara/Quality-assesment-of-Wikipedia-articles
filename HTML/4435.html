Ryszard Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski (; March 4, 1932 <U+2013> January 23, 2007) was a Polish reporter, journalist, traveller, photographer, poet and writer whose dispatches in book form brought him a global reputation. He was born in Pi<U+0144>sknow in Belarusin the Kresy Wschodnie or eastern borderlands of the second Polish Republic, into poverty: he would say later that he felt at home in Africa as "food was scarce there too and everyone was also barefoot."1 He is one of the top Polish writers most frequently translated into foreign languages.2

Biography

Ryszard Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was born in Pinsk, Polesie Voivodeship in 1932 as a son of Maria Bobka (b. 1910) and J<U+00F3>zef Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski (b. 1903), primary school teachers. Next year his sister Barbara was born. In September 1938 Ryszard started attending Primary School No 5 in Pinsk. Summer of 1939 he spent together with his mother and sister in village Paw<U+0142><U+00F3>w near Rejowiec in Lublin Voivodeship. When the Second World War began in September 1939 they came back to Pinsk after the city was captured by the Red Army and Ryszard returned to school there. In 1940 Maria, afraid of deprtation to the East, together with Ryszard and Barbara left Pinsk to village Sierak<U+00F3>w near Warsaw. There they met J<U+00F3>zef. Later the family moved near Otwock. Ryszard continued education in primary school in Otwock (1944<U+2013>45).3 He described his early life in the book Imperium.

In 1945 the family settled in Warsaw where Ryszard began education in Stanis<U+0142>aw Staszic Gymnasium. He became an amateur boxer (bantamweight) and footlball player.4 In 1948, Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski joined the official Communist youth organisationthe ZMPand served lower rank posts.5 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was the hero of the article published in the weekly periodical Odrodzenie reporting on a poetry conference organised at his school, in which the teenager's poems were compared with works of Mayakovsky and Wierzy<U+0144>ski.6

In June 1950 he graduated from Gymnasium and started working for the Sztandar M<U+0142>odych (The Banner of Youth), a nationwide newspaper founded in 1950 as the organ of the ZMP. In October 1950 he bagan his studies at Warsaw University (Department of Polish Studies) and in 1951 he moved to the Department of History after he suspended working for Sztandar M<U+0142>odych till 1955. He participated in the Youth Festival in East Berlin staged in August 1951 in East Germany. This was his first foreign trip. From 1952 and till his death Ryszard Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was married to doctor Alicja Mielczarek. Their daughter Zofia was born in 1953. During the period from 1953 to 1981the year of the imposition of the martial law in PolandKapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was a member of the Polish United Workers' Party (the PZPR). His attitude to the PZPR changed early on, "the decisive moment having come in the year 1956" (presumably a reference to the events of Pozna<U+0144> June and the process of de-Stalinisation brought about by the Thaw of Gomu<U+0142>ka, and the Hungarian Revolution of 1956).78

In June 1955 he graduated from Warsaw University. After publishing, in September 1955, a critical article about the construction of Nowa Huta, a Cracow conurbation built on a site chosen as the "first socialist municipality in Poland",9 which brought to light the inhuman working and living conditions of the labourers involved in the venture<U+2014>a story which occasioned consternation before eventually winning favour with the Communist authorities unsure at first how to react to a fault-finding depiction of their pet project by one of their own<U+2014>Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was awarded the Golden Cross of Merit at the age of 23.

In August 1956 he reported from Kiev and in September he was sent to India, his first travel outside Europe. He returned via Afganistan (where he was detained at the airport in Kabul) and Moscow. In August 1957 he went for half a year to China (via Tokyo and Hong Kong). He came back to Poland by the Trans-Siberian Railway. Beginning with that journey to India undertaken at the age of 24, he travelled across the developing world reporting on wars, coups and revolutions in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. He started learning English in India by reading, with the help of a dictionary, a copy of Hemingway's For Whom the Bell Tolls.10 He wrote about his first travels to Asia in the book Travels with Herodotus.

In 1958 he left Sztandar M<U+0142>odych and started working for the Polish Press Agency and soon after also joined the weekly Polityka (where he worked till 1962). The result of his work for the weekly was the book Busz po polsku (The Polish Bush) published in 1962, a collection of his articles from the "Polish wilderness" that he went into to relate "the perspectives of forgotten, invisible, marginal people and so to record a living history of those seldom deemed worthy to enter the annals of official history" (in the words of Diana Kuprel, the literary scholar and translator of Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's works).11 He was aggrieved at the indifference of the reading public towards the majority of his early books.12

In the late 1950s he went for the first time to Africa (Ghana, Republic of Dahomey and Niger). After honing his skills on domestic stories he was later "'responsible' for fifty countries" for the Polish Press Agency in Africa.1314 (Although a correspondent of an official state press agency, he never in his life asked a single question at any press conference that he attended15). When he finally returned to Poland, he had lived through twenty-seven revolutions and coups, been jailed 40 times and survived four death sentences.16 In the English-speaking world, Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski is best known for his reporting from Africa in the 1960s and 1970s, when he witnessed first-hand the end of the European colonial empires on that continent.

In 1961 he reported from the Republic of the Congo. He described his escape to Bujumbura and subsequent arrest in the book The Soccer War. In the years 1962-65 he lived at the beginning in Dar es Salaam and later in Nairobi from where he travelled to other countries in Africa. He came back to Poland only for few weeks in 1965 but returned to Africa to live in Lagos and continue reporting. In April 1965 he travelled to Senegal and Mauritania what he later described in the book The Shadow of the Sun. In the end of 1966 he came back to Poland. In April 1967 he went to Central Asia and Caucasus. In November the same year he started working as a foreign correspondent in South America, based in Santiago. Later he moved to Mexico (1969<U+2013>72). In 1969 he witnessed war in Honduras which he described in the book The Soccer War. In 1969 he edited and translated from the Spanish El diario del Che en Bolivia, the final literary bequest of Che Guevara.17 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski analyzed the situation in Guatemala after a German diplomat Karl von Spreti was kidnapped. He published his reportage in 1970 entitled Dlaczego zgin<U+0105><U+0142> Karl von Spreti (Why Karl von Spreti Died). He came back to Poland in 1972 and later worked for magazines Kontynenty and Kultura. In September 1975 he went to Angola after which he published the book Another Day of Life. In 1975 and 1977 he went to Ethiopia. The Emperor was written after his travels there.18 In 1979 he visited his birthplace Pinsk for the first time since 1940. In 1979 he went to Iran to witness the Iranian Revolution. His book Shah of Shahs deals with this subject and the fall of Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, the last Shah of Iran.

In 1980 he witnessed the strikes that took place in Gda<U+0144>sk, Poland.19 He travelled in European and Asian parts of the Soviet Union (1989-1992) and witnessed the Dissolution of the Soviet Union. After this experience he wrote Imperium. He was awarded German Academic Exchange Service scholarship in Berlin in 1994.

In a 2006 interview with Reuters, Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski said that he wrote for "people everywhere still young enough to be curious about the world."20 He was fluent in English, Russian, Spanish, French and Portuguese. He was visiting professor in Bangalore (1970s), Bonn, Cape Town, Caracas (1979), Columbia University (1983), Harvard University, Irkutsk, London, Madrid, Mexico (1979), San Sebastian, Temple University (1988) and Vancouver.21

Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski died on January 23, 2007 of a heart attack suffered in a Warsaw hospital where he was being treated for unrelated ailments.22

Literary works

From the early 1960s onwards, Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski published books of increasing literary craftsmanship characterized by sophisticated narrative technique, psychological portraits of characters, a wealth of stylization and metaphor and unusual imagery that serves as means of interpreting the perceived world. Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's best-known book, The Emperor, concerns itself with the decline of Haile Selassie's anachronistic r<U+00E9>gime in Ethiopia. The book<U+2019>s story had a special meaning that was not lost on the people of Poland, especially as dissent against the PZPR was taking root. The Emperor was also the book that established Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski<U+2019>s reputation in the West. When it appeared in English translation in 1983 it received an immediate critical success.23

Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was fascinated by the humanity he found in different worlds and people, as well as the books of these worlds and people: he approached foreign countries first through literature, spending months reading before each trip. He was skilled in listening to the diverse people he met, but he was also capable of "reading" the hidden sense of the scenes he encountered: the way the Europeans moved out of Angola, a discussion regarding alimony in the Tanganyikan parliament, the reconstruction of frescoes in the new Russiahe turned each of these vignettes into a metaphor of historical transformation.

This tendency to process private experiences into a greater social synthesis made Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski an eminent thinker, and the volumes of the ongoing Lapidarium series are a record of the shaping of a reporter's observations into philosophical reflections on the world, its people and their suffering. He had great compassion for the poor, the victimised, and the debased.

Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski himself called his work "literary reportage",24 and reportage d'auteur.25 In the English-speaking world, his genre is sometimes characterised as "magic journalism" (in counterpoint to magic realism), a term coined for him by Adam Hochschild in 1994.26272829 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski often introduced himself with the line "I am a poor reporter who unfortunately lacks the imagination of a writer".30

Italian journalist Tiziano Terzani and Ryszard Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski shared a similar vision of journalism.31 Jaime Abello Banfi, the friend and associate of Gabriel Garc<U+00ED>a M<U+00E1>rquez, reports that Garc<U+00ED>a M<U+00E1>rquez and Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski, unbeknownst to each other, shared the opinion that the way to good journalism led through poetry (on account of the fact that it inculcates both the conciseness of expression and its aptness).32

Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski considered an ancient Greek historian Herodotus a great reporter and his master. He wrote a book Travels with Herodotus where he shows that The Histories of Herodotus are timeless and the masterpiece of reportage. He also considered Curzio Malaparte, Melchior Wa<U+0144>kowicz, Ksawery Pruszy<U+0144>ski and Franciszek Gil (1917<U+2013>1960) to have been his literary models and stylistic precursors.33343536 On some level, Pruszy<U+0144>ski and Wa<U+0144>kowicz shared a very similar approach to facts with Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski, believing that the general picture of the story can be glued from bits and pieces to reveal a truth as a wholly independent construct.37 Students of Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's work observed correspondences between his work and that of J. M. Coetzee in that both writers were supposedly beholden to the theory of "the responsibility of witness".38

One reviewer saw in Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's mixing of subtle psychological reflection with vivid description an invitation to a comparison with Joseph Conrad;39 Binyavanga Wainaina and Aleksandar Hemon made the same comparison, if for other, less laudatory reasons.4041 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski confirmed to Bill Deedes the fact that Conrad was one of his literary inspirations.42 Neal Ascherson likened him to Egon Erwin Kisch (1885<U+2013>1948) considered the father of literary reportage.43 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski himself cites Kisch with approval as the "classic of reportage" who dealt a death blow to traditional forms of reporting by putting the person of the reporter at centre stage.44 Certainly, neither Kisch nor Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski believed in what might be called "journalistic objectivity": whereas Kisch thought it necessary for a (Communist) reporter to "engage politically" with his subject, Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski would put objectivity as a concept out of court altogether, stating explicitly, "There is no such thing as objectivity. Objectivity is the question of the conscience of the one who writes. And he himself should answer the question is this what he writes close to the truth or not".45

Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's views on his craft were published in 2000 in the book in Italian Il cinico non <U+00E8> adatto a questo mestiere: conversazioni sul buon giornalismo (A Cynic wouldn't Suit This Profession: Conversations about Good Journalism),46 the book in Spanish from 2003 (distributed for free) Los cinco sentidos del periodista (estar, ver, o<U+00ED>r, compartir, pensar) (The Journalist<U+2019>s Five Senses: Witnessing, Seeing, Listening, Sharing and Thinking)47 and in his Polish book Autoportret reportera (A Reporter's Self Portrait)48 published the same year. In 1987 Marek Miller talked with Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski on the art of reportage and his life. These conversations were published in Poland in 2012 in the book Pisanie ("Writing")49 but broadcast in Canada on Kalejdoskop Polski TV as early as 1988.

Photographer

Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski debuted as a photographer in the year 2000 with the publication of the album entitled Z Afryki ("Out of Africa"), a photographic harvest of his journeys in that continent. "Every snapshot is a recollection, a remembrance," he writes in the introduction, "and nothing can sensitise us more to the fragility of time, to its impermanent and fleeting naturethan photography."50 A sequel, entitled Ze <U+015B>wiata ("From the World", published in November 2008 with the introduction of John Updike), comprising a cross-section of Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's photographs from all parts of the world, contains some truly outstanding shots.51

Posthumous and non-reportage works

In Ten Inny ("The Other"), a collection of lectures delivered in Vienna, Graz and Cracow, published shortly before his death, Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski laments a state of affairs perpetuated by the myths which inculcate the notion of the Other as sub-human or non-human. He saw encountering the Other as the main challenge for the twenty-first century. The posthumously published Ho dato voce ai poveri: dialogo con i giovani ("I Gave a Voice to the Poor: Conversations with the Youth"; Trent, Il Margine, 2007; subsequently published in Poland as Da<U+0142>em g<U+0142>os ubogim. Rozmowy z m<U+0142>odzie<U+017C><U+0105>; Cracow, Znak, 2008) is a record of Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's interactions with the students of the University of Bolzano in Italy in October 2006;52 while Rw<U+0105>cy nurt historii. Zapiski o XX i XXI wieku ("In the Whirlpools of History: Jottings on the 20th and the 21st Centuries"; Cracow, Znak, 2007) is a compilation of interviews and lectures, reflecting Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's training as a historian and dealing with contemporary issues and their historical and cross-cultural parallels (including such issues as globalisation, Islam, the birth of the Third World, and the dawn of the Pacific civilisation).53 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's pronouncements on current affairs were noteworthy: he thought that the causes of the 9/11 tragedy, for example, were too complex to lend themselves to an exhaustively thorough analysis at present, although he offered an extensive and sophisticated exposition of some of the key elements of the puzzle in the Clash of Civilisations. He was critical on the Clash of Civilisations theory which he saw as an American vision of the world.54 He told a BBC interviewer right after the attacks: "I greatly fear that we will waste this moment. That instead of meaningful dialogue, it will just be gates and metal detectors".55

In an interview granted in 2002 to the then editor-in-chief of the monthly Letras Libres, Ricardo Cayuela Gally, Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski opined that the war on terror, owing to the asymmetrical character of the combatants engaged in it, could only be wonand indeed easily, within a monththrough a (re)introduction of "Stalinism", a method undesirable for the sole reason that it would leave the world under the permanent "hegemony" of the United States, a circumstance that would spell the end of "the free society".56

In Poland, since 1986 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was also known as a poet:57 he privately confided in his Swedish translator, Anders Bodeg<U+00E5>rd, that he considered this to be his primary identity.58 In November 2007 the Canadian publishing house Biblioasis published Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's selected poems in English, I Wrote Stone, the first English translation of his poetry.

Although he was not the sole model for the role, Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was given an unflattering portrayal as the main character in Andrzej Wajda's 1978 film Without Anesthesia.5960 Aleksandar Hemon, the Bosnian-American novelist (who had previously impugned Robert D. Kaplan's stereotyping of "the Balkan mind"), in a critique of Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's Africa writings published in The Village Voice, accused Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's readers of turning a blind eye to "the underlying proto-racist essentialism" that informs his vision of and his approach to the cultures of the continent: "[Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski] fumes against the racism absurdly based on skin colour, and would probably be shocked if told that his obsessive listing of essential differences [between "the African mind" and "the European mind"] is essentially racist".616263

The German journalist Claus Christian Malzahn described him, in an obituary published in Der Spiegel, as "one of the most credible journalists the world has ever seen".64 Daniel Alarc<U+00F3>n, the Peruvian-American novelist cited Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski as a formative influence together with Dostoyevsky.65 The American journalist and reportage-writer Richard Bernstein, saw value in the "penetrating intelligence" of Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's vision and in his "crystallised descriptive" style of writing.66 The British journalist Bill Deedes, who had witnessed the Rwandan Genocide first-hand, said of Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski that what he "writes about Africa is authoritative as well as attractive. His account of how the Hutus and the Tutsis were drawn into that dark night of genocide in Rwanda is the most enlightening I have read anywhere"even while, at the same time, proclaiming that it was Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski who had "transformed journalism into literature in his writings about Africa".67 Professor Philip Melling of Swansea University concurred with this opinion, citing Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski as an authority on the Rwandan conflict.68

Honours and awards

Salman Rushdie wrote about him: "One Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski is worth more than a thousand whimpering and fantasizing scribblers. His exceptional combination of journalism and art allows us to feel so close to what Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski calls the inexpressible true image of war".69

Frequently mentioned as a favorite to win the Nobel Prize in literature, he never did. Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's dying before he could be awarded the Prize was bemoaned in the Swedish press as late as October 2010.70 Since his death he has been offered many epitaphs in the press, such as, "The master of modern journalism",71 "Translator of the World" and "The Greatest Reporter in the World",72 "Herodotus of our times",73 "Third World chronicler".7475

Literary prizes and honours

Over the years, particularly since 1983 when The Emperor was named Book of the Year by The Sunday Times of London, Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was the laureate of many international literary prizes that brought recognition to his creative oeuvre: these included, for example, the biennial Hanseatic Goethe Prize awarded by the Hamburg-based foundation, the Alfred Toepfer Stiftung, which he received in 1999; or the Italian Elsa Morante Prize (Premio Elsa Morante, Sezione Culture D'Europa) in 2005, for his Travels with Herodotus (the new category of the Premio Elsa Morante, called "Cultures of Europe", in effect a separate prize awarded by the same jury, having apparently been created specially for him).76

In 2001 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski received the literary Prix Tropiques of the French Development Agency for his book The Shadow of the Sun, published in France under the title <U+00C9>b<U+00E8>ne: Aventures africaines, which had a year earlier been named the best book of the year by the French literary monthly, Lire; the book also won the Italian literary award, Feudo Di Maida Prize (in full, Premio Letterario Internazionale Feudo Di Maida), for the year 2000.77 That same year (2000) Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was honoured with the prestigious Premio Internazionale Viareggio-Versilia,78 as well as having received the Creola Prize (Premio Creola) in Bologna (awarded for travel books and facilitation of intercultural encounters),79 and the "Premio Letterario 'Della Resistenza'{{-"}} of the Piedmontese city of Omegna (Premio Omegna).80

In 2003 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski received the Premio Grinzane Cavour per la Lettura in Turin;81 shared the Prince of Asturias Award (in the category "Communications and Humanities") with the Peruvian theologian Gustavo Guti<U+00E9>rrez; and was awarded the Kreisky Prize (Bruno-Kreisky-Preis f<U+00FC>r das politische Buch) for the entirety of his work ("Sonderpreis f<U+00FC>r das publizistische Gesamtwerk"; the award ceremony having taken place in Vienna in May of the following year).82 As the doyen of literary reportage, he was the keynote speaker at the inaugural ceremony, held in Berlin in October 2003, for the Lettre Ulysses Awards for the Art of Reportage.83

He was celebrated by other practitioners of the genre, the acclaimed Italian reportage-writer, Tiziano Terzani, Colombian writer Gabriel Garc<U+00ED>a M<U+00E1>rquez and Chilean writer Luis Sep<U+00FA>lveda having accorded him the title "Maestro".848586

In 2005 the Italian edition of Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's poems (which appeared in print the previous year as Taccuino d'appunti in the translation of Silvano De Fanti) won the state-funded Naples Prize (Premio Napoli).87 To complete the round-up of Italian prizes, the next year Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was awarded a special category of the Ilaria Alpi Prize for the entirety of his career (Premio Ilaria Alpi alla carriera), one of the best-known of Italian journalistic awards, named for an Italian investigative reporter murdered in Somalia in 1994 (although the scope of the prize is limited to TV journalism, special categories of prizes for which he would not otherwise qualifyas also for example in the case of the Elsa Morante Prizehave been created for Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski).88 In June 2005 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was invested with an honorary doctorate by the private Ramon Llull University of Barcelona, Spain;89 and in May 2006, just eight months before his death, he received a similar degree from the University of Udine in Italy.90

In 2010, the Council of the Capital City of Warsaw established the Ryszard Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski Award for literary reportage as a form of distinction and promotion of the most worthwhile reportage books which touch on important contemporary issues, evoke reflection, and deepen our knowledge of the world of other cultures.91

Biographies

In 2010, a book about Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski was published in Poland: Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski Non-Fiction, written by Artur Domos<U+0142>awski (b. 1967).92 Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's widow, Alicja Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ska (b. 1933), sought an injunction against this publication, claiming defamation and invasion of privacy.93 In 2013 the publisher agreed to apologise to Alicja Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ska and withdraw the book from circulation.

Polish scholars Beata Nowacka and Zygmunt Zi<U+0105>tek wrote the first Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski's artistic biography Ryszard Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski. Biografia pisarza, published in Poland by Znak in 2008. The book was later translated into Spanish (2010) and Italian (2012).94 They responded to Domos<U+0142>awski's accusations with a book Literatura <U+201E>non-fiction<U+201D>. Czytanie Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>skiego po Domos<U+0142>awskim (Non<U+2011>fiction literature: Reading Kapu<U+015B>ci<U+0144>ski after Domos<U+0142>awski) which was published in Polish by the University of Silesia Press in 2013.95 They oppose the accusation of creating a myth, and his own legend, as well as confabulations and opportunism, showing a selected and tendentious usage of the author<U+2019>s life knowledge, the lack of comprehension of a literary reportage, manipulation with texts and quotations, as well as numerous factual and technical mistakes made by Domos<U+0142>awski.

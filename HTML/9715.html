Thurman Lowell Tucker (September 26, 1917 May 7, 1993) was an American center fielder in Major League Baseball who played for nine seasons in the American League with the Chicago White Sox and Cleveland Indians. In 701 career games, Tucker posted a batting average of .255 and had 24 triples, 9 home runs, and 179 runs batted in (RBI). He was nicknamed "Joe E." Tucker because of his resemblance to comedian Joe E. Brown.1

Born and raised in Texas, Tucker first played professionally with the Siloam Springs Travelers. He gradually moved up the levels of minor league baseball and was signed by the Chicago White Sox before the 1941 season. He made his major league debut a year later. Tucker spent two seasons as the starting center fielder for the White Sox before enlisting in the armed forces for World War II. He played two more seasons for the White Sox after returning, then he was traded to the Cleveland Indians. Tucker played four seasons for the Indians, and continued to play minor league baseball throughout the 1950s. After retirement, he worked as a major league scout and insurance agent until his death in 1993.

Early life

Thurman Tucker was born and raised in Gordon, Texas. While attending high school, he was a three-sport athlete, playing baseball (where he was a second baseman), basketball, and track and field. After graduating from high school in 1935, he played some semi-professional baseball, and later enrolled in a baseball school located in Hot Springs, Arkansas. Tucker began his professional career at the age of 18 in 1936 when he signed with the Fayetteville Bears of the Arkansas-Missouri League. He lasted two weeks with the team, not playing in a game for them. However, he was signed soon after by the Siloam Springs Travelers in the same league, where he began his professional career.2

Minor league career

Tucker spent his first season with the Travelers, where he changed positions and became an outfielder.3 He played in 117 games for the Travelers, finishing the season with a .319 batting average and 25 doubles.4 In 1937, he was to play for the El Dorado Lions of the Cotton States League, but a back injury caused him to miss nearly the entire season.5 Tucker spent the 1938 season with two separate minor league clubs. He played 55 games for the Abbeville A's of the Evangeline Baseball League and 50 games for the Greenville Bucks of the Cotton States League.6 In 1939, he remained in the Cotton States League and played for the Clarksdale Red Sox, who were a minor league affiliate of the Boston Red Sox at the time. In 136 games for Clarksdale, Tucker had a .298 batting average and 10 triples.7

Tucker had a breakthrough minor league season in 1940 when he continued to play for Clarksdale. By the end of June, he was leading the Cotton State League with a batting average of .374.8 After playing in 97 games, finishing with an average of .390, Tucker played in 40 games for the Oklahoma City Indians.9 After the season ended, Chicago White Sox farm manager Billy Webb was impressed enough with Tucker's play that he purchased Tucker's contract from Oklahoma City.10 The 1941 season began with Tucker competing with Dave Short for the final outfield spot on the White Sox roster.11 Tucker lost the battle and spent the 1941 season at Oklahoma City, where he was coached by Hall of Famer Rogers Hornsby.12 In 141 games for the Indians, Tucker had a batting average of .246 and 12 triples.13

As spring training for the 1942 season began, Tucker found himself competing with Dave Philley for the final outfield spot.14 He was on the major league roster when opening day began, and made his major league debut on April 14, 1942.15 He only played in two games, however, before being sent to the Fort Worth Cats of the Texas League.16 He spent most of the season with the Cats, hitting .313 in 144 games.17 He was brought back up to the major league squad late in the season, and finished the year with seven games played for the White Sox.18

Chicago White Sox

The Chicago White Sox had several question marks in the outfield to begin the 1943 season, with only Wally Moses being assured of a job. After spring training began, manager Jimmy Dykes liked what he saw out of Tucker while competing for the starting center fielder position alongside Moose Solters.19 As a result, after spring training ended Tucker not only became the starting center fielder during the 1943 season, but was the team's leadoff hitter as well.20 Partway through the season, Tucker's performance caught the eye of American League President Will Harridge, who noted him as someone the public was coming out to watch with the absence of stars who were serving in World War II.21 His performance during the season included a walk-off home run to win a game against the New York Yankees on July 26, 2â€“1.22 Tucker finished the season with a .235 batting average, six triples, and 79 walks in 135 games. He also stole 29 bases, the third best total in the AL, and was caught stealing 17 times, which was second in the league.23

Tucker passed a physical examination for the United States Navy before the 1944 season began, and was projected to be called up to serve in World War II that year.24 He was not called in, and ended up playing the entire season for the White Sox. Tucker hit very well during the first month of the season; he had a .403 batting average on May 16, which easily led the American League.25 His hitting and fielding abilities, combined with the possibility of joining the Navy at any time, led to sportswriter Fred Lieb noting him as a breakout performer that year.26 Tucker and Dixie Walker continued to lead their respective leagues in batting average throughout June; at month's end Tucker had an average of .369 in the American League while Walker had an average of .377 in the National League.27 Because of his efforts, Tucker was named to the All-Star roster for the only time in his career.28 He was the leadoff hitter in the 1944 Major League Baseball All-Star Game and went hitless in four at-bats.29

Tucker's hitting began to slow down after being named to the All-Star Game. In early July, he had a hitless streak of 28 at-bats, causing his batting average to shrink from .375 to .327, losing his status as league leader in batting average in the process.30 After his batting average fell to .320 after hitting once in 35 at-bats, he was benched for a weekend matchup against the Detroit Tigers in an attempt to stop his sudden cold streak.31 At the end of July, both Tucker and George Case participated in a 75â€“yard dash at part of the White Sox's annual benefit for the war; Tucker lost the race to Case by a yard.32 Tucker finished the season with a batting average of .287 and six triples.33 After the season ended, Tucker formally joined the Navy, and he spent the 1945 season serving in World War II.34

Upon everyone returning to their teams at the end of the war, Tucker was slated to be the starting center fielder as the 1946 season began, working alongside Wally Moses and Taffy Wright.35 Unlike the previous season, he originally struggled with the bat, and was relegated to his original status as a good fielder but a poor hitter.36 At the end of June, Tucker had a batting average of .229, nearly 150 points lower than his average at the same point in 1944.37 He rebounded in the second half of the season though, finishing the year with a batting average of .288 and 20 doubles, both career highs.38 As the 1947 Chicago White Sox season began, Tucker remained in his center field position after hitting .400 in the last month of the 1946 season, while rookie Dave Philley was in left field and Taffy Wright and Bob Kennedy platooned in right field.39 He started off the year, however, by missing some playing time due to a stomach ailment, and did not start playing for the White Sox full time until the middle of May.40 Tucker ended up splitting time with Philley due to his light hitting, and he finished the season with a .236 batting average in 89 games.41

Cleveland Indians

On January 27, 1948, Tucker was traded to the Cleveland Indians for Ralph Weigel; the Indians made the move because they regarded Tucker as "the finest defensive player in baseball."42 He started off the year as the starting center fielder for the Indians, and in one early matchup against the Detroit Tigers, he was the only player not to record a hit in an 8â€“2 victory.43 He missed a couple weeks of playing time in June due to being hit by a pitch, resulting in a broken finger.44 After returning to the lineup, Tucker spent most of the second half of the season as a fourth outfielder, splitting time with Allie Clark and playing int he outfield alongside Larry Doby and Dale Mitchell.45 He finished the season with a .260 batting average and 52 runs in 83 games, and also ended the season with a perfect fielding percentage of 1.000.46 Tucker also participated in game six of the 1948 World Series, scoring a run in the sixth inning on a walk en route to a 4â€“3 win for the Indians.47

The Indians planned to use Tucker as their fourth outfielder for the 1949 season; he served mostly as the backup to Doby for that season.48 He was also relegated mostly to pinch hitting duties, and he finished the season with a .244 batting average in 20 games and under 200 at-bats.49 Due to his hitting struggles, Indians manager Lou Boudreau tried converting Tucker to a switch hitter to start off the 1950 season.50 Tucker was again set to be a backup outfielder in 1950. His performances on the year included hitting a home run, the only one he hit that season, in an 8â€“5 victory over the Brooklyn Dodgers; the teams raised $60,000 in that game to benefit sandlot teams in Cleveland.51 Tucker finished the season with a .178 batting average in 54 games, the lowest mark of his career.52

Tucker and Allie Clark were both looking to crack the Indians roster to begin the 1951 season, as the additions of Harry Simpson and Minnie Mi<U+00F1>oso made it likely that one or both of them would be traded or released.53 Tucker ended up playing only one game for the Indians on April 29, recording a strikeout in his lone at-bat.54 In early May, the Indians sent him to their Triple-A minor league affiliate, the San Diego Padres of the Pacific Coast League, a move that ended his major league career.55

Later life

After being sent to the Padres, Tucker completed the 1951 season with them. In 88 games, Tucker had two triples and a .222 batting average.56 In the offseason, Tucker began to operate a taxicab in Texas, and he contemplated retirement from baseball during a contract in February 1952.57 He did end up playing 47 games for the now-unaffiliated Padres, hitting .225 in the process.58 In mid-June, the Padres sold his contract to the Oklahoma City Indians of the Texas League.59 Tucker played in 72 games for the Indians that season, hitting .263.60 He retired from baseball during the 1953 season, and did not play with any professional team during that time.

Tucker returned to baseball in 1954 to play for the Lubbock Hubbers of the West Texas-New Mexico League. He played part-time for the team, serving as a replacement when players needed time off, whether it was for an injury or family matter.61 He hit .360 in 25 games for the Hubbers.62 The following year, he served as player-manager for the Carlsbad Potashers of the Longhorn League. He noted during his time there that the Hobbs Sports were the top team in heckling.63 Tucker hit .275 in 114 games for the Potashers, hitting 25 doubles and eight home runs as well.64 He was the player-manager for the Potashers in 1956 as well, but management considered firing him from that position during a 14-game losing streak.65 He finished the year with a .306 batting average in 128 games. The following season, he was the player-manager of the Hobbs Sports, but only played in 16 games for them, hitting .273.66 In 1958, he ended his playing career, and became the general manager of the Hobbs team.67

After retiring, Tucker became an insurance agent and lived in Oklahoma City with his wife and four children.68 In 1962, he also became one of the Houston Astros' first scouts.69 Tucker died on May 7, 1993 in Oklahoma City and is buried at Gordon Cemetery in his hometown of Gordon, Texas.70

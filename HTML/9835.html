Severe Tropical Cyclone Glenda (JTWC designation: 20S, also known as simply Cyclone Glenda) was among the strongest tropical cyclones to make landfall in Western Australia, though it moved ashore in a lightly populated region. It began as a tropical low on March 15 in the Gulf of Carpentaria. The precursor disturbance drifted over Top End and later across the northeastern portion of Western Australia, and after emerging into the Indian Ocean it strengthened into a tropical storm. Aided by favourable environmental conditions, Glenda rapidly intensified to reach Category 5 status on the Australian cyclone scale, and with a peak intensity of 910 mbar it was among the strongest cyclones on record within the Australia region. On March 30 it moved ashore near Onslow as a Category 3 cyclone, and the next day it degenerated into a remnant tropical low over land.

The precursor disturbance produced heavy rainfall in the Kimberley region of Western Australia, causing record flooding and some road damage. Minor damage was reported at the final landfall of Glenda. Due to the sparse population and preparations made, the cyclone was not responsible for any deaths or injuries. However, its name was later retired from the list of tropical cyclone names.

Storm history

On March 15, a tropical disturbance developed in the southwestern Gulf of Carpentaria. It moved westward, drifting across Top End, and it exited into the Joseph Bonaparte Gulf on March 22.1 The Bureau of Meteorology (BoM) office in Darwin, which is the local Tropical Cyclone Warning Centre, began issuing advisories on the system late on March 23 while it was located about 85 km (50 mi) east-southeast of Wyndham, Western Australia. Environmental conditions favored intensification as an anticyclone developed over the storm, which provided good outflow and low vertical wind shear. Initially, the primary inhibiting factor was land interaction.2 After executing a small loop over water, the disturbance continued westward, crossing over the northern portion of Western Australia before emerging into the Indian Ocean on March 26.3 It began tracking west-southwestward just offshore of the Kimberley coastline, and its convection quickly concentrated. At 0000 UTC on March 27, the Joint Typhoon Warning Center (JTWC) classified it as Tropical Cyclone 20S. Three hours later, the BoM office in Perth upgraded the storm to Tropical Cyclone Glenda about 260 km (160 mi) north of Derby, Western Australia.4

Upon reaching open waters, Glenda quickly intensified, and midday on March 27 the BoM upgraded it to tropical cyclone status, or the equivalence of a minimal hurricane.5 Shortly thereafter, the JTWC followed suit by upgrading it to cyclone status just 12 hours after first warning on the storm.6 A wind gust of 113 km/h (70 mph) was reported on Adele Island as the cyclone passed nearby.7 The track turned southwestward around a steering ridge over Australia, aided by a mid-latitude trough. By March 27, Glenda had developed a banding eye, and subsequently began rapid deepening, with warm water temperatures of over 30<U+00BA> C (86<U+00BA> F) and a very favorable upper-level environment. At 1200 UTC on March 28, the JTWC classified Glenda with peak winds of 260 km/h (160 mph) about 235 km (145 mi) west-northwest of Broome, or about 455 km (280 mi) north-northeast of Port Hedland.8 At the same time the BoM estimated the cyclone attained peak winds of 215 km/h (135 mph) with gusts to 300 km/h (185 mph), or a Category 5 on the Australian cyclone scale.9 Its peak intensity of 910 mbar was tied for the fifth most intense tropical cyclone on record in the Australian basin.10

Initially, Cyclone Glenda was forecasted to intensify further. However, a gradual increase in vertical shear caused the eye to become disorganized,11 with land interaction contributing to further weakening. The BoM maintained Glenda as a Category 5 cyclone until March 29,12 and initially it was forecasted to turn southward to move ashore near the populated region of Karratha at high tide.13 It retained its southwest track and passed over several weather stations, one of which recorded sustained winds of 176 km/h (109 mph).14 Glenda made meteorology) near the less populated city of Onslow at around 1200 UTC on March 30.15 Sustained winds were estimated at Category 3 status on the Australian cyclone scale at landfall, or between 118–159 km/h (73–98 mph);16 this ranked Glenda as one of the strongest tropical cyclones to make landfall in Western Australia.17 The JTWC issued its final warning on Glenda shortly after it moved ashore. The cyclone turned south and south-southeastward and rapidly weakened over land in an area of increasing wind shear, and early on March 31 the BoM downgraded Glenda to a tropical low.18

Preparations and impact

The precursor system dropped heavy rainfall on March 23 in eastern Kimberley in the state of Western Australia. The rainfall led to record flooding in the area; the flooding washed out several roads near Kununurra, including a portion of the Great Northern Highway. Six people were evacuated due to the flooding.19

Offshore, the threat of Glenda prompted officials to close oil, representing a lack of production of 154,000 barrels of oil. Additionally, natural gas fields were closed, and several ports along the coastline were shut down during the passage of the storm.20 Prior to it making landfall, officials issued a Red Alert for several communities in the path of the cyclone. Storm shelters were opened in Karratha and Onslow.21 A few hundred people evacuated along the coastline.22

Glenda made landfall near Onslow, where sustained winds reached 117 km/h (72 mph). There, the storm produced a 24 hour rainfall total of 206 mm (8.11 in), which is the sixth greatest daily precipitation on record in the city. Several other locations reported over 200 mm (8 in), though overall precipitation was less than a usual landfalling tropical cyclone.23 The rainfall flooded several roads.24 The winds downed several trees and power lines,25 which left about 2,000 people in Karratha without electricity;26 the power outage was quickly repaired.27 Several windows at the hospital in Onslow were broken, resulting in some minor water damage.28 Overall damage was minor,29 and no deaths or injuries were reported,30 which was credited to the storm's weakening and preparations in the landfall area.31

The Bureau of Meteorology retired the name Glenda following its usage.32

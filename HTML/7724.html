The rings of Neptune, discovered in 1989 by the Voyager 2 spacecraft,1 are tenuous, faint and dusty, and resemble the rings of Jupiter more closely than those of Saturn or Uranus. As of 2008, Neptune possesses five known rings, each named for an astronomer who contributed important work on the planet:2 the Galle, LeVerrier, Lassell, Arago and Adams rings.34 Neptune also has a faint unnamed ring coincident with the orbit of Neptunian moon Galatea. Three other moons orbit between the rings: Naiad, Thalassa and Despina.5

The rings of Neptune are made of extremely dark material, likely organic compounds processed by radiation similar to that found in the rings of Uranus.6 The proportion of dust in the rings (between 20 and 70%) is high,7 while their optical depth is low, at less than 0.1.8 Uniquely, the Adams ring is divided into five discrete arcs, named Fraternit<U+00E9>, Egalite 1 and 2, Libert<U+00E9>, and Courage. The arcs occupy a narrow range of orbital longitudes and are remarkably stable, having changed only slightly since their initial detection in 1980.9 How the arcs maintain stability is still under debate. However, their stability is probably related to the resonant interaction between the Adams ring and its inner shepherd moon, Galatea.10

Discovery and observations

The first mention of rings around Neptune dates back to 1846 when William Lassell, the discoverer of Neptune's largest moon Triton, thought he had seen a ring around the planet.11 However, his claim was never confirmed and was likely an observational artifact. The first reliable detection of a ring was made in 1968 via stellar occultation, although that result had gone unnoticed until 1977 when the rings of Uranus were discovered.12 Soon after the Uranus discovery, a team from Villanova University led by Harold J. Reitsema began searching for rings around Neptune. On 24 May 1981, they detected a dip in a star's brightness during one occultation; however, the manner in which the star dimmed did not suggest a ring. Later, after the Voyager fly-by, it was found that the occultation was due to the small Neptunian moon Larissa, a highly unlikely event.13

In the 1980s, occultations were much rarer for Neptune than for Uranus, which lay near the Milky Way at the time and was thus moving against a denser field of stars. Neptune's next occultation, on 12 September 1983, resulted in a possible detection of a ring.14 However, ground-based results were inconclusive. Over the next six years, approximately 50 other occultations were observed with only about one third of them yielding positive results.15 Something (probably incomplete arcs) definitely existed around Neptune, but the parameters of the ring system remained a mystery.16 The Voyager 2 spacecraft made the definitive discovery of the Neptunian rings during its historic fly-by of Neptune in 1989. It confirmed that occasional occultation events observed before were indeed caused by the arcs within the Adams ring (see below).17 After the Voyager fly-by the previous terrestrial occultation observations were reanalyzed yielding parameters of the ring's arcs as they were in 1980s, which matched those found by Voyager almost perfectly.18

Recently, the brightest rings (Adams and LeVerrier) have been imaged with the Hubble Space Telescope and Earth-based telescopes, owing to advances in resolution and light-gathering power.19 They are visible, slightly above background noise levels, at methane-absorbing wavelengths in which the glare from Neptune is significantly reduced. The fainter rings are still far below the visibility threshold.20

General properties

Neptune possesses five distinct rings21 named, in order of increasing distance from the planet, Galle, LeVerrier, Lassell, Arago and Adams.22 In addition to these well-defined rings, Neptune may also possess an extremely faint sheet of material stretching inward from the LeVerrier to the Galle ring, and possibly farther in toward Neptune.2324 Three of the Neptunian rings are narrow, with widths of about 100 km or less;25 in contrast, the Galle and Lassell rings are broad—their widths are between 2,000 and 5,000 km.26 The Adams ring consists of five bright arcs embedded into a fainter continuous ring.27 Counting counterclockwise, the arcs are: Fraternit<U+00E9> , Egalite 1 and 2, Libert<U+00E9>, and Courage.2829 The first three names mean liberty, equality, and fraternity, after the famous motto of the French Revolution. The terminology was suggested by their original discoverers, who had found them during stellar occultations in 1984 and 1985.30 Four small Neptunian moons have orbits inside the ring system: Naiad and Thalassa orbit in the gap between Galle and LeVerrier rings, Despina is just inward of LeVerrier ring, and Galatea lies slightly inward of the Adams ring.31 Galatea is embedded in an unnamed narrow and faint ringlet.32

The Neptunian rings contain a large quantity of micrometer-sized dust: the dust fraction by cross-section area is between 20% and 70%.33 In this respect they are similar to the rings of Jupiter, in which the dust fraction is 50–100%, and are very different from the rings of Saturn and Uranus, which contain little dust (less than 0.1%).3435 The particles in Neptune's rings are made from a dark material; probably a mixture of ice with radiation-processed organics.3637 The rings are a red in color, and their geometrical (0.05) and bond (0.01–0.02) albedos are similar to those of the Uranian rings' particles and the inner Neptunian moons.38 The rings are generally optically thin (transparent); their normal optical depths do not exceed 0.1.39 As a whole, the Neptunian rings resemble those of Jupiter; both systems consist of faint narrow dusty ringlets and even more faint broad dusty rings.40

The rings of Neptune, like those of Uranus, are thought to be relatively young; their age is probably significantly less than that of the Solar System.41 Also, like those of Uranus, Neptune's rings likely resulted from the collisional fragmentation of onetime inner moons.42 Such events create moonlet belts, which act as the sources of dust for the rings. In this respect rings of Neptune are similar to faint dusty bands observed by Voyager 2 between the main rings of Uranus.43

Inner rings

The innermost ring of Neptune is called the Galle ring after Johann Gottfried Galle, the first man to see Neptune through a telescope.44 It is about 2,000 km wide and orbits 41,000–43,000 km from the planet.45 It is a faint ring with an average normal optical depth of around 10−4, and with an equivalent depth of 0.15 km.46 The fraction of dust in this ring is estimated from 40 to 70%.4748

The next ring is the LeVerrier ring after Urbain Le Verrier, who predicted Neptune's position in 1846.49 Its orbital radius is about 53,200 km,50 but it is narrow, with a width of about 113 km.51 Its normal optical depth is 0.0062 <U+00B1> 0.0015, which corresponds to an equivalent depth of 0.7 <U+00B1> 0.2 km.52 The dust fraction in the LeVerrier ring ranges from 40 to 70%.5354 The small Neptunian moon Despina, which orbits just inside of it at 52,526 km, may play a role in the ring's confinement by acting as a shepherd.55

The Lassell ring, also known as the plateau, is the broadest ring in the Neptunian system.56 It is named after William Lassell, the English astronomer who discovered Neptune's largest moon, Triton.57 This ring is a faint sheet of material occupying the space between the LeVerrier ring at about 53,200 km and the Arago ring at 57,200 km.58 Its average normal optical depth is around 10−4, which corresponds to an equivalent depth of 0.4 km.59 The ring's dust fraction is in the range from 20 to 40%.60

There is a small peak of brightness near the outer edge of the Lassell ring located at 57,200 km from Neptune and less than 100 km wide,61 which some planetary scientists call the Arago ring after Fran<U+00E7>ois Arago, a French mathematician, physicist, astronomer and politician.62 However, many publications do not mention Arago ring at all.63

Adams ring

The outer Adams ring, with an orbital radius of about 63,930 km,64 is the most famous and best studied of the Neptune's rings.65 It is named after John Couch Adams, who predicted the position of the Neptune independently of LeVerrier.66 This ring is narrow, slightly eccentric and inclined, with total width of about 35 km (15–50 km),67 and its normal optical depth is around 0.011 <U+00B1> 0.003 outside the arcs, which corresponds to the equivalent depth of about 0.4 km.68 The fraction of dust in this ring is from 20 to 40%—lower than in other narrow rings.69 Neptune's small moon Galatea, which orbits just inside of the Adams ring at 61,953 km, acts like a shepherd, keeping ring particles inside a narrow range of orbital radii through a 42:43 outer Lindbland resonance.70 Galatea's gravitational influence creates 42 radial wiggles in the Adams ring with an amplitude of about 30 km, which have been used to infer Galatea's mass.71

Arcs

The brightest parts of the Adams ring, the ring arcs, were the first elements of Neptune's ring system to be discovered.72 The arcs are discrete regions within the ring in which the particles that comprise it are mysteriously clustered together. The Adams ring is known to comprise five short arcs, which occupy a relatively narrow range of longitudes from 247<U+00B0> to 294<U+00B0>. In 1986 they were located between 247–257<U+00B0> (Fraternit<U+00E9> ), 261–264<U+00B0> (<U+00C9>galit<U+00E9> 1), 265–266<U+00B0> (<U+00C9>galit<U+00E9> 2), 276–280<U+00B0> (Libert<U+00E9>), and 284.5–285.5<U+00B0> (Courage) longitudes.7374 The brightest and longest arc was Fraternit<U+00E9>; the faintest was Courage. The normal optical depths of the arcs are estimated to lie in the range 0.03–0.0975 (0.034 <U+00B1> 0.005 for the leading edge of Libert<U+00E9> arc as measured by stellar occultation), the radial widths are approximately the same as those of the continuous ring—about 30 km.76 The fraction of dust in the arcs is from 40 to 70%.77 The arcs in the Adams ring are somewhat similar to the arc in Saturn's G ring.78

The highest resolution Voyager 2 images revealed a pronounced clumpiness in the arcs, with a typical separation between visible clumps of 0.1<U+00B0> to 0.2<U+00B0>, which corresponds to 100–200 km along the ring. Because the clumps were not resolved, they may or may not include larger bodies, but are certainly associated with concentrations of microscopic dust as evidenced by their enhanced brightness when backlit by the Sun.79

The arcs are quite stable structures. They were detected by ground based stellar occultations in the 1980s, by Voyager 2 in 1989 and by Hubble Space Telescope and ground based telescopes in 1997–2005 and remained at approximately the same orbital longitudes.8081 However some changes have been noticed. The overall brightness of arcs decreased since 1986.82 The Courage arc jumped forward by 8<U+00B0> to 294<U+00B0> (it probably jumped over to the next stable co-rotation resonance position) while the Libert<U+00E9> arc had almost disappeared by 2003.83 The Fraternit<U+00E9> and <U+00C9>galit<U+00E9> (1 and 2) arcs have demonstrated irregular variations in their relative brightness. Their observed dynamics is probably related to the exchange of dust between them.84 Courage, a very faint arc found during the Voyager flyby, was seen to flare in brightness in 1998, while more recently it was back to its usual dimness. However, visible light observations show that the total amount of material in the arcs has remained approximately constant, but they are dimmer in the infra-red light where previous observations were taken.85

Confinement

As of 2008, the arcs in the Adams ring remain unexplained.86 Their existence is a puzzle because basic orbital dynamics imply that they should spread out into a uniform ring over a matter of years. Several theories about the arcs' confinement have been suggested, the most widely publicized of which holds that Galatea confines the arcs via its 42:43 co-rotational inclination resonance (CIR).87 The resonance creates 84 stable sites along the ring<U+2019>s orbit, each 4<U+00B0> long, with arcs residing in the adjacent sites.88 However measurements of the rings' mean motion with Hubble and Keck telescopes in 1998 led to the conclusion that the rings are not in CIR with Galatea.8990

A later model suggested that confinement resulted from a co-rotational eccentricity resonance (CER).91 The model takes into account the finite mass of the Adams ring, which is necessary to move the resonance closer to the ring. A byproduct of this theory is a mass estimate for the Adams ring—about 0.002 of the mass of Galatea.92 A third theory proposed in 1986 requires an additional moon orbiting inside the ring; the arcs in this case are trapped in its Lagrangian points. However Voyager 2 placed strict constraints on the size and mass of any undiscovered moons, making such a theory unlikely.93 Some other more complicated theories hold that a number of moonlets are trapped in co-rotational resonances with Galatea, providing confinement of the arcs and simultaneously serving as sources of the dust.94

Exploration

The rings were investigated in detail during the Voyager 2 spacecraft's flyby of Neptune in August 1989.95 They were studied with optical imaging, and through observations of occultations in ultraviolet and visible light.96 Voyager 2 observed the rings in different geometries relative to the Sun, producing images of back-scattered, forward-scattered and side-scattered light.97 Analysis of these images allowed derivation of the phase function (dependence of the ring's reflectivity on the angle between the observer and Sun), and geometrical and bond albedo of ring particles.98 Analysis of Voyager's images also led to discovery of six inner moons of Neptune, including Galatea that is a shepherd of the Adams ring.99

Notes

  1. The normal optical depth <U+03C4> of a ring is the ratio of the total geometrical cross-section of the ring's particles to the square area of the ring. It assumes values from zero to infinity. A light beam passing normally through a ring will be attenuated by the factor e−<U+03C4>.116

  2. The equivalent depth ED of a ring is defined as an integral of the normal optical depth across the ring. In other words ED=<U+222B><U+03C4>dr, where r is radius.117

  3. The equivalent depth of Galle and Lassel rings is a product of their width and the normal optical depth.

  4. The corotation inclination resonance (CIR) of the order m between a moon on inclined orbit and a ring occurs if the pattern speed of the perturbing potential Ω (from a moon) equals the mean motion of the ring particles np. In other words the following condition should be met mΩ = npm = (m − 1)ns + Ω̇s, where Ω̇s and ns are the nodal precession rate and mean motion of the moon, respectively.118 CIR supports 2m stable cites along the ring.

  5. The corotation eccentricity resonance (CER) of the order m between a moon on eccentric orbit and a ring occurs if the pattern speed of the perturbing potential Ω (from a moon) equals the mean motion of the ring particles np. In other words the following condition should be met mΩ = npm = (m − 1)ns + ω̇s, where ω̇s and ns are the apsidal precession rate and mean motion of the moon, respectively.119 CER supports m stable cites along the ring.

  6. The longitude system is fixed as of 18 August 1989. The zero point corresponds to the zero meridian on Neptune.120

  7. The forward-scattered light is the light scattered at a small angle relative to solar light. The back-scattered light is the light scattered at an angle close to 180<U+00B0> (in the back direction) relative to solar light. The scattering angle is close to 90<U+00B0> for the side-scattered light.

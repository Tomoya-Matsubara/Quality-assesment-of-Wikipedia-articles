Ratanakiri () is a province in northeastern Cambodia that borders Laos to the north, Vietnam to the east, Mondulkiri Province to the south, and Stung Treng Province to the west. The province extends from the mountains of the Annamite Range in the north, across a hilly plateau between the Tonle San and Tonle Srepok rivers, to tropical deciduous forests in the south. In recent years, logging and mining have scarred Ratanakiri's environment, long known for its beauty.

For over a millennium, Ratanakiri has been occupied by the highland Khmer Loeu, who are a minority elsewhere in Cambodia. During the region's early history, its Khmer Loeu inhabitants were exploited as slaves by neighboring empires. The slave trade economy ended during the French colonial era, but a harsh Khmerization campaign after Cambodia's independence again threatened Khmer Loeu ways of life. The Khmer Rouge built its headquarters in the province in the 1960s, and bombing during the Vietnam War devastated the region. Today, rapid development in the province is altering traditional ways of life.

Ratanakiri is sparsely populated; its 150,000 residents make up just over 1% of the country's total population. Residents generally live in villages of 20 to 60 families and engage in subsistence shifting agriculture. Ratanakiri is among the least developed provinces of Cambodia. Its infrastructure is poor, and the local government is weak. Health indicators in Ratanakiri are extremely poor, and almost one in four children die before reaching the age of five. Education levels are also low; three quarters of the population is illiterate.

History

Present-day Ratanakiri has been occupied since at least the stone or bronze age, and trade between the region's highlanders and towns along the Gulf of Thailand dates to at least the 4th century A.D.1 The region was invaded by Annamites, the Cham, the Khmer, and the Thai during its early history, but no empire ever brought the area under centralized control.2 From the 13th century or earlier until the 19th century, highland villages were often raided by Khmer, Lao, and Thai slave traders.3 The region was conquered by local Laotian rulers in the 18th century and then by the Thai in the 19th century.4 The area was incorporated into French Indochina in 1893, and colonial rule replaced slave trading.5 The French built huge rubber plantations, especially in Labansiek (present-day Banlung); indigenous workers were used for construction and rubber harvesting.6 While under French control, the land comprising present-day Ratanakiri was transferred from Siam (Thailand) to Laos and then to Cambodia.7 Although highland groups initially resisted their colonial rulers, by the end of the colonial era in 1953 they had been subdued.8

Ratanakiri Province was created in 1959 from land that had been the eastern area of Stung Treng Province.9 The name Ratanakiri (<U+179A><U+178F><U+1793><U+1782><U+17B7><U+179A><U+17B8>) is formed from the Khmer words <U+179A><U+178F><U+1793><U+17C8> (ratana, gem) and <U+1782><U+17B7><U+179A><U+17B8> (kiri, mountain), describing two features for which the province is known.10 During the 1950s and 1960s, Norodom Sihanouk instituted a development and Khmerization campaign in northeast Cambodia that was designed to bring villages under government control, limit the influence of insurgents in the area, and "modernize" indigenous communities.11 Some Khmer Loeu were forcibly moved to the lowlands to be educated in Khmer language and culture, ethnic Khmer from elsewhere in Cambodia were moved into the province, and roads and large rubber plantations were built.12 After facing harsh working conditions and sometimes involuntary labor on the plantations, many Khmer Loeu left their traditional homes and moved farther from provincial towns.13 In 1968, tensions led to an uprising by the Brao in which several Khmer were killed.14 The government responded harshly, torching settlements and killing hundreds of villagers.15

In the 1960s, the ascendant Khmer Rouge forged an alliance with ethnic minorities in Ratanakiri, exploiting Khmer Loeu resentment of the central government.16 The Communist Party of Kampuchea headquarters was moved to Ratanakiri in 1966, and hundreds of Khmer Loeu joined CPK units.17 During this period, there was also extensive Vietnamese activity in Ratanakiri.18 Vietnamese communists had operated in Ratanakiri since the 1940s; at a June 1969 press conference, Sihanouk said that Ratanakiri was "practically North Vietnamese territory".19 Between March 1969 and May 1970, the United States undertook a massive covert bombing campaign in the region, aiming to disrupt sanctuaries for communist Vietnamese troops. Villagers were forced outside of main towns to escape the bombings, foraging for food and living on the run with the Khmer Rouge.20 In June 1970, the central government withdrew its troops from Ratanakiri, abandoning the area to Khmer Rouge control.21 The Khmer Rouge regime, which had not initially been harsh in Ratanakiri, became increasingly oppressive.22 The Khmer Loeu were forbidden from speaking their native languages or practicing their traditional customs and religion, which were seen as incompatible with communism.23 Communal living became compulsory, and the province's few schools were closed.24 Purges of ethnic minorities increased in frequency, and thousands of refugees fled to Vietnam and Laos.25 Preliminary studies indicate that bodies accounting for approximately 5% of Ratanakiri's residents were deposited in mass graves, a significantly lower rate than elsewhere in Cambodia.26

After the Vietnamese defeated the Khmer Rouge in 1979, government policy toward Ratanakiri became one of benign neglect.27 The Khmer Loeu were permitted to return to their traditional livelihoods, but the government provided little infrastructure in the province.28 Under the Vietnamese, there was little contact between the provincial government and many local communities.29 Long after the fall of the Khmer Rouge regime, however, Khmer Rouge rebels remained in the forests of Ratanakiri.30 Rebels largely surrendered their arms in the 1990s, though attacks along provincial roads continued until 2002.31

Ratanakiri's recent history has been characterized by development and attendant challenges to traditional ways of life.32 The national government has built roads, encouraged tourism and agriculture, and facilitated rapid immigration of lowland Khmers into Ratanakiri.33 Road improvements and political stability have increased land prices, and land alienation in Ratanakiri has been a major problem.34 Despite a 2001 law allowing indigenous communities to obtain collective title to traditional lands, some villages have been left nearly landless.35 The national government has granted concessions over land traditionally possessed by Ratanakiri's indigenous peoples,36 and even land "sales" have often involved bribes to officials, coercion, threats, or misinformation.37 Following the involvement of several international non-governmental organizations (NGOs), land alienation has decreased in frequency.38 In the 2000s, Ratanakiri also received hundreds of Degar (Montagnard) refugees fleeing unrest in neighboring Vietnam; the Cambodian government was criticized for its forcible repatriation of many refugees.39

Geography and climate

The geography of Ratanakiri Province is diverse, encompassing rolling hills, mountains, plateaus, lowland watersheds, and crater lakes.40 Two major rivers, Tonle San and Tonle Srepok, flow from east to west across the province. The province is known for its lush forests; as of 1997, 70<U+2013>80% of the province was forested, either with old-growth forest or with secondary forest regrown after shifting cultivation.41 In the far north of the province are mountains of the Annamite Range; the area is characterized by dense broadleaf evergreen forests, relatively poor soil, and abundant wildlife.42 In the highlands between Tonle San and Tonle Srepok, the home of the vast majority of Ratanakiri's population, a hilly basalt plateau provides fertile red soils.43 Secondary forests dominate this region.44 South of the Srepok River is a flat area of tropical deciduous forests.45

Like other areas of Cambodia, Ratanakiri has a monsoonal climate with a rainy season from June to October, a cool season from November to January, and a hot season from March to May.46 Ratanakiri tends to be cooler than elsewhere in Cambodia.47 The average daily high temperature in the province is , and the average daily low temperature is .48 Annual precipitation is approximately .49 Flooding often occurs during the rainy season and has been exacerbated by the newly built Yali Falls Dam.50

Ratanakiri has some of the most biologically diverse lowland tropical rainforest and montane forest ecosystems in mainland Southeast Asia.51 One 1996 survey of two sites in Ratanakiri and one site in neighboring Mondulkiri recorded 44 mammal species, 76 bird species, and 9 reptile species.52 A 2007 survey of Ratanakiri's Virachey National Park recorded 30 ant species, 19 katydid species, 37 fish species, 35 reptile species, 26 amphibian species, and 15 mammal species, including several species never before observed.53 Wildlife in Ratanakiri includes Asian elephants, gaur, and monkeys.54 Ratanakiri is an important site for the conservation of endangered birds, including the giant ibis and the greater adjutant.55 The province's forests contain a wide variety of flora; one half-hectare forest inventory identified 189 species of trees and 320 species of ground flora and saplings.56

Nearly half of Ratanakiri has been set aside in protected areas,57 which include Lomphat Wildlife Sanctuary and Virachey National Park. Even these protected areas, however, are subject to illegal logging, poaching, and mineral extraction.58 Though the province has been known for its relatively pristine environment, recent development has spawned environmental problems.59 The unspoiled image of the province often conflicts with the reality on the ground: visitors "expecting to find pristine forests teeming with wildlife are increasingly disappointed to find lifeless patches of freshly cut tree stumps".60 Land use patterns are changing as population growth has accelerated and agriculture and logging have intensified.61 Soil erosion is increasing, and microclimates are being altered.62 Habitat loss and unsustainable hunting have contributed to the province's decreasing biodiversity.63

Government and administrative divisions

Government in Ratanakiri is weak, largely due to the province's remoteness, ethnic diversity, and recent history of Khmer Rouge dominance.64 The provincial legal framework is poor, and the rule of law is even weaker in Ratanakiri than elsewhere in Cambodia.65 Furthermore, government services are ineffective and insufficient to meet the needs of the province.66 The Cambodian government has traditionally accepted substantial support from NGOs in the region.67

Pao Ham Phan is the provincial governor.68 Commune councils in Ratanakiri are composed of 219 members representing the CPP, 21 members representing the Sam Rainsy Party, and 13 members representing the Funcinpec Party.69 Political scientist Caroline Hughes has suggested that the CPP's overwhelming dominance in rural areas such as Ratanakiri stems from the central government's ability to suppress collective action, which in urban areas is offset by international donors and NGOs that provide support for opposition parties.70 Thirty-six commune council members in Ratanakiri (14.2%) are women, and 98% of Ratanakiri's government staff is Khmer.71 Bou Thang, a member of the CPP, represents Ratanakiri in the National Assembly of Cambodia.72

Village government in Ratanakiri has both traditional and administrative components. Traditional forms of government, namely village elders and other indigenous institutions, are dominant.73 Members of each village designate one or more community elders to manage village affairs, mediate conflicts, and ensure that villagers follow customary laws, particularly about land and resource use.74 Elders do not play an autocratic role, and are instead primarily respected advisors and consensus builders.75 Village elders are generally male, but women also play a role in the management of the community and its resources.76 A village may also have a village chief, i.e., a local government person who is appointed by a higher governmental official.77 The village chief serves as a liaison between the village and outside government officials, but lacks traditional authority.78 The role of the village chief in village governance may be poorly defined; in one Kreung village, residents told a researcher that they were "very unclear exactly what the work of the village chief entailed."79

Economy and transportation

Most of the indigenous residents of Ratanakiri are subsistence farmers, practicing slash and burn shifting cultivation. (See Culture below for more information on traditional subsistence practices.) Many families are beginning to shift production to cash crops such as cashews, mangoes, and tobacco, a trend that has accelerated in recent years.81 Ratanakiri villagers have traditionally had little contact with the cash economy.82 Barter exchange remains widespread, and Khmer Loeu villagers tended to visit markets only once per year until quite recently.83 As of 2005, monetary income in the province averaged US$5 per month per person; purchased possessions such as motorcycles, televisions, and karaoke sets have become extremely desirable.84

Larger-scale agriculture occurs on rubber, coffee, and cashew plantations.85 Other economic activities in the province include gem mining and commercial logging. The most abundant gem in Ratanakiri is blue zircon. Small quantities of amethyst, peridot, and black opal are also produced.86 Gems are generally mined using traditional methods, with individuals digging holes and tunnels and manually removing the gems; recently, however, commercial mining operations have been moving into the province.87 Logging, particularly illegal logging, has been a problem both for environmental reasons and because of land alienation. This illegal logging has been undertaken by the Cambodian military and by Vietnamese loggers.88 In 1997, an estimated 300,000 cubic meters of logs were exported illegally from Ratanakiri to Vietnam, compared to a legal limit of 36,000 cubic meters.89 John Dennis, a researcher for the Asian Development Bank, described the logging in Ratanakiri as a "human rights emergency".90

Ratanakiri's tourist industry is rapidly expanding: visits to the province increased from 6,000 in 2002 to 105,000 in 2008.91 The region's tourism development strategy focuses on encouraging ecotourism.92 Increasing tourism in Ratanakiri has been problematic because local communities receive very little income from tourism and because guides sometimes bring tourists to villages without residents' consent, disrupting traditional ways of life.93 A few initiatives have sought to address these issues: a provincial tourism steering committee aims to ensure that tourism is non-destructive, and some programs provide English and tourism skills to indigenous people.94

Ox-cart and motorcycle are common means of transportation in Ratanakiri.95 The provincial road system is better than in some parts of the country, but remains in somewhat bad condition.96 In January 2007, construction started on National Road 78 between Banlung and the Vietnam border; the road is expected to increase trade between Cambodia and Vietnam.97 There is a small airport in Banlung,98 but flights to Ratanakiri had been discontinued as of 2008.99

Demographics and towns

As of 2008, Ratanakiri Province had a population of approximately 150,000.100 Its population grew by 59% between 1998 and 2008, largely due to internal migration.101 In 2008, Ratanakiri made up 1.1% of Cambodia's total population; its population density of 13.9 residents per square kilometer was less than one fifth the national average.102 About 70% of the province's population lives in the highlands; of the other 30%, approximately half live in more urbanized towns, and half live along rivers and in the lowlands, where they practice wetland rice cultivation and engage in market activities.103 Banlung, the provincial capital located in the central highlands, is by far the province's largest town, with a population of approximately 25,000.104 Other significant towns include Veun Sai in the north and Lomphat in the south, with populations of 2,000 and 3,000 respectively.105

In 1998, 44.4% of Ratanakiri residents were aged 14 or younger, 52.1% were aged 15 to 64, and 3.5% were aged 65 or older; 49.2% of residents were male, and 50.8% were female.106 Of Ratanakiri residents aged 15 or older, 20.9% were single and had never been married, 71.6% were married, 5.1% were widowed, and 2.4% were divorced or separated.107 Each household had an average of 5.6 members, and most households (87.5%) were headed by men.108

While highland peoples have inhabited Ratanakiri for well over a millennium, lowland peoples have migrated to the province in the last 200 years.109 As of 1998, various highland groups collectively called Khmer Loeu made up approximately 70% of Ratanakiri's population.110 These groups included the Tampuan (24.3%), Jarai (17.1%), Kreung (16.3%), Brou (7.0%), Kachok (2.7%), Kavet (1.9%), Kuy (0.5%), and Lun (0.1%).111 Ethnic Khmers made up 19.1% of the population, and ethnic Lao made up 9.6%.112 The remainder consisted of Vietnamese (0.7%), Cham (0.6%), and Chinese (0.3%).113 Since the 1998 census, migration to Ratanakiri from elsewhere in Cambodia has accelerated, which has likely increased the proportion of Khmers in the province.114 Though the official language of Ratanakiri (like all of Cambodia) is Khmer, each indigenous group speaks its own language.115 Less than 10% of Ratanakiri's indigenous population can speak Khmer fluently.116

Health, education, and development

Health indicators in Ratanakiri are the worst in Cambodia.117 Malaria, tuberculosis, intestinal parasites, cholera, diarrhea, and vaccine-preventable diseases are endemic.118 Ratanakiri has Cambodia's highest rates of maternal and child mortality, with 22.9% of children dying before the age of five.119 Ratanakiri also has the country's highest rates of severe malnutrition.120 Ratanakiri residents' poor health can be attributed to a variety of factors, including poverty, remoteness of villages, poor quality medical services, and language and cultural barriers that prevent Khmer Loeu from obtaining medical care.121 The province has one referral hospital, 10 health centers, and 17 health posts.122 Medical equipment and supplies are minimal, and most health facilities are staffed by nurses or midwifes, who are often poorly trained and irregularly paid.123

As of 1998, Ratanakiri had 76 primary schools, one junior high school, and one high school.124 Education levels, particularly among Khmer Loeu, are very low. A 2002 survey of residents in six villages found that fewer than 10% of respondents had attended any primary school.125 Access to education is limited because of the expense of books, distance to schools, children's need to contribute to their families' livelihood, frequent absence of teachers, and instruction that is culturally inappropriate and in a language foreign to most students.126 Only 23.5% of Ratanakiri residents are literate (compared to 67.3% in Cambodia overall), with lower rates among those living outside Banlung District (15.7%) and among women (15.3%).127 Bilingual education initiatives, in which students begin instruction in native languages and gradually transition to instruction in Khmer, began in Ratanakiri in 2002 and appear to have been successful.128 The programs aim to make education more accessible to speakers of indigenous languages, as well as to give Khmer Loeu access to national political and economic affairs by providing Khmer language skills.129

Ratanakiri is one of the least developed provinces in Cambodia.130 Most Ratanakiri residents (61.1%) obtain water from springs, streams, ponds, or rain; much of the remainder (32.2%) obtains water from dug wells.131 Only 5.5% of Ratanakiri residents obtain water from sources that are considered safe (purchased water, piped water, or tube/piped wells).132 Most households use kerosene lamps and other sources such as oil lamps for lighting, and few (39.5% in Banlung District and 2.1% elsewhere) have toilet facilities.133 Almost all households (96.2%) use firewood as the main fuel for cooking.134 A variety of NGOs, including Oxfam and Health Unlimited, work to improve health and living conditions in the province.135

Culture

Khmer Loeu typically practice subsistence slash and burn shifting cultivation in small villages of between 20 and 60 nuclear families.136 Each village collectively owns and governs a forest territory whose boundaries are known though not marked.137 Within this land, each family is allocated, on average, 1<U+2013>2 hectares (2.5<U+2013>5 acres) of actively cultivated land and 5<U+2013>6 hectares (12.5<U+2013>15 acres) of fallow land.138 The ecologically sustainable cultivation cycle practiced by the Khmer Loeu generally lasts 10 to 15 years.139 Villagers supplement their agricultural livelihood with low-intensity hunting, fishing, and gathering over a large area.140

Khmer Loeu diets in Ratanakiri are largely dictated by the food that is available for harvesting or gathering.141 Numerous food taboos also limit food choice, particularly among pregnant women, children, and the sick.142 The primary staple grain is rice, though most families experience rice shortages during the six months before harvest time.143 Some families have begun to plant maize to alleviate this problem; other sources of grain include potatoes, cassava, and taro.144 Most Khmer Loeu diets are low in protein, which is limited in availability.145 Wild game and fish are major protein sources, and smaller animals such as rats, wild chickens, and insects are also sometimes eaten.146 Domestic animals such as pigs, cows, and buffaloes are only eaten when sacrifices are made.147 In the rainy season, many varieties of vegetables and leaves are gathered from the forest.148 (Vegetables are generally not cultivated.149) Commonly eaten fruits include bananas, jackfruit, papayas, and mangoes.150

Houses in rural Ratanakiri are made from bamboo, rattan, wood, saek, and kanma leaves, all of which are collected from nearby forests; they typically last for around three years.151 Village spatial organization varies by ethnic group.152 Kreung villages are constructed in a circular manner, with houses facing inwards toward a central meeting house.153 In Jarai villages, vast longhouses are inhabited by all extended families, with the inner house divided into smaller compartments.154 Tampuan villages may follow either pattern.155

Nearly all Khmer Loeu are animist, and their cosmologies are intertwined with the natural world.156 Some forests are believed to be inhabited by local spirits, and local taboos forbid cutting in those areas.157 Within spirit forests, certain natural features such as rock formations, waterfalls, pools, and vegetation are sacred.158 Major sacrificial festivals in Ratanakiri occur during March and April, when fields are selected and prepared for the new planting season.159 Christian missionaries are present in the province, and some Khmer Loeu have converted to Christianity.160 The region's ethnic Khmer are Buddhist.161 There is also a small Muslim community, consisting mainly of ethnic Cham.162

Because of the province's high prevalence of malaria and its distance from regional centers, Ratanakiri was isolated from Western influences until the late 20th century.163 Major cultural shifts have occurred in recent years however, particularly in villages near roads and district towns; these changes have been attributed to contact with internal immigrants, government officials, and NGO workers.164 Clothing and diets are becoming more standardized, and traditional music is being displaced by Khmer music.165 Many villagers have also observed a loss of respect for elders and a growing divide between the young and the old.166 Young people have begun to refuse to abide by traditional rules and have stopped believing in spirits.167

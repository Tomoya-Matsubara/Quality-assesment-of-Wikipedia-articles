After World War II, socialist realism on the Soviet model was imposed on the USSR's new satellites, including Romania. This was accompanied by a series of organisational and repressive moves, for instance the incarceration of numerous poets (some of whom had had links to the Iron Guard). Cultural Stalinism, between 1948 and 1956, broke down Romania's pre-existing system of values and corresponding cultural institutions in an attempt to create a "new man". As in the political and economic spheres, cultural Stalinism was forcibly imposed, intellectuals' links with the West were completely severed, and the Romanian Academy and long-standing professional organisations such as the Society of Romanian Writers or the Society of Romanian Composers were dissolved and replaced with new ones, from which members inconvenient to the new regime were purged. In 1948 a catalogue 522 pages long was printed, encompassing some 8,000 banned books and magazines, which were removed from public libraries and school textbooks. Certain authors' works were banned entirely, including those of Octavian Goga, Nichifor Crainic and Mircea Vulc<U+0103>nescu. Western authors on the banned list included Plato, Spinoza, Nietzsche, Bergson, Poe and Gide.

In literature

The symbolic debut of socialist realism in literature, as an official ideology, took place in January 1948, when three articles signed by Sorin Toma were published in Sc<U+00EE>nteia. Titled "The Poetry of Putrefaction or the Putrefaction of Poetry", they dealt with the poetic works of Tudor Arghezi. The language used was extremely harsh and marked a complete break with interwar values: "With a foul-smelling vocabulary [...], Arghezi does in poetry only what Picasso did in painting, introducing excrement as artistic material... One finds bits of real beauty here and there in Arghezi's poetry." In 1950, the Mihai Eminescu School of Literature was founded, with the aim of forming a new generation of writers in the Romanian People's Republic. In an article published in Via<U+0163>a Rom<U+00E2>neasc<U+0103> (nr. 3 of 1951), Mihai Beniuc, a member of the Writers' Union of Romania, offered a definition of the socialist-realist poet: "He must be a philosopher familiar with the most profound ideas of the age [...], toward which Marx, Engels, Lenin and Stalin opened the way [...], and an activist in service of those ideas."

Newly appeared literary critics, guided by Leonte R<U+0103>utu, published studies in the spirit of the socialist-realist doctrine. These included Ovid S. Crohm<U+0103>lniceanu—Un roman al industrializ<U+0103>rii socialiste ("A Novel of Socialist Industrialization"), Silvian Iosifescu—Pe drumul <U+00EE>nfloririi gospod<U+0103>riei agricole colective ("On the Road to the Flowering of the Administration of Collective Agriculture"), Mihai Gafi<U+0163>a—Romanul luptei tractori<U+015F>tilor ("The Novel of the Tractor-Drivers' Struggle"), Nestor Ignat—O carte despre frumuse<U+0163>ea vie<U+0163>ii noi ("A Book about the Beauty of the New Life"), Mihai Novicov—Pe marginea poeziei lui Dan De<U+015F>liu ("On the Margin of the Poetry of Dan De<U+015F>liu"), Traian <U+015E>elmaru—Mitrea Cocor de Mihail Sadoveanu ("Mitrea Cocor by Mihail Sadoveanu") and Ion Vitner—Poezia lui A. Toma ("The Poetry of A. Toma").

Prose works

A few representative examples:

  • Mihail Sadoveanu: Fantezii r<U+0103>s<U+0103>ritene ("Eastern Fantasies"; 1946), P<U+0103>una Mic<U+0103> ("The Little Peahen", 1948), Mitrea Cocor ("Mitrea the Crane", 1950), a novel that became a symbol of socialist-realist prose, with its depiction of class struggle, of positive heroes, of the Communist ironworker Florea Costea and the boiler-maker Voicu Cernea, of the moral and ideological transformation of Mitrea during his imprisonment in the Soviet Union.
  • Zaharia Stancu, first de facto president of the newly founded Writers' Union of Romania (1949): the novel Descul<U+0163> ("Barefoot; first edition, 1948).
  • Alexandru Jar: Sf<U+00E2>r<U+015F>itul jalbelor ("The End of Complaints"; 1950), Marea preg<U+0103>tire ("The Great Preparation"; 1952), novels about the Grivi<U+0163>a Strike of 1933 that followed the class-struggle pattern and gave its own historical truth.
  • Petru Dumitriu: Drum f<U+0103>r<U+0103> pulbere ("Road without Dust") and Pas<U+0103>rea furtunii ("The Bird of the Storm"), novels that hailed the "achievements" realised while digging the Danube-Black Sea Canal, known even at the time as a harsh political prison.
  • Eusebiu Camilar: the novel Negura ("The Fog"; 1949), a book filled with barbarism, stupidity and cruelty, all ascribed to the Romanian Army during its war "of conquering the Soviet Union".
  • Eugen Barbu: author of the novels Groapa ("The Pit"; 1957) and <U+015E>oseaua Nordului ("The Highway of the North").

Poetry

Alexandru Toma, later judged a mediocre poet, was proclaimed the greatest Romanian poet alive. With verses like "imperialist american/c<U+0103>dea-<U+0163>i-ar bomba <U+00EE>n ocean" ("American imperialist/Would that your bomb fell into the ocean"), A. Toma was an official model for Romanian poets until his death in 1954.

Other important representatives of socialist-realist poetry, who transformed slogans of the Romanian Communist Party into their own verses, included:

  • Dan De<U+015F>liu: C<U+00E2>ntec pentru tovar<U+0103><U+015F>ul plan (Song for Comrade Plan"), Laz<U+0103>r de la Rusca ("Laz<U+0103>r of Rusca"), massive poems that appeared on two pages of Sc<U+00EE>nteia.
  • Victor Tulbure: Balada tovar<U+0103><U+015F>ului c<U+0103>zut <U+00EE>mp<U+0103>r<U+0163>ind Sc<U+00EE>nteia <U+00EE>n ilegalitate ("Ballad of the Comrade Who Fell while Illegally Distributing Sc<U+00EE>nteia").
  • Marcel Bresla<U+015F>u: C<U+00EE>ntec de leag<U+0103>n al Donc<U+0103>i ("Donca's Lullaby").
  • Mihai Beniuc: <U+00CE>n frunte comuni<U+015F>tii ("Communists at the Top"), C<U+00E2>ntec pentru tovar<U+0103><U+015F>ul Gheorghiu-Dej ("Song for Comrade Gheorghiu-Dej, Partidul m-a <U+00EE>nv<U+0103><U+0163>at ("The Party Taught Me").

Other poets who practiced the style include: Eugen Frunz<U+0103>, Miron Radu Paraschivescu, Nina Cassian (An viu, nou<U+0103> sute <U+015F>i <U+015F>aptesprezece — "Living Year, Nine Hundred and Seventeen), Ion Brad (Cincisutistul — "The Five Hundredist"), Veronica Porumbacu (Tovar<U+0103><U+015F>ul Matei a primit Ordinul Muncii — "Comrade Matei Has Received the Order of Labour"), Maria Banu<U+015F> (<U+0162>ie-<U+0163>i vorbesc, Americ<U+0103>! — "I Am Speaking to You, America!"), <U+015E>tefan Iure<U+015F> (Ucenicul Partidului — "The Disciple of the Party"), Virgil Teodorescu and Mihu Dragomir.

Drama

A "militant" theatre was conceived, with an active presence in the ongoing class struggle and solidarity with the entire people around the ideals of the Communist Party. Notable examples include:

  • Mihail Davidoglu: Omul din Ceatal ("The Man from Ceatal"), Minerii ("The Miners"), Cetatea de foc ("The Citadel of Fire").
  • Aurel Baranga: Bal la F<U+0103>g<U+0103>d<U+0103>u ("Ball at the Inn"; 1946) and, together with Nicolae Moraru, Anii negri ("The Black Years").
  • Maria Banu<U+015F>: Ziua cea Mare ("The Big Day"), the first play about the newly collectivised Romanian village.
  • Lucia Demetrius: Cump<U+0103>na ("The Shadoof", 1949), Vadul nou ("The New Crossing", 1951), Oameni de azi ("People of Today", 1952).
  • Alexandru Mirodan: Ziari<U+015F>tii ("The Newspapermen", 1956), <U+015E>eful sectorului suflete ("The Chief of the Souls' Sector", 1963).

Another characteristic of socialist realism was the necessity of a positive hero. Apparently, Ion Luca Caragiale's O scrisoare pierdut<U+0103> could not be staged due to the absence of such a hero.

Architecture

Bucharest's Casa Sc<U+00EE>nteii is a signature example of a socialist-realist building in Romania.

Plastic arts

Beginning in 1948, avant-garde currents of the first half of the 20th century, considered decadent and detached from reality, were totally rejected for their "bourgeois formalism". In 1949, the Plastic Artists' Cooperative was founded in Bucharest. There, promising young artists such as Ion Bi<U+0163>an, Traian Trestioreanu, Paul Gherasim, Virgil Alm<U+0103><U+015F>an and <U+015E>tefan Sevastre were obliged to execute works of "visual agitation" and decoration, painting onto huge posters the portraits of the "four teachers" of Marxism-Leninism and of the heads of party and state in the Romanian People's Republic. As the state was artists' sole patron, through the Plastic Fund, even established artists could not avoid adopting the conventional themes imposed by socialist realism. In spite of the works' banal content, these talented individuals nevertheless managed to produce important works of art. Among them were Camil Ressu (Semnarea apelului pentru pace — "The Signing of the Peace Appeal"), Alexandru Ciucurencu (1 Mai — "May 1", Ana Ip<U+0103>tescu) and Corneliu Baba (O<U+0163>elari — "Steel-workers").

Other socialist-realist painters, with representative works, included:

Theodor Har<U+015F>ia (<U+015E>antierul de la Bicaz — "The Bicaz Construction Site"), Gavril Miklossy (Grivi<U+0163>a, 1933; Lupeni, 1929), Spiru Chintil<U+0103> (Femei <U+00EE>narmate <U+00EE>n g<U+0103>rzile patriotice — Women Armed in the Patriotic Guards"), Br<U+0103>du<U+0163> Covaliu (Greva de la Lupeni — "The Lupeni Strike"), Insurec<U+0163>ia armat<U+0103> din 23 August 1944 — "The Armed Insurrection of 23 August 1944"), Constantin Piliu<U+0163><U+0103> (Revolu<U+0163>onari <U+00EE>ncarcera<U+0163>i — "Incarcerated Revolutionaries"), Gheorghe Iacob (Propagandist de partid la sat — "Party Propagandist in the Village"), Coriolan Hora (Sudorii — "The Welders", Recoltarea porumbului — "The Corn Harvest"), Ion Bi<U+0163>an (Recolta — "The Harvest", Victoria — "The Victory"), Gheorghe <U+015E>aru (Sudori<U+0163><U+0103> — "The Female Welder"), <U+015E>tefan Sz<U+00F6>ny (Tipografie clandestin<U+0103> — "Clandestine Typography", Moartea partizanului — "The Death of the Partisan"), Iulia H<U+0103>l<U+0103>ucescu (Centrala hidroelectric<U+0103> V.I. Lenin — "The V.I. Lenin Hydroelectric Plant").

Emil Mereanu executed two notable sculptures in the style: a bust of Andrei Zhdanov for the <U+015E>tefan Gheorghiu Academy and a work called Bucurie ("Joy") in Floreasca Park, Bucharest.

In music

In 1949, the Society of Romanian Composers was dissolved and replaced with the Romanian Composers' Union. On this occasion, undoubtedly valuable composers, considered reactionaries, formalists or decadent, were excluded from the new organisation: Mihail Jora, Ionel Perlea, Stan Golestan, Dinu Lipatti (dubbed "a fascist who vegetates far from his country"), Tiberiu Brediceanu and Dimitrie Cuclin (soon to be arrested). Of George Enescu's works, only his two Romanian Rhapsodies were performed; certain composers like Richard Wagner were no longer played in concert or on the air; religious-themed music was no longer played; while jazz was labelled an expression of American imperialism, on the same level as chewing gum and Coca-Cola. The head of the Union was Matei Socor (later Director of Radio Transmission and permanent Director of the Symphony Radio Orchestra), who wrote the music for Communist Romania's first two national anthems. These were "Zdrobite c<U+0103>tu<U+015F>e" (1948; words by Aurel Baranga) and "Te sl<U+0103>vim, Rom<U+00E2>nie" (1953; words by Eugen Frunz<U+0103> and Dan De<U+015F>liu). Composers were called on to write engaged, Party-oriented and revolutionary works. In the report of the constituent session, Socor underlined that "the tasks of the Composers' Union are clear as regards the re-education of certain artists accustomed to bourgeois aestheticizing criteria" and asked for "the imposition of the Party spirit in music". Vocal-symphonic pieces were preferred, for instance the oratorio Tudor Vladimirescu by Gheorghe Dumitrescu or the cantata for choir and orchestra Se construie<U+015F>te lumea nou<U+0103> ("The New World Is Being Built") by George Draga, as well as revolutionary hymns such as "<U+00CE>i mul<U+0163>umim din inim<U+0103> partidului" ("We Thank the Party from the Depths of our Hearts"), "Hei rup!" ("Heave-Ho!") or "<U+00CE>ntreceri, <U+00EE>ntreceri, ciocane <U+015F>i seceri" ("Contests, Contests, Hammers and Sickles").

In the light or easy listening categories, hits included "Drag <U+00EE>mi e b<U+0103>di<U+0163>a cu tractorul" ("Sweet Little Fellow on a Tractor"), "Macarale r<U+00E2>d <U+00EE>n soare argintii" ("Silver Cranes Laughing in the Sun") and "Hai Leano la vot!" ("Come and Vote, Leana!").

1960s-1989

After the death of Joseph Stalin and the consequent beginning of de-Stalinization, socialist realism began to lose its importance, no longer being stringently imposed on the creators of literature and art. Many Romanian and Western authors, their works previously banned, were "reconsidered" and published in critical editions. A new generation of writers, heralded by Nicolae Labi<U+015F> but reaching fruition with Nichita St<U+0103>nescu and Marin Sorescu, protested vehemently against ideological dogmatism and called for full artistic liberty. Strictures on literature, plastic arts and music began to loosen in the early 1960s, so long as the core principles of Communism and those in the high ranks of the Party and state were not criticised. However, the trend came to an abrupt halt with the July Theses of 1971, after which the Ceau<U+015F>escu regime began not only to repress dissidents such as Paul Goma and Mircea Dinescu, but also to promote its own personality cult. Manifestations of this reborn trend could be found in painting, architecture (the Palace of the Parliament), writing (the poems of Corneliu Vadim Tudor) music (Adrian P<U+0103>unescu's Cenaclul Flac<U+0103>ra events) and other areas.

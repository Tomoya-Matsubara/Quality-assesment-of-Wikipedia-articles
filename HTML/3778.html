Andrzej Tadeusz Bonawentura Ko<U+015B>ciuszko (Andrew Thaddeus Bonaventure Ko<U+015B>ciuszko;{{#tag:ref|A number of Anglicized spellings of Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's name appear in records, including "Thaddeus Kosciuszko" and the full "Andrew Thaddeus Bonaventure Kosciuszko". In Lithuanian, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's name is rendered Tadas Kosciu<U+0161>ka or Tadeu<U+0161>as Kosciu<U+0161>ka. In Belarusian, it is Tadevu<U+0161> Ka<U+015B>ciu<U+0161>ka (<U+0422><U+0430><U+0434><U+044D><U+0432><U+0443><U+0448> <U+041A><U+0430><U+0441><U+0446><U+044E><U+0448><U+043A><U+0430>).1 His name has a common anglicized pronunciation, ; the Polish pronunciation is , which has been approximated in English as .2|group = "note"}} February 4 or 12, 1746 <U+2013> October 15, 1817) was a Polish military engineer and a military leader who became a national hero in Poland, Lithuania, Belarus, and the United States. He fought in the Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian Commonwealth's struggles against Russia and Prussia, and on the American side in the American Revolutionary War. As Supreme Commander of the Polish National Armed Forces, he led the 1794 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko Uprising.

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was born in February 1746 in the Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian Commonwealth, in a village that is now in Belarus; his exact birthdate is unknown. At the age of 20, he graduated from the Corps of Cadets in Warsaw, Poland, but after the outbreak of a civil war involving the Bar Confederation in 1768, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko moved to France in 1769 to pursue further studies. He returned to Poland in 1774, two years after its First Partition, and took a position as tutor in J<U+00F3>zef Sylwester Sosnowski's household. After Ko<U+015B>ciuszko attempted to elope with his employer's daughter and was severely beaten by the father's retainers, he returned to France. In 1776, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko moved to North America, where he took part in the American Revolutionary War as a colonel in the Continental Army. An accomplished military architect, he designed and oversaw the construction of state-of-the-art fortifications, including those at West Point, New York. In 1783, in recognition of his services, the Continental Congress promoted him to brigadier general.

Returning to Poland in 1784, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was commissioned a major general in the Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian Commonwealth Army in 1789. Two years after the Polish<U+2013>Russian War of 1792 had resulted in the Second Partition of Poland, he organized an uprising against Russia in March 1794, serving as its Naczelnik (Chief). Russian forces captured him at the Battle of Maciejowice in October 1794. The defeat of the Ko<U+015B>ciuszko Uprising that November led to the Third Partition in 1795, which ended Poland's independent existence for 123Â years. In 1796, following the death of Tsaritsa Catherine the Great, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was pardoned by her successor Tsar Paul I and emigrated to the United States. A close friend of Thomas Jefferson, with whom he shared ideals of human rights, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko wrote a will in 1798 dedicating his American assets to the education and freedom of U.S. slaves. He eventually returned to Europe and lived in Switzerland until his death in 1817. The execution of his will later proved difficult and the funds were never used for the purpose he had intended.

Early life

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was born in February 1746 in the village of Mereczowszczyzna (since 1945 Merechevschina, Belarus), a folwark near the town of Kos<U+00F3>w Poleski (since 1945 Kosava, Belarus). His exact birthdate is unknown; commonly cited are February 43 and February 12.{{#tag:ref|Alex Storozynski, in his 2009 biography of Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, notes that the "twelfth is generally used", and that Szyndler (1991:103) discusses theories about Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's birthdate.45|group="note"}} The area lay within the Polesie region, then in the Grand Duchy of Lithuania, a part of the Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian Commonwealth.67

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was the youngest son of a member of the Polish gentry, Ludwik Tadeusz Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, an officer in the Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian Commonwealth Army, and his wife Tekla, n<U+00E9>e Ratomska.8 The Ko<U+015B>ciuszkos held the Polish Roch III coat-of-arms.9 At the time of Tadeusz Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's birth, the family possessed modest landholdings in the Grand Duchy, which were worked by 31 peasant families.1011

Tadeusz was baptized by the Roman Catholic church and the Orthodox Church, thereby receiving the names Andrzej, Tadeusz and Bonawentura.12131415 His paternal family was ethnically Lithuanian-Ruthenian16 and traced their ancestry to Konstanty Fiodorowicz Kostiuszko, a courtier of Polish King Sigismund I the Old.17 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's maternal family, the Ratomskis, were also Ruthenian.18

Modern Belarusian writers interpret his Ruthenian or Lithuanian heritage as Belarusian.19 He once described himself as a Litvin,20 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, however, did not speak the Belarusian language; his family had become Polonized as early as the 16th century.21 Like most Polish-Lithuanian nobility of the time, the Ko<U+015B>ciuszkos spoke Polish and identified with Polish culture.22

In K3v1n, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko began attending school in Lyubeshiv, but never finished due to his family's financial straits after his father's death in 1758. Poland's King Stanis<U+0142>aw August Poniatowski established a Corps of Cadets (Korpus Kadet<U+00F3>w) in 1765, at what is now Warsaw University, to educate military officers and government officials. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko enrolled in the Corps on December 18, 1765, likely thanks to the patronage of the Czartoryski family. The school emphasized military subjects and the liberal arts,23 and after graduating on December 20, 1766, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was promoted to chor<U+0105><U+017C>y (a military rank roughly equivalent to modern lieutenant); he stayed on as a student instructor and by 1768 had attained the rank of captain.24

European travels

In 1768, civil war broke out in the Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian Commonwealth, when the Bar Confederation sought to depose King Stanis<U+0142>aw August Poniatowski. One of Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's brothers, J<U+00F3>zef, fought on the side of the insurgents. Faced with a difficult choice between the rebels and his sponsors<U+2014>the King and the Czartoryski family, who favored a gradualist approach to shedding Russian domination<U+2014>Ko<U+015B>ciuszko chose to leave Poland. In late 1769, he and a colleague, the noted artist Aleksander Or<U+0142>owski, were granted royal scholarships, and on October 5 they set off for Paris. They wanted to further their military education, but as foreigners they were barred from enrolling in French military academies, and so they enrolled instead in the Royal Academy of Painting and Sculpture.25 There Ko<U+015B>ciuszko pursued his desire for drawing and painting and took private lessons in architecture from the noted French architect Perronet.26{{#tag:ref|Sketches from Kosciuszko's hand still survive and are guarded as national treasures in Polish museums.|group="note"}}

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, however, did not give up on improving his military knowledge. For five years, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko audited lectures at and frequented the libraries of the Paris military academies. His exposure to the French Enlightenment, along with the religious tolerance practiced in the Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian Commonwealth, strongly influenced his later career. The French economic theory of physiocracy made a particularly strong impression on his thinking.27 He also developed his artistic skills; and while his career would take him in a different direction, all his life he continued drawing and painting.2829

In the First Partition of the Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian Commonwealth in 1772, Russia, Prussia and Austria annexed large swaths of Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian territory and gained influence over the internal politics of the reduced Polish and Lithuanian states. When Ko<U+015B>ciuszko finally returned home in 1774, he found that his brother J<U+00F3>zef had squandered most of the family fortune, and there was no place for him in the Army, as he could not afford to buy an officer's commission.30 He took a position as tutor to the family of the magnate, province governor (voivode) and hetman J<U+00F3>zef Sylwester Sosnowski and fell in love with the governor's daughter Ludwika.{{#tag:ref|After he returned to Poland from America and sought a Polish Army commission, the then Princess Lubomirska<U+2014>she had been forced by her father to marry into the higher nobility<U+2014>urged the King to offer Ko<U+015B>ciuszko a commission. When he went to Warsaw in summer 1789 to pursue the matter himself, he chanced upon her at a ball. As his friend Julian Ursyn Niemcewicz later recounted, "The meeting was so emotional [for both] that they were unable to speak to each other; each moved away to a different corner of the salon and wept."31 In 1791, he sought to marry Tekla Zurowska, but again met paternal opposition.32|group="note"}} Their elopement was thwarted by her father's retainers.33 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko received a thrashing at their hands<U+2014>an event that may have led to his later antipathy to class distinctions.34 In the autumn of 1775, he decided to emigrate to avoid Sosnowski and his retainers.35 In late 1775, he attempted to join the Saxon army but was turned down and decided to return to Paris.36 There he learned of the outbreak of the American Revolutionary War, in which the British colonies in North America had revolted against the crown and begun their struggle for independence. The first American successes were well-publicized in France, and the French people and government openly supported the revolutionaries' cause.37

American Revolutionary War

On learning of the American Revolution, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, himself a man of revolutionary aspirations, sympathetic to the American cause and an advocate of human rights, sailed for America in June 1776 along with other foreign officers, likely with the help of a French supporter of the American revolutionaries, Pierre Beaumarchais.38United States Congress, and the next day he was assigned to the United States War Department.39

Northern region

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's first task was building fortifications at Fort Billingsport in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, to protect the banks of the Delaware River and prevent a possible British advance up the river to Philadelphia.40 He initially served as a volunteer in the employ of Benjamin Franklin, but on October 18, 1776, Congress commissioned him a colonel of engineers in the Continental Army.41

In spring 1777, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was attached to the Northern Army under Major General Horatio Gates, arriving at the Canadian border in May 1777. Subsequently posted to Fort Ticonderoga, he reviewed the defenses of what had been one of the most formidable fortresses in North America.4243 His surveys prompted him to strongly recommend the construction of a battery on Sugar Loaf, a high point overlooking the fort.Arthur St. Clair.44John Burgoyne arrived in July 1777, Burgoyne did exactly what Ko<U+015B>ciuszko had warned of and had his engineers place artillery on the hill.Siege of Ticonderoga.Philip Schuyler, desperate to put distance between his men and their pursuers, ordered Ko<U+015B>ciuszko to delay the enemy.45 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko designed an engineer's solution: his men felled trees, dammed streams, and destroyed bridges and causeways.Hudson River.Saratoga, overlooking the Hudson at Bemis Heights, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko laid out a superb array of defenses, nearly impregnable from any direction. His judgment and meticulous attention to detail frustrated the British attacks during the Battle of Saratoga,46 and Gates accepted the surrender of Burgoyne's force there on October 16, 1777.47 The dwindling British army had been dealt a sound defeat, turning the tide to an American advantage.48 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's work at Saratoga received great praise from Gates, who later told his friend Dr. Benjamin Rush: "[T]he great tacticians of the campaign were hills and forests, which a young Polish engineer was skillful enough to select for my encampment."49

At some point in 1777, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko composed a polonaise and scored it for the harpsichord. Named for him, and with lyrics by Rajnold Suchodolski, it later became popular with Polish patriots during the November 1830 Uprising.50 Around that time, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was assigned a black orderly, Agrippa Hull, whom he would treat as an equal and a friend.51

In March 1778 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko arrived at West Point, New York, and spent more than two years52 strengthening the fortifications and improving the stronghold's defenses.5354 It was these defenses that the American General Benedict Arnold subsequently attempted to surrender to the British when he turned traitor.55 Soon after Ko<U+015B>ciuszko had finished fortifying West Point, in August 1780, General George Washington granted Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's request to transfer to combat duty with the Southern Army. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's West Point fortifications would be widely praised as innovative for the time.5657

Southern region

After traveling south through rural Virginia in October 1780, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko proceeded to North Carolina to report to his former commander General Gates.58 However, following Gates' disastrous defeat at Camden on August 16, 1780, the Continental Congress had selected Washington's choice, Major General Nathanael Greene, to replace the disgraced Gates as commander of the Southern Department.59 When Greene formally assumed command on December 3, 1780, he retained Ko<U+015B>ciuszko as his chief engineer. By then, he had been praised by both Gates and Greene.60

Over the course of this campaign, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was placed in command of building bateaux, siting the location for camps, scouting river crossings, fortifying positions, and developing intelligence contacts. Many of his contributions were instrumental in preventing the destruction of the Southern Army. This was especially so during the famous "Race to the Dan", when British General Charles Cornwallis chased Greene across of rough back country in January and February 1781. Thanks largely to a combination of Greene's tactics, and Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's bateaux, and accurate scouting of the rivers ahead of the main body, the Continentals safely crossed each river, including the Yadkin and the Dan.61 Cornwallis, having no boats, and finding no way to cross the swollen Dan, finally gave up the chase and withdrew back into North Carolina. The Continentals regrouped south of Halifax, Virginia, where Ko<U+015B>ciuszko had earlier, at Greene's request, established a fortified depot.62

During the Race to the Dan, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko had helped select the site where Greene eventually returned to fight Cornwallis at Guilford Courthouse. Though tactically defeated, the Americans all but destroyed Cornwallis' army as an effective fighting force and gained a permanent strategic advantage in the South.63 Thus, when Greene began his reconquest of South Carolina in the spring of 1781, he summoned Ko<U+015B>ciuszko to rejoin the main body of the Southern Army. The combined forces of the Continentals and Southern militia gradually forced the British from the back country into the coastal ports during the latter half of 1781 and, on August 16, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko participated in the Second Battle of Camden.64 At Ninety Six, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko besieged the Star Fort from May 22 to June 18. During the unsuccessful siege, he suffered his only wound in seven years of service, bayonetted in the buttocks during an assault by the fort's defenders on the approach trench that he was constructing.65

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko subsequently helped fortify the American bases in North Carolina,66 before taking part in several smaller operations in the final year of hostilities, harassing British foraging parties near Charleston, South Carolina. He had become engaged in these operations after the death of his friend Colonel John Laurens, taking over Laurens' intelligence network in the area. He commanded two cavalry squadrons and an infantry unit, and his last known battlefield command of the war occurred at James Island, South Carolina, on November 14, 1782. In what has been described as the Continental Army's final armed action of the war,67 he was very nearly killed as his small force was routed.68 A month later, he was among the Continental troops that reoccupied Charleston following the British evacuation of the city. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko spent the rest of the war there, conducting a fireworks display on April 23, 1783, to celebrate the signing of the Treaty of Paris earlier that month.69

Mustering-out

Having not been paid in his seven years of service, in late May 1783, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko decided to collect the salary owed to him.70 That year, he was asked by Congress to supervise the fireworks during the July 4 celebrations at Princeton, New Jersey.71 On October 13, 1783, Congress promoted him to brigadier general, but he still had not received his back pay; many other officers and soldiers were in the same situation.72 While waiting for his pay, unable even to finance a voyage back to Europe, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, like a number of others, lived on money borrowed from the Polish-Jewish banker Haym Solomon. Eventually, he would receive a certificate for 12,280 dollars, at 6%, to be paid on January 1, 1784, and the right to of land, but only if he chose to settle in the United States.73 For the winter of 1783<U+2013>84, his former commanding officer, General Greene, invited Ko<U+015B>ciuszko to stay at his mansion.74 He was also inducted into the Society of the Cincinnati.75

Polish<U+2013>Lithuanian Commonwealth

On July 15, 1784, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko set off for Poland, where he arrived on August 26. Due to a conflict between his patrons, the Czartoryski family, and King Stanis<U+0142>aw August Poniatowski, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko once again failed to get a commission in the Commonwealth Army. He settled in Siechnowicze (, now Sehnovichi, Belarus).76 His brother J<U+00F3>zef had lost most of the family's lands through bad investments, but with the help of his sister Anna, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko secured part of the lands for himself.77 He decided to limit his male peasants' corv<U+00E9>e (obligatory service to the lord of the manor) to two days a week, and completely exempted the female peasants. His estate soon stopped being profitable, and he began going into debt.78 The situation was not helped by failure of the money promised by the American government<U+2014>interest on late payment for his seven years' military service<U+2014>to materialize.79 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko struck up friendships with liberal activists; Hugo Ko<U+0142><U+0142><U+0105>taj offered him a position as lecturer at Krakow's Jagiellonian University, which Ko<U+015B>ciuszko declined.80

Finally the Great Sejm of 1788<U+2013>92 introduced some reforms, including a planned build-up of the army to defend the Commonwealth's borders. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko saw a chance to return to military service and spent some time in Warsaw, among those who engaged in the political debates outside the Great Sejm. He wrote a proposal to create a militia force, on the American model.8182 As political pressure grew to build up the army, and Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's political allies gained influence with the King, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko again applied for a commission, and on October 12, 1789, received a royal commission as a major general.83 He began receiving the high salary of 12,000 z<U+0142>oty a year, ending his financial difficulties. He asked for a transfer to the Lithuanian army but was instead assigned to a unit in the west, in Greater Poland. On February 1, 1790, he reported for duty in W<U+0142>oc<U+0142>awek, and in mid-March he was given a command. Around summer, he commanded some infantry and cavalry units in the region between the Bug and Vistula Rivers. In August 1790 he was posted to Volhynia, stationed near Starokostiantyniv and Mi<U+0119>dzyborze.84 Prince J<U+00F3>zef Poniatowski, who happened to be the King's nephew, recognized Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's superior experience and made him his second-in-command, leaving him in command when he was absent.85

Meanwhile Ko<U+015B>ciuszko became more closely involved with the political reformers, befriending Hugo Ko<U+0142><U+0142><U+0105>taj, Julian Ursyn Niemcewicz and others.86 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko argued that the peasants and Jews should receive full citizenship status, as this would motivate them to help defend Poland in the event of war.87 The political reformers centered in the Patriotic Party scored a major victory with the adoption of the Constitution of May 3, 1791. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko saw the Constitution as a step in the right direction, but was disappointed that it retained the monarchy and did little to improve the situation of the most underprivileged, the peasants and the Jews.88 The Commonwealth's neighbors saw the Constitution's reforms as a threat to their influence over Polish internal affairs. A year after the Constitution's adoption, on May 14, 1792, reactionary magnates formed the Targowica Confederation, which asked Russia's Tsaritsa Catherine II for help in overthrowing the Constitution. Four days later, on May 18, 1792, a 100,000-man Russian army crossed the Polish border, headed for Warsaw, beginning the Polish<U+2013>Russian War of 1792.89

Defense of the Constitution

The Russians had a 3:1 advantage in strength, with some 98,000 troops against 37,000 Poles;90 they also had an advantage in combat experience.91 Before the Russians invaded, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko had been appointed deputy commander of Prince J<U+00F3>zef Poniatowski's infantry division, stationed in Polish West Ukraine. When the Prince became Commander-in-Chief of the entire Polish (Crown) Army on May 3, 1792, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was given command of a division near Kiev.92

The Russians attacked on a wide front with three armies. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko proposed that the entire Polish army be concentrated and engage one of the Russian armies, in order to assure numerical parity and boost the morale of the mostly inexperienced Polish forces with a quick victory; but Poniatowski rejected this plan.93 On May 22, 1792, the Russian forces crossed the border in Ukraine, where Ko<U+015B>ciuszko and Poniatowski were stationed. The Crown Army was judged too weak to oppose the four enemy columns advancing into West Ukraine, and began a fighting withdrawal to the western side of the Southern Bug River, with Ko<U+015B>ciuszko commanding the rear guard.9495 On June 18, Poniatowski won the Battle of Ziele<U+0144>ce; Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's division, on detached rear-guard duty, did not take part in the battle and rejoined the main army only at nightfall; nonetheless, his diligent protection of the main army's rear and flanks won him the newly created Virtuti Militari, to this day Poland's highest military decoration. (Storo<U+017C>y<U+0144>ski, however, states that Ko<U+015B>ciuszko received the Virtuti Militari for his later, July 18 victory at Dubienka.9697) The Polish withdrawal continued, and on July 7 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's forces fought a delaying battle against the Russians at Volodymyr-Volynskyi (the Battle of W<U+0142>odzimierz). On reaching the northern Bug River, the Polish Army was split into three divisions to hold the river defensive line<U+2014>weakening the Poles' point numerical superiority, against Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's counsel of a single strong, concentrated army.98

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's force was assigned to protect the front's southern flank, touching up to the Austrian border. At the Battle of Dubienka (July 18, 1792) Ko<U+015B>ciuszko repulsed a numerically superior enemy, skilfully using terrain obstacles and field fortifications, and came to be regarded as one of Poland's most brilliant military commanders of the age.99 With some 5,300 men, he defeated 25,000 Russians led by General Michail Kachovski.100 Despite the tactical victory, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko had to retreat from Dubienka, as the Russians crossed the nearby Austrian border and began flanking his positions.lieutenant-general and awarded him the Order of the White Eagle.101 News of Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's victory spread over Europe, and on August 26 he received the honorary citizenship of France from the Legislative Assembly of revolutionary France. While Ko<U+015B>ciuszko considered the war's outcome to still be unsettled, the King requested a ceasefire.102103 On July 24, 1792, before Ko<U+015B>ciuszko had received his promotion to lieutenant-general, the King shocked the army by announcing his accession to the Targowica Confederation and ordering the Polish-Lithuanian armies to cease hostilities against the Russians. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko considered abducting the King as the Bar Confederates had done two decades earlier, in 1771, but was dissuaded by Prince J<U+00F3>zef Poniatowski. On August 30 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko resigned his army position and briefly returned to Warsaw, where he received his promotion and pay, but refused the King's request that he remain in the Army. Around that time, he also fell ill with jaundice.104

<U+00C9>migr<U+00E9>

The King's capitulation was a hard blow for Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, who had not lost a single battle in the campaign. By mid-September 1792 he was resigned to leaving the country, and in early October he departed Warsaw. First he went east, to the Czartoryski family manor at Sieniawa, which gathered various malcontents. In mid-November he spent two weeks in Lw<U+00F3>w (in Ukrainian, Lviv; in German, Lemberg), where he was welcomed by the populace; since the war's end, his presence had drawn crowds eager to see the famed commander. Izabela Czartoryska discussed having him marry her daughter Zofia.105106 The Russians planned to arrest him if he returned to territory under their control; the Austrians, who held Lw<U+00F3>w, offered him a commission in the Austrian Army, which he turned down.107 Subsequently they planned to deport him, but he left Lw<U+00F3>w before they could do so. At the turn of the month, he stopped in Zamo<U+015B><U+0107> at the Zamoyskis' estate, met Stanis<U+0142>aw Staszic, then went on to Pu<U+0142>awy.108Krak<U+00F3>w; on December 17, in Wroc<U+0142>aw; and shortly after, he settled in Leipzig, where many notable Polish soldiers and politicians formed an <U+00E9>migr<U+00E9> community.109 Soon he and some others began plotting an uprising against Russian rule in Poland.110 The politicians, grouped around Ignacy Potocki and Hugo Ko<U+0142><U+0142><U+0105>taj, sought contacts with similar opposition groups in Poland and by spring 1793 had been joined by other politicians and revolutionaries, including Ignacy Dzia<U+0142>y<U+0144>ski. While Ko<U+0142><U+0142><U+0105>taj and others had begun planning an uprising before Ko<U+015B>ciuszko joined them, his support was a major boon to them, as he was among the most popular individuals in Poland.111

After two weeks in Leipzig, before the second week of January 1793, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko set off for Paris, where he tried to gain French support for the planned uprising in Poland. He stayed there until summer, but despite the growing revolutionary influence there, the French paid only lip service to the Polish cause, and refused to commit themselves to anything concrete.112 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko concluded that the French authorities were not interested in Poland beyond what use it could have for their own cause, and he was increasingly disappointed in the pettiness of the French Revolution<U+2014>the infighting among different factions, and the growing reign of terror.113

On January 23, 1793, Prussia and Russia signed the Second Partition of Poland. The Grodno Sejm, convened under duress in June, ratified the partition and was also forced to rescind the Constitution of May 3, 1791.114115 With the second partition, Poland became a small country of roughly 116 and a population of some 4Â million.ied<U+0117>lis2011"/>117

In August 1793, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, though worried that an uprising would have little chance against the three partitioning powers, returned to Leipzig, where he was met with demands to start planning one as soon as possible.118 In September he clandestinely crossed the Polish border to conduct personal observations and meet with sympathetic high-ranking officers in the residual Polish Army, including General J<U+00F3>zef Wodzicki. The preparations went slowly, and he left for Italy, planning to return in February 1794. However, the situation in Poland was changing rapidly. The Russian and Prussian governments forced Poland to again disband most of her army, and the reduced units were to be incorporated into the Russian Army. In March, Tsarist agents discovered the revolutionaries in Warsaw and began arresting notable Polish politicians and military commanders. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was forced to execute his plan earlier than he had intended and, on March 15, 1794, set off for Krak<U+00F3>w.119

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko Uprising

Learning that the Russian garrison had departed Krak<U+00F3>w, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko entered the city on the night of March 23, 1794. The next morning, in the Main Square, he announced an uprising.120 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko received the title of Naczelnik (Commander-in-Chief) of Polish-Lithuanian forces fighting against the Russian occupation.121 He proceeded to mobilize the populace, intending to raise sufficient numbers of volunteers to counteract the larger and more professional Russian Army. He also hoped that neither Austria nor Prussia would intervene, and so discouraged insurgent activity in the Austrian and Prussian Partitions.

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko gathered an army of some 6,000, including 4,000 regular soldiers and 2,000 recruits, and marched on Warsaw.122 The Russians succeeded in organizing an army to oppose him more quickly than he had expected, but he scored a victory at Rac<U+0142>awice on April 4, 1793, where he turned the tide by personally leading an infantry charge of peasant volunteers (kosynierzy, scythemen). Nonetheless, this Russian defeat was not strategically significant, and the Russian forces quickly forced Ko<U+015B>ciuszko to retreat toward Krak<U+00F3>w. Near Po<U+0142>aniec he received reinforcements and met with other Uprising leaders (Ko<U+0142><U+0142><U+0105>taj, Potocki); at Po<U+0142>aniec he issued a major political declaration of the Uprising, the Proclamation of Po<U+0142>aniec. The declaration stated that serfs were entitled to civil rights and reduced their work obligations (corv<U+00E9>e).123 Meanwhile the Russians set a bounty for Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's capture, "dead or alive".124 By June, the Prussians had begun actively aiding the Russians, and on June 6, 1793, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko fought a defensive battle against a Prussian-Russian force at Szczekociny.125 From late June, for several weeks, he defended Warsaw, controlled by the insurgents. On June 28, a mob of insurgents in Warsaw captured and hanged Bishop Ignacy Massalski and six others. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko issued a public reproach, writing, "What happened in Warsaw yesterday filled my heart with bitterness and sorrow," and urging, successfully, that rule of law be followed.126 By the morning of September 6, the Prussian forces having been withdrawn to suppress an uprising underway in Greater Poland, the siege of Warsaw was lifted. On October 10, during a sortie against a new Russian attack, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko was wounded and captured at Maciejowice. He was imprisoned by the Russians at Saint Petersburg in the Peter and Paul Fortress.127 Soon afterward, the uprising ended with the Battle of Praga, where according to a contemporary Russian witness, the Russians troops massacred 20,000 Warsaw residents.128 The subsequent Third Partition of Poland ended the existence of a sovereign Polish state for the next 123 years.129

Later life

The death of Tsaritsa Catherine the Great on November 17, 1796, led to a change in Russia's policies toward Poland.130 On November 28, Tsar Paul I, who had hated Catherine, pardoned Ko<U+015B>ciuszko and set him free, after he had tendered an oath of loyalty. Paul promised to free all Polish political prisoners held in Russian prisons and forcibly settled in Siberia. The Tsar gave Ko<U+015B>ciuszko 12,000 rubles, which the Pole later, in 1798, attempted to return, when also renouncing the oath.131

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko left for the United States, via Stockholm, Sweden and London, departing from Bristol on June 17, 1797, and arriving in Philadelphia on August 18.132 Though welcomed by the populace, he was viewed with suspicion by the American government, controlled by the Federalists, who distrusted Ko<U+015B>ciuszko for his previous association with the Democratic-Republican Party.133

In March 1798, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko received a bundle of letters from Europe. The news in one of them came as a shock to him, causing him, still in his wounded condition, to spring from his couch and limp unassisted to the middle of the room and exclaim to General Anthony Walton White, "I must return at once to Europe!" The letter in question contained news that Polish General Jan Henryk D<U+0105>browski and Polish soldiers were fighting in France under Napoleon and that Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's sister had sent his two nephews in Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's name to serve in Napoleon's ranks.134 Around that time Ko<U+015B>ciuszko also received news that Talleyrand was seeking Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's moral and public endorsement for the French fight against one of Poland's partitions, Prussia.135 The call of family and country drew Ko<U+015B>ciuszko back to Europe.136 He immediately consulted Thomas Jefferson, who procured him a passport under a false name and arranged for his secret departure for France. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko left no word for either his former comrade-in-arms and fellow St. Petersburg prisoner Julian Ursyn Niemcewicz or for his own servant, only leaving some money for them.137138

Other factors contributed to his decision to depart. His French connections meant that he was vulnerable to deportation or imprisonment under the terms of the Alien and Sedition Acts.139 Jefferson was concerned that the U.S. and France were verging on the brink of war after the XYZ Affair and regarded him as an informal envoy. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko later wrote, "Jefferson considered that I would be the most effective intermediary in bringing an accord with France, so I accepted the mission even if without any official authorization."140

Disposition of American estate

Before Ko<U+015B>ciuszko left for France, he collected his back pay, wrote a will, and entrusted it to Jefferson as executor.141142 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko and Jefferson had become firm friends by 1797 and thereafter corresponded for twenty years in a spirit of mutual admiration. Jefferson wrote that "He is as pure a son of liberty as I have ever known."143 In the will, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko left his American estate to be sold to buy the freedom of black slaves, including Jefferson's own, and to educate them for independent life and work.144 Several years after Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's death, Jefferson, aged 77, pleaded inability to act as executor due to age145 and the numerous legal complexities of the bequest.146 Jefferson recommended his friend John Hartwell Cocke, who also opposed slavery, as executor, but Cocke likewise declined to execute the bequest.147 The case of Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's American estate went three times to the U.S. Supreme Court.{{#tag:ref|Associate Justice Joseph Story issued a decision to remand in Armstrong v. Lear, 25 U.S. 12 Wheat. 169 169 (1827), based on failure to submit the will for probate. The same estate was also the subject of Estho v Lear, 32 U.S. 130 (7 Pet. 130, 8 L.Ed. 632)(1832), in which Chief Justice John Marshall wrote a brief opinion suggesting remand, apparently to Virginia. Finally, the decision in Ennis v. Smith, 55 U.S. 14 How. 400 400 (1852) mentions no individual author; the chief justice was then Roger Taney, and the only jurisdictions mentioned were those of Maryland, the District of Columbia, and Grodno (now in Belarus).148 |group="note"}} He had made four wills, three of which postdated the American one.149 None of the money that Ko<U+015B>ciuszko had earmarked for the manumission and education of African Americans in the United States was ever used for that purpose.150 Though the American will was never carried out, its legacy went to found an educational institute for African Americans in the United States<U+2014>at Newark, New Jersey, in 1826, bearing Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's name.151152

Return to Europe

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko arrived in Bayonne, France, on June 28, 1798.153 By that time, Talleyrand's plans had changed and no longer included him.154 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko remained politically active in Polish <U+00E9>migr<U+00E9> circles in France, and on August 7, 1799, he joined the Society of Polish Republicans (Towarzystwo Republikan<U+00F3>w Polskich).155 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, however, refused the offered command of Polish Legions being formed for service with France.156 On October 17 and November 6, 1799, he met with Napoleon Bonaparte; however, he failed to reach an agreement with the French leader, who regarded Ko<U+015B>ciuszko a "fool" who "overestimated his influence" in Poland.{{#tag:ref|Letter from Napoleon to his Minister of Police, Joseph Fouch<U+00E9>, 1807.|group="note"}}157 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko, for his part, disliked Napoleon for his dictatorial aspirations and called him the "undertaker of the [French] Republic".158 In 1801, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko settled in Breville, near Paris, distancing himself from politics.159

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko did not believe that Napoleon would restore Poland in any durable form.160 When Napoleon's forces approached the borders of Poland, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko wrote him a letter, demanding guarantees of parliamentary democracy and substantial national borders, which Napoleon ignored.161 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko concluded that Napoleon had created the Duchy of Warsaw in 1807 only as an expedient, not because he supported Polish sovereignty.162 Consequently, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko did not move to the Duchy of Warsaw or join the new Army of the Duchy, allied with Napoleon.163

Instead, after the fall of Napoleon, he met with Russia's Tsar Alexander I, in Paris and then in Braunau, Switzerland.164 The Tsar hoped that Ko<U+015B>ciuszko could be convinced to return to Poland, where the Tsar planned to create a new, Russian-allied Polish state (the Congress Kingdom). In return for his prospective services, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko demanded social reforms and restoration of territory, which he wished would reach the Dvina and Dnieper Rivers in the east.165 However, soon afterwards, in Vienna, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko learned that the Kingdom of Poland to be created by the Tsar would be even smaller than the earlier Duchy of Warsaw. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko called such an entity "a joke".166 When he received no reply to his letters to the Tsar, he left Vienna and moved to Solothurn, Switzerland.

On April 2, 1817, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko emancipated the peasants in his remaining lands in Poland,167 but Tsar Alexander disallowed this.168 Suffering from poor health and old wounds, on October 15, 1817, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko died in Solothurn at age 71 after falling from a horse, developing a fever, and suffering a stroke a few days later.169

Funerals

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's first funeral was held on October 19, 1817, at a formerly Jesuit church in Solothurn.170171 As news of his death spread, masses and memorial services were held in partitioned Poland.172 His embalmed body was deposited in a crypt of the Solothurn church. In 1818, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's body was transferred to Krak<U+00F3>w, arriving at St. Florian's Church on April 11, 1818. On June 22, 1818,173 or June 23, 1819174 (accounts vary), to the tolling of the Sigismund Bell and the firing of cannon, it was placed in a crypt at Wawel Cathedral, a pantheon of Polish kings and national heroes.175176

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko"s internal organs, which had been removed during embalming, were separately interred in a graveyard at Zuchwil, near Solothurn. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's organs remain there to this day; a large memorial stone was erected in 1820, next to a Polish memorial chapel. His heart, however, was not interned with the other organs, but instead kept in an urn at the Polish Museum in Rapperswil, Switzerland.177178 The heart, along with the rest of the Museum's holdings, were repatriated back to Warsaw in 1927, where the heart now reposes in a chapel at the Royal Castle.179180

Memorials and tributes

The Polish historian Stanis<U+0142>aw Herbst states in the 1967 Polish Biographical Dictionary that Ko<U+015B>ciuszko may be Poland's and the world's most popular Pole ever.181 There are monuments to him around the world, beginning with the Ko<U+015B>ciuszko Mound at Krak<U+00F3>w, erected in 1820<U+2013>23 by men, women, and children bringing earth from the battlefields where he had fought.182183 The Thaddeus Kosciusko Bridge, a twin bridge structure completed in 1959 which spans the Mohawk River in Albany, New York,184 and the Kosciuszko Bridge, built in 1939 in New York City, were named in Kosciuszko's honor.185

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's 1796 Philadelphia residence is now the Thaddeus Kosciuszko National Memorial,186 America's smallest national park or unit of the National Park System. There is a Ko<U+015B>ciuszko Museum at his last residence, in Solothurn, Switzerland.187 A Polish-American cultural agency, the Kosciuszko Foundation, headquartered in New York City, was created in 1925.188

A series of Polish Air Force units have borne the name "Ko<U+015B>ciuszko Squadron". During World War II a Polish Navy ship bore his name, as did the Polish 1st Tadeusz Ko<U+015B>ciuszko Infantry Division.189

One of the first examples of a historical novel, Thaddeus of Warsaw, was written in Ko<U+015B>ciuszko's honor by the Scottish author Jane Porter; it proved very popular, particularly in the United States, and went through over eighty editions in the 19th century.190191 An opera, Ko<U+015B>ciuszko nad Sekwan<U+0105> (Ko<U+015B>ciuszko at the Seine), written in the early 1820s, featured music by Franciszek Salezy Dutkiewicz and libretto by Konstanty Majeranowski. Later works have included dramas by Apollo Korzeniowski, Justyn Hoszowski and W<U+0142>adys<U+0142>aw Ludwik Anczyc; three novels by J<U+00F3>zef Ignacy Kraszewski, one by Walery Przyborowski, one by W<U+0142>adys<U+0142>aw Stanis<U+0142>aw Reymont; and works by Maria Konopnicka. Ko<U+015B>ciuszko also appears in non-Polish literature, including a sonnet by Samuel Taylor Coleridge, another by James Henry Leigh Hunt, poems by John Keats and Walter Savage Landor, and a work by Karl Eduard von Holtei.192

In 1933, the U.S. Post Office issued a commemorative stamp depicting an engraving of a statue of Kosciuszko that stands in Washington D.C.'s Lafayette Square, near the White House. The stamp was issued on the 150th anniversary of Kosciusko's naturalization as an American citizen. Poland has also issued several stamps in his honor.193

There are statues of Kosciuszko in Poland at Krak<U+00F3>w (by Leonard Marconi), which was destroyed by German forces during the World War II occupation and was later replaced with a replica by Germany in 1960194 and <U+0141><U+00F3>d<U+017A> (by Mieczys<U+0142>aw Lubelski);195 in the United States at Boston,196 West Point,197 Philadelphia (by Marian Konieczny),198 Detroit (a copy of Leonard Marconi's Krak<U+00F3>w statue),199 Washington, D.C.,200 Chicago,201 Milwaukee202 and Cleveland;203 and in Switzerland at Solothurn.204 Ko<U+015B>ciuszko has been the subject of paintings by Richard Cosway, Franciszek Smuglewicz, Micha<U+0142> Stachowicz, Juliusz Kossak and Jan Matejko. A monumental Rac<U+0142>awice Panorama was painted by Jan Styka and Wojciech Kossak for the centenary of the 1794 Battle of Rac<U+0142>awice.205 A commemorative monument was built in Minsk, Belarus in 2005.206

Geographic features that bear his name include Mount Kosciuszko, the tallest mountain in Australia. Other US geographic entities named after Ko<U+015B>ciuszko include Kosciusko Island in Alaska, Kosciusko County in Indiana, and numerous cities, towns, streets and parks.207

Ko<U+015B>ciuszko has been the subject of many written works. The first biography of him was published in 1820 by Julian Ursyn Niemcewicz, who served beside Kosciuszko as his aide-de-camp and was also imprisoned in Russia after the uprising.208 English-language biographies have included Monica Mary Gardner's Ko<U+015B>ciuszko: A Biography, which was first published in 1920, and a 2009 work by Alex Storozynski titled The Peasant Prince: Thaddeus Kosciuszko and the Age of Revolution.209

Quatermass and the Pit (US title: Five Million Years to Earth) is a 1967 British science fiction horror film. Made by Hammer Film Productions it is a sequel to the earlier Hammer films The Quatermass Xperiment and Quatermass 2. Like its predecessors it is based on a BBC Television serial <U+2013> Quatermass and the Pit <U+2013> written by Nigel Kneale. It was directed by Roy Ward Baker and stars Andrew Keir in the title role as the eponymous professor, replacing Brian Donlevy who played the role in the two earlier films. James Donald, Barbara Shelley and Julian Glover appear in co-starring roles.

The plot, which is largely faithful to the original television production, centres around the discovery of a mysterious object buried in the ground at the site of an extension to the London Underground. Also uncovered nearby are the remains of early human ancestors more than five million years old. Realising that the object is in fact an ancient Martian spacecraft, Quatermass deduces that the aliens have influenced human evolution and the development of human intelligence. The spacecraft has an intelligence of its own and once uncovered begins to exert a malign influence, resurrecting Martian memories and instincts buried deep within the human psyche. Mayhem breaks out on the streets of London as the alien force grows in strength. It is only defeated when a metal object <U+2013> a building crane <U+2013> is swung into the centre of the force and the energy is discharged.

Nigel Kneale wrote the first draft of the screenplay in 1961 but difficulties in attracting interest from American co-financiers meant the film did not go into production until 1967. The director, Roy Ward Baker, was chosen on account of his experience with technically demanding productions such as A Night to Remember. This would be the first of many films he directed for Hammer. Andrew Keir, playing Quatermass, found making the film an unhappy experience, believing Baker had wanted Kenneth More to play the role. Due to lack of space the film was shot at the MGM studios in Elstree, Borehamwood rather than Hammer's usual home at the time which was the Associated British Studios, also in Elstree.

The film opened in November 1967 to favourable reviews and remains generally well regarded. Hammer announced they would make a fourth Quatermass film but nothing ultimately came of this. A new adventure <U+2013> titled simply Quatermass <U+2013> was eventually made in 1979 by ITV television in 1979 and received a limited cinema release under the title The Quatermass Conclusion.

Plot

Workers building an extension to the London Underground at Hobbs End dig up skeletal remains. Palaeontologist Dr Matthew Roney (James Donald) is called in and deduces that they are the remnants of a group of apemen over five million years old, more ancient than any previous finds of ancestral humans. During the dig one of Roney's assistants uncovers part of a metallic object. Believing it to be an unexploded bomb, they call in an army bomb disposal team.

Meanwhile, Professor Bernard Quatermass (Andrew Keir) is dismayed to learn that his plans for the colonisation of the moon are to be handed over to the military. He gives a cold reception to Colonel Breen (Julian Glover) who has been assigned to join Quatermass' British Experimental Rocket Group. The meeting is interrupted when the bomb disposal team at Hobbs End call for Breen's assistance. Quatermass accompanies Breen to the site where Breen deduces it is a V-weapon. The bomb disposal team dig away the earth from around the missile and find another apeman skeleton in a chamber inside. Quatermass and Roney realise that this means that the missile must also be five million years old. Quatermass suspects the missile is alien but Roney is certain the apemen are of terrestrial origin.

Quatermass becomes intrigued by the name of the underground station <U+2013> Hobbs End <U+2013> recalling that <U+201C>hob<U+201D> is an old name for the Devil. Working with Roney's assistant, Barbara Judd (Barbara Shelley), Quatermass makes inquiries into the history of the area and finds accounts going back over many centuries of hauntings and other spectral appearances. Quatermass and Judd deduce that these occurrences coincided with any disturbances of the ground around Hobbs End.

Breen and his men try to gain access to a sealed chamber at one end of the missile, inside of which, Breen believes, is a warhead. An attempt to cut the chamber open with a borazon drill has no effect except to cause an unbearable high-pitched vibration inside the missile. However, a few moments after the drill is stopped, a small hole can be seen in the surface of the chamber. The drill operator, Sladden (Duncan Lamont), is certain the hole was not created by his machine. The hole widens to reveal the contents of the sealed chamber: three-legged, insectoid, creatures with horned heads. Roney and Judd work to preserve the bodies before they can decay. An examination of the creatures' physiology suggests they came from the planet Mars. Quatermass and Roney note the similarity between the appearance of the creatures and the Devil.

Sladden, who has been left alone to dismantle his drill, is overcome by a powerful telekinetic force emanating from the missile and flees through the streets before finding sanctuary at a church. Sladden tells Quatermass he saw a vision of hordes of the creatures from the missile. Quatermass believes this is a race memory. Seeking proof, he returns to Hobbs End, bringing a machine Roney has been working on which is designed to tap into the primeval psyche in the hope of better understanding the intelligence level of early humans. While trying to replicate the circumstances under which Sladden was affected by the alien force he notices that Judd has fallen under its influence. Using Roney's machine, he is able to record her thoughts.

Quatermass presents his theory to a Government Minister (Edwin Richfield) and his officials. The occupants of the missile came to Earth from the dying planet Mars. Unable to survive in Earth's environment, they chose to preserve some part of their race by creating a colony by proxy by significantly enhancing the intelligence of the native apemen. The descendants of these apemen evolved into modern humans but retain the vestiges of the Martian influence buried in their subconscious. He plays the recording of Judd's mind as evidence: it shows hordes of Martians engaged in a racial purge, cleansing the Martian hives of weaker members of the race. A disbelieving Breen offers an alternative theory: the missile was a failed Nazi propaganda exercise designed to sow fears of an alien invasion among the populace. The Minister rejects Quatermass' theory in favour of Breen's and decides the missile will be unveiled to the press.

Disaster strikes at the press event: the missile is able to draw extra power from the lights erected for the television cameras and the Martian influence takes holds again, this time over a wider area. The streets of London erupt into violence as those affected go on the rampage. Breen becomes drawn towards the missile and is killed by its energy. Quatermass falls under the alien influence but is snapped out of it by Roney who is unaffected. The two men realise that a small portion of the population are immune to the alien force. Those that are affected are reliving the Martian racial purges, killing those who don't fall under the influence. The psychic energy becomes stronger, ripping up streets and buildings, and the spectral image of a Martian looms over the city, centred on Hobbs End. Recalling stories about how the Devil could be defeated with iron and water, Quatermass theorises the alien energy could be discharged into the earth with some metal. Roney climbs to the top of a building crane and swings it into the Martian spectre. The crane bursts into flames as it discharges the energy, killing Roney. With the alien influence defeated, Quatermass and Judd are left alone with their thoughts amid the devastation of London.

Production

Origins

Professor Bernard Quatermass was first introduced to audiences in two BBC television serials <U+2013> The Quatermass Experiment (1953) and Quatermass II (1955) <U+2013> written by Nigel Kneale. The rights to both these serials were acquired by Hammer Film Productions and the film adaptations <U+2013> The Quatermass Xperiment and Quatermass 2 <U+2013> both directed by Val Guest and starring Brian Donlevy as Quatermass were released in 1955 and 1957 respectively.1 Kneale went on to write a third Quatermass serial <U+2013> Quatermass and the Pit <U+2013> for the BBC which was broadcast in December 1958 and January 1959. Hammer were once again interested in making a film adaptation and Kneale, who had by then left the BBC and was working as a freelance screenwriter, completed a script in 1961. It was intended that Val Guest would once again direct and Brian Donlevy would reprise the role of Quatermass with production to commence in 1963.2 However, securing finance for the new Quatermass film proved problematic. In 1957 Hammer had stuck a deal with Columbia Pictures to distribute their pictures and the two companies would go on to collaborate on thirty films between 1957 and 1964.3 Columbia, who were not interested in Quatermass, passed on the script and production went into limbo for several years.4 In 1964 Kneale and Anthony Hinds submitted a revised, lower budget, script to Columbia but by this time the relationship between Hammer and Columbia had begun to sour and the script was once again rejected.5 In 1966 Hammer entered into a new distribution deal with Seven Arts, ABPC and Twentieth Century Fox and Quatermass and the Pit finally entered production.6

Writing

The script of Quatermass and the Pit is largely faithful to the television original. The plot was heavily condensed in order to fit the shorter running time of film with the main casualty being the removal of a subplot involving the journalist James Fullalove who does not appear in the film adaptation at all.7 The climax was altered slightly to make it more cinematic with Roney using a crane to short out the Martian influence whereas in the television version he merely throws a metal chain into the pit.8 The setting for the pit itself was changed from a building site to the London Underground.9 The closing scene of the television version, in which Quatermass pleads with humanity to prevent Earth becoming the <U+201C>second dead planet<U+201D>, was also dropped in favour of a shot of Quatermass and Judd sitting alone amid the devastation wrought by the Martian spacecraft.10 The script was sent to John Trevelyan of the British Board of Film Censors in December 1966.11 Trevelyan replied that the film would require an <U+2018>X<U+2019>-Certificate and raised concerns regarding the sound of the vibrations from the alien ship, the scenes of the Martian massacre, the scenes of destruction and panic as the Martian influence takes hold and the image of the Devil.12

Casting

  • James Donald as Doctor Roney: Donald first came to prominence playing Theo van Gogh in Lust for Life (1956) before going on to play a string of roles in the World War II prisoner of war films The Bridge on the River Kwai (1957), The Great Escape (1963) and King Rat (1965).13 Although not playing the title role, Donald was accorded top-billing status.14
  • Andrew Keir as Quatermass: Nigel Kneale had long been highly critical of Brian Donlevy's interpretation of Quatermass and lobbied for the role to be recast, arguing that enough time had passed that audiences would not resist a change of actor.15 A number of actors were considered for the part including Andr<U+00E9> Morell who had played Quatermass in the television version of Quatermass and the Pit.16 However, Morell was not interested in revisiting a role he had already played.17 The producers eventually settled on Scottish actor Andrew Keir who had appeared in supporting roles in a number of Hammer productions including Pirates of Blood River (1962), The Devil-Ship Pirates (1964) and Dracula: Prince of Darkness (1966).18 Keir found the shoot an unhappy experience: he later recalled, <U+201C>The director <U+2013> Roy Ward Baker <U+2013> didn't want me for the role. He wanted Kenneth More... and it was a very unhappy shoot. [<U+2026>] Normally I enjoy going to work every day. But for seven and a half weeks it was sheer hell.<U+201D>19 Roy Ward Baker denied he had wanted Kenneth More, who he felt would be <U+201C>too nice<U+201D> for the role,20 saying, <U+201C>I had no idea he [Keir] was unhappy while we were shooting. His performance was absolutely right in every detail and I was presenting him as the star of the picture. Perhaps I should have interfered more<U+201D>.21 Keir went on to appear for Hammer in The Viking Queen (1967) and Blood from the Mummy's Tomb (1971).22 He reprised the role of Quatermass for BBC Radio 3 in The Quatermass Memoirs (1996).23
  • Barbara Shelley as Barbara Judd: Shelley was a regular leading lady for Hammer, having appeared in The Camp on Blood Island (1958), Shadow of the Cat (1961), The Gorgon (1964), The Secret of Blood Island (1964), Dracula: Prince of Darkness and Rasputin: The Mad Monk (1966) for them.24 Quatermass and the Pit was her last film for the company and she subsequently worked in television and the theatre.25 Roy Ward Baker was particularly taken with his leading lady, telling Bizarre Magazine in 1974 he was <U+201C>mad about her in the sense of love. We used to waltz about the set together, a great love affair<U+201D>.26
  • Julian Glover as Colonel Breen: Roy Ward Baker first met Glover when he directed him in an episode of The Avengers (1961-69). Baker said of Glover's performance, <U+201C>He turned in a tremendous character, forceful, autocratic but never over the top<U+201D>.27 Glover recalled of the role, <U+201C>I think I was too young for it. [<U+2026>] I think I played it all right. It was very straightforward. Bit of a stereotype. [<U+2026>] The obligatory asshole!<U+201D>.28

Other actors appearing in the film include Robert Morris, Bryan Marshall, Edwin Richfield and Peter Copley.29 Duncan Lamont, playing Sladden, had appeared in the original BBC production of The Quatermass Experiment in the key role of the hapless astronaut Victor Carroon.30 Quatermass and the Pit also features an early film role for Sheila Steafel who makes a brief appearance as a journalist near the start of the movie.31

Filming

By the time Quatermass and the Pit finally entered production Val Guest was occupied on Casino Royale (1967) and so directing duties went instead to Roy Ward Baker.32 Baker's first film had been The October Man (1947) and he was best known for The One That Got Away (1957) and A Night to Remember (1958).33 Following the failure of Two Left Feet (1963), he moved into television directing episodes of The Human Jungle (1963-64), The Saint (1962-69) and The Avengers.34 Producer Anthony Nelson Keys chose Baker as director because he felt his experience on such films as A Night to Remember gave him the technical expertise to handle the film's significant special effects requirements.35 Baker, for his part, felt that his background on fact-based dramas such as A Night to Remember and The One That Got Away enabled him to give Quatermass and the Pit the air of realism it needed to be convincing to audiences.36 He was impressed by Nigel Kneale's screenplay, feeling the script was "taut, exciting and an intriguing story with excellent narrative drive. It needed no work at all. All one had to do was cast it and shoot it."37 He was also impressed with Hammer Films<U+2019> lean set-up: having been used to working for major studios with thousands of full-time employees, he was surprised to find that Hammer's core operation consisted of just five people and enjoyed how this made the decision making process fast and simple.38 Quatermass and the Pit was the first film the director was credited as <U+201C>Roy Ward Baker<U+201D>, having previously been credited as <U+201C>Roy Baker<U+201D>. The change was made to avoid confusion with another Roy Baker who was a sound editor.39 Baker later regretted making the change as many people assumed he was a new director.40

Filming took place between 27 February and 25 April 1967.41 The budget was <U+00A3>275,000.42 At this time, Hammer was operating out of the Associated British Studios in Elstree, Borehamwood. However, a lack of space meant that production was relocated to the nearby MGM Borehamwood studio.43 There were no other productions working at the MGM Studios at this time so the Quatermass crew had full access to all the facilities of the studio.44 Roy Ward Baker was particularly pleased to be able to use MGM's extensive backlot for the exteriors of the Underground station.45 The production team included many Hammer regulars,46 including production designer Bernard Robinson who, as an in-joke, incorporated a poster for Hammer's The Witches (1966) into the dressing of his set for the Hobbs End station.47 Another Hammer regular was special effects supervisor Les Bowie. Roy Ward Baker recalled he had a row with Bowie, who believed the film was entirely a special effects picture, when he tried to run the first pre-production conference.48 Bowie's contribution to the film included the Martian massacre scene, which was achieved with a mixture of puppets and live locusts, and model sequences of London's destruction, including the climatic scene of the crane swinging into the Martian apparition.49

Music

Chosen to provide the score for Quatermass and the Pit was Tristram Cary. He developed an interest in electronic music while serving in the Royal Navy as an electronics expert working on radar during the Second World War.50 He became a professional composer in 1954, working in film, theatre, radio and television,51 with credits including The Ladykillers (1955).52 He said of his assignment, <U+201C>I was not mad about doing the film because Hammer wanted masses of electronic material and a great deal of orchestral music. But I had three kids, all of which were at fee-paying schools, so I needed every penny I could get!<U+201D>.53 Cary also recalled that, <U+201C>The main use of electronics in Quatermass, I think, was the violent shaking, vibrating sound that the <U+201C>thing in the tunnel<U+201D> gave off [<U+2026>] It was not a terribly challenging sound to do, though I never played it very loud because I didn't want to destroy my speakers <U+2013> I did have hopes of destroying a few cinema loudspeaker systems, though it never happened<U+201D>.54 Carey went on to write the score for another Hammer film, Blood from the Mummy's Tomb, in 1971.55 Several orchestral and electronic cues from the film were released by GDI Records on a compilation titled The Quatermass Film Music Collection.56

Reception

Quatermass and the Pit premiered on 9 November 1967 and went on general release in a double bill with Circus of Fear on 19 November 1967.57 It was released under the title Five Million Years to Earth in the US in March 1968.58 The critical reception was generally positive. Writing in The Times, John Russell Taylor found that, <U+201C>After a slowish beginning, which shows up the deficiencies of acting and direction, things really start hopping when a mysterious missile-like object discovered in a London excavation proves to be a relic of a prehistoric Martian attempt (successful. it would seem) to colonize Earth [<U+2026>] The development of this situation is scrupulously worked out and the film is genuinely gripping even when (a real test this) the Power of Evil is finally shown personified in hazy glowing outline, a spectacle as a rule more likely to provoke titters than gasps of horror.<U+201D>59 Paul Errol of the Evening Standard described the film as a <U+201C>well-made, but wordy, blob of hokum<U+201D>, a view echoed by William Hall of the Evening News who described the film as <U+201C>entertaining hokum<U+201D> with an <U+201C>imaginative ending<U+201D>.60 A slightly more critical view was espoused by Penelope Mortimer in The Observer who said, <U+201C>This nonsense makes quite a good film, well put together, competently photographed, on the whole sturdily performed. What it totally lacks is imagination.<U+201D>61

Legacy

The film was a success for Hammer and they quickly announced that Nigel Kneale was writing a new Quatermass story for them but the script never went further than a few prelimininary discussions.62 Kneale did eventually write a fourth Quatermass story, broadcast as a four-part serial, titled Quatermass, by ITV television in 1979, an edited version of which was also given a limited cinema release under the title The Quatermass Conclusion.63 Quatermass and the Pit marked the return to directing for the cinema for Roy Ward Baker and he went on to direct such films as The Anniversary (1968), Moon Zero Two (1969), The Vampire Lovers (1970), Scars of Dracula (1970), Dr. Jekyll and Sister Hyde (1971) and The Legend of the 7 Golden Vampires (1974) for Hammer. He also directed Asylum (1972), And Now the Screaming Starts! (1973) and The Vault of Horror (1973) for Hammer's rivals Amicus Productions.64

Quatermass and the Pit continues to be generally well-regarded among critics. John Baxter notes in Science Fiction in the Cinema that <U+201C>Baker's unravelling of this crisp thriller is tough and interesting. [<U+2026>] The film has moments of pure terror, perhaps the most effective that in which the drill operator, driven off the spaceship by the mysterious power within is caught up in a whirlwind that fills the excavation with a mass of flying papers.<U+201D>65 John Brosnan, writing in The Primal Scream, found that, <U+201C>As a condensed version of the serial, the film is fine but the old black-and-white version, though understandably creaky in places and with inferior effects, still works surprisingly well, having more time to build up a disturbing atmosphere.66 Bill Warren in Keep Watching the Skies! said, <U+201C>The ambition of the storyline is contained in a well-constructed mystery that unfolds carefully and clearly<U+201D>.67 Nigel Kneale had mixed feelings about the end result: he said, <U+201C>I was very happy with Andrew Keir, who they eventually chose, and very happy with the film. There are, however, a few things that bother me... The special effects in Hammer films were always diabolical.<U+201D>68

DVD release

The region 1 release of Quatermass and the Pit from Anchor Bay includes a commentary from Nigel Kneale and Roy Ward Baker as well as trailers and an instalment of a documentary called The Worlds of Hammer devoted to Hammer's forays into science fiction.69

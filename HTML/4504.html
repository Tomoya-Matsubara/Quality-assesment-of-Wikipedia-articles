Quartermaster is the highest rank attainable in Sea Scouting program of the Boy Scouts of America.

Award

Insignia includes a Medal, Knot (blue on white), pin, charm and emblems. The blue stands for the loyalty to country. The compass suggests the importance of a carefully chosen direction in life. The wheel reminds us that we are the guides of our own future and that we must persevere with self discipline. The Scout Badge - The emblem of purposeful brotherhood <U+2013> has challenged and strengthened the lives of more than 40 million men. It shows Sea Scouting as an important part of the scouting tradition. The anchor reminds us that a truly worthy life must be anchored in duty to God. 1
The award is presented at a special Bridge of Honor just for the Recipient.

Requirements

After completing all previous ranks, Apprentice, Ordinary, Able, the Sea Scout can earn the Quartermaster Award.

  • Ideals: Must lead a discussion on "participating citizenship" and submit a paper on the "World Brotherhood of Scouting."
  • Membership: Attend at least 75 percent of ship meetings and activities for 18 months. Present a talk on Sea Scouting and complete a service project.
  • Special Skills: Complete the 11 special skills required for Quartermaster, which include:
    • Boats
    • Marlinspike Seamanship
    • Ground Tackle
    • Piloting
    • Signaling
    • Swimming
    • Cruising
    • Safety
    • First Aid
    • Rules of the Road
    • Weather
  • Electives: Complete four of the following electives: sailing, engine, radio, boat maintenance, electricity, navigation, drill, piloting, rigging, yacht racing crew.2

After completion of requirements, the Sea Scout needs to receive approval from the Skipper, typically by a conference, the Quarterdeck, by the Boatswain at the Quarterdeck Meeting, the Committee and the Council Advancement Committee by Board of Review.

Origins

It is currently unkown when the Quartermaster Award was introduced, however the first recorded Quartermaster was in May 1929. On December 12, 1930, the Quartermaster badge was approved by the National Executive board. It cost 3.00$ Extensive changes to Advancement requirements were made in October 1938 by the National Sea scouting Committee. On September 1, 1949 Sea Exploring was created. The only change in name was from Sea Scouts to Sea Explorers, or Quartermaster Explorers. In 1962 there are 26,751 registered Sea Explorers, and 124 achieved the rank of Quartermaster in that year. In 1966 there are 18,210 registered Sea Explorers, and 82 achieved the rank of Quartermaster in that year.3 Current statistics for Sea Scouts who earn Quartermaster are not published, however probably less are earned that the years that were published.

At one time there was a National Quartermaster Association, however their website does not exist anymore. It's website was located at http://nqma.org

Highest awards in other programs

The Quartermaster Award is the highest award in Sea Scouting. The highest awards in other BSA membership divisions are: the Cub Scouting Arrow of Light, the Boy Scouting Eagle Scout, the Venturing Silver Award, and the Varsity Scouting Denali Award. Other Scouting movements and many non-Scouting organizations have similar programs and awards.

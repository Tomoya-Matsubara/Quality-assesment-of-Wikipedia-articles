The Boston and Montana Consolidated Copper and Silver Mining Company (the "Boston and Montana" or "B&M") was a mining, smelting, and refining company which operated primarily in the state of Montana in the United States. It was established in 1887 and merged with the Amalgamated Copper Company in 1901. The Amalgamated Copper Company changed its name to Anaconda Copper in 1910, and became one of America's largest corporations.1 Historian Michael P. Malone has written, "Well financed and well managed, the Boston and Montana came to rank among the world's greatest copper companies."2

Corporate history

Adolph and Leonard Lewisohn were German Jews whose father had established in 1858 an American subsidiary, Lewisohn Brothers, which bought and sold bristles, feathers, hair, metals, and wood.3 In 1867, Adolph and Leonard emigrated to the United States and took over the company, focusing their attention on the trading of copper, lead, and zinc.4 In 1879, the brothers were invited to purchase some mines in Arizona, Montana, and Tennessee.5 With $75,000, they organized the Montana Copper Company, the Tennessee Copper and Chemical Company, and the Miami Copper Company (in Arizona).6 In 1887, Adolph and Leonard bought Lewisohn Brothers from their father and turned it into an independent company.7

The Boston and Montana was organized on July 22, 1887, by the merger of Lewisohn Brothers, the Montana Copper Company, and the mine holdings of C. X. Larrabee (owner of the highly productive and famous Larrabee and Mountain View mines).8 It quickly became the second-largest company in Montana (only the Anaconda Copper Company in Butte was larger).9 It owned mines in Arizona, Michigan, Montana, and Tennessee.10 For many years, it was managed by "Captain" Thomas Couch, a Cornish immigrant and expert miner.11

In Montana, the B&M relied heavily on copper mined from the Leonard and the Colusa mines, near Butte.12 The company owned a small smelter in Meaderville, a small town just east of Butte (now consumed by the Berkeley Pit mine).13 Other productive mines (the Badger State, Comanche, Liquidator, Mountain View, Pennsylvania, Wandering Jew, and West Colusa) now added their ores to the stream needing smelting and refining.14 The Meaderville smelter was too small, and any smelter would need cheap power and lots of water. Leonard Lewisohn considered both Helena and Great Falls for his new smelter.15

The balance was tipped in favor of Great Falls due to several factors. First was the existence of a railroad to Great Falls. James Jerome Hill, primary stockholder and president of the Great Northern Railway had established a subsidiary, the Montana Central Railway, on January 25, 1886.16 Few railroads served Montana at that time. But Butte was a booming mining town that needed to get its metals to market, gold and silver had been discovered near Helena, and coal companies in Canada were eager to get their fuel to Montana's smelters.17 Hill's close friend and business associate, Paris Gibson, had founded the town of Great Falls on the Great Falls of the Missouri River in 1883, and was promoting it as a site for the development of cheap hydroelectricity and heavy industry.18 Hill was building the Great Northern across the northern tier of Montana, and it made sense to build a north-south railroad through central Montana to connect Great Falls with Helena and Butte.19 Surveyors and engineers had begun grading a route between Helena and Great Falls in the winter of 1885-1886 (even before the company had been incorporated), and by the end of 1886 had surveyed a route from Helena to Butte.20 Construction on the Great Northern's line westward began in late 1886, and on October 16, 1887, the link beween Devils Lake, North Dakota; Fort Assinniboine (near the present-day city of Havre); and Great Falls was complete.21 Service to Helena began in November 1887, and Butte followed the next spring.22

The second reason was Hill's own investment in the city of Great Falls. Hill organized the Great Falls Water Power & Townsite Company in 1887,2324 with the goal of developing the town of Great Falls; providing it with power, sewage, and water; and attracting commerce and industry to the city.2526 To attract industry to the new city, he offered low rates on the Montana Central Railway.27 In case cheap power, plenty of water, and low shipping rates were not enough of an inducement, Hill also gave Leonard Lewisohn 1,500 shares in the Great Falls Water Power & Townsite Company.28

On September 12, 1889, the Boston and Montana signed an agreement with Great Falls Water Power & Townsite Company in which the power company agreed to build a dam that would supply the mining firm with at least 1,000 horsepower (or 0.75 MW) of power by September 1, 1890, and 5,000 horsepower (or 3.73 MW) of power by January 1, 1891.2930 In exchange, Boston and Montana agreed to build a $300,000 copper smelter near the dam.3132 Black Eagle Dam began generating electricity in December 1890.33 Water was permitted to flow over the crest of the dam on January 6, 1891, and the dam was considered complete on March 15, 1891.34

Ground was broken on the smelter in the spring of 1890.35 It was located on a bluff overlooking Black Eagle Dam next to the hamlet of Black Eagle, Montana. The concentrator36 opened in March 1891; Br<U+00FC>ckner cylinders37 and reverberatory furnaces in April 1892; converters in August 1892; refining furnaces38 in January 1893; an electrolytic refinery in February 1893; and blast furnaces in April 1893.3940 The cost of the original plant was $2 million,41 and by 1892 more than 1,000 workers were employed at the smelter.42

Along with Anaconda Copper, the Butte and Boston Consolidated Mining Company,43 and the Montana Ore Purchasing Company, the Boston and Montana was the most powerful mining and refining concern in Montana in the early 1890s.44 In 1890, 50 percent of all copper mined in the U.S. came from Montana.45 In 1897, Anaconda Copper supplied of copper; Calumet and Hecla (in Michigan) supplied of copper; and the Boston and Montana .46

In 1899, Henry Huttleston Rogers, a business associate of John D. Rockefeller, proposed the creation of a giant copper company through the consolidation of smaller firms.4748 F. Augustus Heinze, owner of Montana Ore, opposed these efforts. After losing several battles in Montana state courts, Rogers and his business associates incorporated in the state of New York and attempted to obtain control of several Montana copper mining and smelting companies.49 Their goal was to establish diversity of jurisdiction so that any future lawsuits would be heard by federal courts (which Rogers believed would be less considerate of Heinze's interests).50 Heinze's associates bought stock in the New York company, and then initiated a lawsuit (financed by Heinze) in which they claimed that the reincorporation had been done without shareholder consent.51 Rogers lost the suit, and was forced to re-reincorporate (a process which took several years).52 Rogers purchased Anaconda Copper in 1899, then established a new holding company (the Amalgamated Copper Company) to control Anaconda and the other firms Rogers coveted.53 By November 1900, Amalgamated had purchased the Colorado Smelter and Mining Company and the Washoe Copper Company, and had a controlling interest in the stock of the Parrott Silver and Copper Mining Company.54 The following year, Rogers obtained a majority stockholder position in the Boston and Montana and the Butte and Montana companies.55

Post-consolidation

In 1903, the Amalgamated shut down its smelting operations in an attempt to force Heinze to shut down his mines. This would turn Heinze's miners, who idolized their young chief executive officer, against him.56 Unfortunately, Heinze kept his mining operations open. In 1904, he disobeyed a federal injunction and began mining an underground area whose ownership was in dispute.57 Heinze was fined $20,000, and held in contempt of court.58 John D. Ryan, president of the Daly Bank and Trust Co. in Butte and former president of the Anaconda Company,59 convinced Heinze to sell out to Amalgamated.60 Heinze did so in 1906, accepting $10.5 million in compensation and agreeing to end more than 110 lawsuits which had tied up $70 to $100 million worth of Amalgamated property.6162

On April 7, 1908, construction began on the "Big Stack," a chimney for dispersal of fumes high, with an interior measurement of in diameter at the base and in diameter at the top.63 Built by the Alphonse Custodis Construction Co. of New York, it was completed on October 23, 1908,64 and was the tallest chimney in the world when finished.65

The former Boston and Montana smelter in Great Falls closed in 1980.66

In 2011, the old B&M smelter was listed by the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) as a Superfund hazardous and toxic waste site.67 The EPA also said it would begin sampling for the riverbed above and below Black Eagle Dam, as well as residential areas in the town of Black Eagle, for heavy metals contamination.68 Historic records show that plant wastes were routinely dumped into the Missouri River below Black Eagle dam.697071 MDEQ estimated in 2002 that between and of waste were dumped into the river between 1892 and 1915.72 EPA samples indicated that the contamination could extend as far downstream as Fort Benton, away.73 Toxins present in the water and riverbed, according to the EPA, include antimony, arsenic, cadmium, chromium, cobalt, copper, iron, lead, manganese, mercury, nickel, silver, sodium, and zinc.74

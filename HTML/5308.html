Hurricane Inez was a late-season major hurricane{{#tag:ref|A major hurricane is a tropical cyclone with maximum sustained winds of at least 111 mph (179 km/h), or a Category 3 or higher on the Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale.1|group="nb"}} that affected the Caribbean, Bahamas, Florida, and Mexico, the first such storm on record to affect those areas. It originated from a tropical wave over Africa, and became a tropical depression by September 21. It moved slowly westward, initially failing to intensify much; it was upgraded to Tropical Storm Inez on September 24. The storm strengthened into a hurricane and was quickly intensifying when it struck the French overseas region of Guadeloupe on September 27. Inez severely damaged the island's banana and sugar crops, and thousands of homes were damaged, leaving 10,000 people homeless. Damage on Guadeloupe was estimated at $50 million,{{#tag:ref|All damage totals are in 1955 United States dollars unless otherwise noted.|group="nb"}} and there were 40 deaths. After entering the Caribbean, Inez briefly weakened before restrengthening, attaining peak sustained winds of on September 28. That day, a Hurricane Hunters flight reported a gust of , which was the highest recorded at the time.

Continuing westward, Inez made landfall as a small hurricane on the Barahona Peninsula of the Dominican Republic. There, the storm flooded many rivers and destroyed over 800 houses. Nationwide, there were about 100 deaths and $12 million in damage. After affecting the country, Inez struck southwestern Haiti, where it was considered the worst hurricane since the 1920s. As many as 1,000 people were killed, and 60,000 people were left homeless. Damage totaled $20.35 million in Haiti, prompting the United States government to send aid for the first time in nearly ten years due to previous mishandling of funds. Inez weakened quickly over Hispaniola, although it reintensified into a major hurricane before striking southeastern Cuba on September 30. There, 125,000 people were forced to evacuate, and there were three deaths and $20 million in damage.

The hurricane moved slowly over Cuba for two days before emerging into the Atlantic Ocean near the Bahamas. It was expected to continue out to sea, although it stalled and resumed its previous westward path. In the Bahamas, heavy rainfall and high tides caused flooding, which killed five people and left $15.5 million in damage. Hurricane-force winds occurred throughout the Florida Keys, where 160 homes and 190 trailers were damaged. Salt spray damaged crops in the region, and there was $5 million in damage and four deaths. In the Straits of Florida, Inez capsized a boat of Cuban refugees, killing 45 people. In the northern Gulf of Mexico, a helicopter crashed after carrying evacuees from an oil rig, killing 11 people. The hurricane threatened the northern Yucat<U+00E1>n Peninsula, and although it remained offshore, Inez produced flooding and caused some power outages. At its final landfall, Inez flooded portions of Tamaulipas and cut off roads to Tampico. 84,000 people were left homeless, and the hurricane destroyed at least 2,500 houses. Damage was estimated at $104 million, and there were 74 deaths in Mexico.

Meteorological history

The origins of Inez were from a tropical wave that formed over the Sahara in central Africa on September 15 from the monsoon. It moved westward in the prevailing winds, exiting the west coast of Africa on September 18. The precursor to Inez was a disturbance along the northern portion of the wave,2 which was considered a tropical depression in the annual tropical cyclone summary on September 18. Tracked by satellites, the system moved to the west-southwest,3 and was officially classified a tropical depression on September 21 about halfway between the Lesser Antilles and Africa. The system remained weak as it turned toward the west-northwest. A Hurricane Hunters flight indicated some intensification in the system, prompting the National Hurricane Center (NHC) to initiate advisories on Tropical Storm Inez, located about east of Martinique.4 With warm water temperatures, the storm developed a circular area of convection over the center.5 Inez slowed while curving to the west due to a ridge to the north and quickly intensified,6 reaching hurricane status on September 26.

On September 27, Inez intensified to reach winds of 120 mph (195 km/h), making it a Category 3 on the Saffir-Simpson scale. Later that day, the hurricane moved directly over Guadeloupe. At the time, Inez was a small cyclone, with hurricane-force winds extending from the center.7 Subsequent meteorologists labeled the storm as a "micro-hurricane... because of its characteristic small, tight cloud structure and strong maximum surface wind."8 Its structure became disrupted by the island, and the pressure rose from to , indicative of weakening. However, Inez re-intensified after entering the eastern Caribbean Sea, reaching a minimum pressure of on September 28 while south of Puerto Rico. The Hurricane Hunters reported sustained winds of at a height of , which was the highest recorded at the time. The same flight estimated surface winds between near the center.9 The peak intensity listed in the Atlantic hurricane database is , making Inez a strong Category 4 hurricane.

While near peak intensity, Inez was located about southwest of San Juan, Puerto Rico, and the eye was tracked by radar from the island. Its increasing outflow caused Tropical Storm Judith to the east to dissipate. On September 29, the small but powerful hurricane made landfall on the Barahona Peninsula, and quickly weakened over land. It briefly moved offshore and struck the southern peninsula of Haiti near Jacmel. The eye emerged into the Windward Passage near L<U+00E9>og<U+00E2>ne, although severely weakened; the pressure rose to ,10 and the winds decreased to 90 mph (150 km/h) over land. However, Inez quickly re-intensified over water, reaching winds of 115 mph (185 km/h) on September 30 before striking southeastern Cuba near Guant<U+00E1>namo Bay. When Inez was over Cuba, forecasters had difficulty in predicting the storm's future, and due to a weakness in the ridge to the north, it was expected that the hurricane would move to the north off the east coast of the United States. The hurricane drifted to the west-northwest due to weak steering currents, weakening over the high terrain but later moving offshore the southern coast of Cuba. While over water, the eye reorganized, and the storm maintained hurricane intensity. Inez eventually turned to a northward drift over central Cuba and emerged into the Atlantic Ocean on October 2.11

Shortly after exiting Cuba into the Atlantic, Inez weakened into a tropical storm, and was moving northeastward through the Bahamas about 24 hours later than forecast.12 At the time, the storm was expected to continue to the northeast around the Bermuda high.13 With little outflow and a large, diffuse circulation, Inez did not re-intensify much despite otherwise favorable conditions,14 potentially due to a nearby trough.15 The movement slowed, and the 1966 hurricane summary remarked that "Inez probably became nearly stationary closer to the United States mainland than any other storm." On October 3, Inez turned to the west-southwest due to a strengthening ridge over the western Gulf of Mexico. Over the subsequent few days, the eye moved directly over the Florida Keys from Key Largo to Key West and continued through the Gulf of Mexico while slowly intensifying. On October 7, Inez brushed the northern coast of the Yucat<U+00E1>n Peninsula with hurricane force winds. Subsequently, the ridge weakened, allowing the hurricane to turn more to the west-northwest.16 On October 8, Inez attained a secondary peak intensity of 140 mph (220 km/h), and a pressure of . Briefly threatening the coastline of Texas, Inez turned turned to the southwest after the ridge again strengthened. On October 10, the hurricane made its final landfall near Tampico, Tamaulipas in northeastern Mexico. It rapidly weakened over land, dissipating on October 11.17

The unusual track of Inez marked the first time on record that a tropical cyclone affected the Lesser and Greater Antilles, Bahamas, Florida, and Mexico.18 The Associated Press described Inez as the "first to strike the Yucat<U+00E1>n Peninsula in many years."19 It was also the first to move across the Gulf of Mexico without turning to the north so late in the season, and the 65 advisories issued by the NHC was the highest at the time. The path taken by Inez was compared to Hurricane Betsy in 1965, which also slowed and moved erratically due to a ridge.20 Forecasters had difficulty with the hurricane due to the unusual path, as well as the inability to fly the Hurricane Hunters into Inez while it was over Cuba.21

Preparations

In Guadeloupe, which was the first area the hurricane affected, residents evacuated to schools set up as shelters. Pan American World Airways canceled flights to the region.22 When Inez was near peak intensity, the NHC warned residents in southern Dominican Republic and Haiti of the danger posed by the hurricane. The agency emphasized that the eye was visible on radar to calm tension of an unexpected change in track. In general, hurricane warnings were issued 24 hours in advance, with the exception of southeastern Florida due to the hurricane's slowing track.23 Overall, 250,000 people evacuated along the path of Inez.24 At Guant<U+00E1>namo Bay, officials ordered residents at the naval base to evacuate to storm shelters. Large ships at the port there were moved to Jamaica before the storm struck.25 Throughout eastern Cuba, Premier Fidel Castro ordered about 125,000 people to evacuate in low-lying areas, mostly in Oriente Province.26 United States Air Force tracking stations in the Bahamas were secured or moved to safer locations. Residents in the Florida Keys boarded up windows and store fronts,27 and hundreds of people evacuated to a shelter in Key West. In southeastern Florida, government offices and schools closed.28

Along the Yucat<U+00E1>n peninsula, about 15,000 people were evacuated from the coastal city of Progreso,29 in a region that seldom experiences hurricanes.30 In the northern Gulf of Mexico, a helicopter crashed after evacuating workers from an oil platform about south-southwest of Morgan City, Louisiana in otherwise calm conditions; 11 people died, including the pilot.31 When Inez was approaching its final landfall, the United States Weather Bureau issued hurricane warnings from Brownsville, Texas to Port Isabel, with gale warnings northward to Corpus Christi. At least 2,000 people evacuated from Port Isabel alone,32 and many shrimp boats were secured.33 In the Tampico area where Inez made its final landfall, over 31,000 people evacuated to higher grounds.34

Impact

Overall, Hurricane Inez killed about 1,000 people, mostly on Hispaniola, and caused over $200 million in damage. Crop damage was less than other storms of similar magnitude, due to Inez's small size and its occurrence late in the season.35

Caribbean and Bahamas

When Inez struck Guadeloupe, a station on the island reported sustained winds of , with gusts to , before communications failed. Rains on the island reached .36 High winds destroyed the roofs of thousands of houses, leaving 10,000 people homeless. There was heavy damage to both the banana and sugar crops, described as "flattened" by the St. Petersburg Times.37 The storm flooded a power plant in Pointe-<U+00E0>-Pitre, and many power lines were downed, causing a power outage.38 The Associated Press described the hurricane as having "virtually leveled a whole district of Pointe-<U+00E0>-Pitre".39 Hundreds of people were injured, although due damaged hospitals and a power outage, most only received makeshift treatment.40 There was $50 million in damage,41 and 40 deaths.42 Strong winds also affected nearby Antigua,43 and damage was reported from that island to Dominica.44

In the United States Virgin Islands, a station on St. Croix reported gusts. When Inez passed south of Puerto Rico, its rainbands produced gusty winds along the island's southern coast, reaching in Pe<U+00F1>uelas. On Isla de Mona, wind gusts were estimated at .45 Flooding occurred in southern Puerto Rico, and high winds damaged 20 houses.46

Hurricane Inez struck the Dominican Republic near peak intensity.47 Along the Barahona Peninsula, more than 800 homes were destroyed, and nationwide about 5,000 people were left homeless. There was heavy damage to the country's cotton industry,48 as well as the coffee, sugar cane, and cocoa crops along the Barahona Peninsula. In the capital city of Santo Domingo, heavy rains flooded hundreds of homes along the Ozama River, forcing thousands to evacuate.49 High seas there damaged a portion of a seawall. Many rivers along the Barahona Peninsula were also flooded.50 Throughout the country, Inez killed about 100 people,51 including 55 in Juancho,52 and left $12 million in damage.53

In neighboring Haiti, high winds downed power lines and radio towers, which cut outside communication. High rainfall led to flash flooding between mountain ranges,54 in a location nicknamed the "valley of death"; there, the Weather Bureau remarked that "local winds... may well have exceeded ".55 Thousands of homes were either destroyed or lost their roof.56 The Haitian government reported over 1,000 deaths in the weeks after the storm. The United States government reported that the hurricane "killed no more than 50" in Haiti, and "nor was there a real emergency," due to president Fran<U+00E7>ois Duvalier misusing previous aid.57 The U.S. assessment was incorrect, as the 1966 annual tropical cyclone summary in the Monthly Weather Review indicated 750 deaths in Haiti,58 mostly in Jacmel. President Duvalier considered the hurricane the "worst storm... since the 1920s."59 In the country, 1,000 people were injured and 60,000 were left homeless, and damage was estimated at $20.35 million.60 On Hispaniola, about 1,500 people were seriously injured, and many people were still recovering from Hurricane Flora in 1963.61

A station at Guant<U+00E1>namo Bay reported winds of when Inez moved ashore in Cuba, and rainfall reached .62 Tides were above normal there.63 The hurricane knocked down trees and power lines, and many roofs were damaged on the naval base.64 In the city of Guant<U+00E1>namo, the hurricane destroyed 500 houses.65 High winds heavily damaged the country's sugar and coffee crops.66 When Inez re-entered the Gulf of Mexico, it threatened northwestern Cuba and produced of rainfall. High waves and strong winds affected the coastline.67 In La Habana Province, flooding forced 21,000 people to evacuate,68 mostly in low-lying areas. One building was destroyed in Havana.69 Nationwide, Inez injured at least 38 people,70 and caused five deaths,71 three of which occurred after Inez moved southwestward from the Florida Keys.72 Damage totaled $20 million.73

In the Bahamas, the hurricane dropped heavy rainfall over three days, reaching in Nassau. There, Inez spawned a small tornado that produced winds of over ,74 killing one person and injuring three. High tides flooded portions of Nassau.75 Two waterspouts were reported in Freeport. Wind gusts in the archipelago reached at West End, Grand Bahama, and sustained winds reached in Freeport. Five people died throughout the country, and damage was estimated at $15.5 million.76

United States

In the Straits of Florida, high waves from Inez overturned a blockade runner carrying Cuban exiles to Miami. Ten of the passengers escaped on a damaged raft, but the only survivor was the captain;77 45 people died due to the boat capsizing.78 High waves also disabled three ships and beached a Coast Guard boat near Miami;79 a lifeguard and people on the beach assisted in rescuing the crew of nine Coast Guardsmen amid rough seas.80

When the hurricane moved across the Florida Keys, it dropped light rainfall, reaching near Kendall.81 Inez was considered a "dry hurricane", and about of the precipitation that fell was largely in the form of sea water spray.82 Light rainfall occurred in Miami, where strong winds damaged crops due to salt spray onto local vegetation.83 During its passage, Inez produced above normal tides, reaching above normal on Big Pine Key. Inez produced strong winds while it was intensifying,84 and the strongest winds occurred after the passage of the eye.85 The highest sustained winds were on Plantation Key, and peak gusts were in the Dry Tortugas. On Big Pine Key, sustained winds were estimated at , with gusts to . On the Florida mainland, the highest gusts were at Flamingo.86 The winds damaged smaller tree limbs and uprooted some trees. About 20% of the local avocado crop was blown off trees, although most was salvageable. Winds and salt spray severely damaged vegetables in southern Dade County, including up to 80% losses of tomatoes in some farms.87 Power outages were reported in Key West.88 In the Florida Keys, the hurricane damaged 160 homes and 190 trailers.89 Statewide, damage totaled $5 million,90 and there were four deaths; three were elderly people who died of heart attacks while installing shutters, and the fourth was a teenager who was swept by large surf.91 Inez was the last hurricane to threaten the Miami metropolitan area until Hurricane David in 1979,92 and the last hurricane to strike the Florida Keys until Hurricane Kate in 1985.93

When Inez was making its final landfall in Texas, its outer circulation reached southern Texas, producing winds of and trace rainfall. Tides reached above normal in Corpus Christi,94 and waves caused $5,000 in damage to piers.95

Mexico

While the hurricane was off the north coast of the Yucat<U+00E1>n peninsula, Inez produced sustained winds of , with gusts to , in M<U+00E9>rida.96 The winds cut power and knocked down trees in Progreso, as well as nearby cities. High waves destroyed a dock in Telchac Puerto.97 Heavy rainfall flooded streets in M<U+00E9>rida, although damage was not severe.98

At the hurricane's final landfall, a station in Tampico reported sustained winds of , with gusts to before communications were cut. Inez dropped heavy rainfall upon moving ashore, causing flooding. In Soto la Marina, Tamaulipas, rainfall totaled .99 In northeastern Mexico, more than 3,000 people were stranded by flooding after dozens of rivers and streams rose above their banks, forcing some families to hold onto trees.100 The floods washed out roads around Tampico and cut communications and the water supply.101 In Tamaulipas, 15 people died while trying to deliver supplies to residents along the Tambesi River.102 Throughout the country, the hurricane left about 84,000 people homeless,103 and destroyed at least 2,500 houses.104 Agricultural damage totaled over $80 million, and property damage was estimated at $24 million in Tampico and Ciudad Madero alone.105 Overall, Inez killed 74 people in Mexico.106

Aftermath

In the days after the storm struck Guadeloupe, there was a food shortage.107 Officials quickly cleared roads and worked to restore the damaged banana crop. Charles de Gaulle visited the island, and the French population unified to provide assistance to the island.108 In late October 1966, the French government set up a relief fund for Guadeloupe totaling $34 million (<U+20A3>170 million francs).109 The government initiated plans to rebuild the island to withstand the winds of strong hurricanes.110 The United States Agency for International Development flew 20,000 pounds of blankets and clothing to the island.111 Due to the heavy damage in Hispaniola, VMM-365, a United States Marine Corps helicopter division, spent 454 hours and flew 740 sorties to provide 186,676 tons of relief supplies to Haiti and the Dominican Republic. The division also helped evacuate 142 hospital patients.112 The Dominican Republic Air Force also assisted in transporting injured people from outlying areas to Santo Domingo.113 The country deployed 5,000 troops to send food, medicine, and clothing to the storm-damaged areas in the interior,114 as well as Barahona Peninsula.115 The Holy See sent $2,000 to assist the residents affected by the storm in Haiti.116 After a nearly ten year boycott due to the actions of Haitian president Fran<U+00E7>ois Duvalier, the United States provided a $1.3 million loan to the country to help rebuild infrastructure and provide education. Industrialist Clint Murchison gave $50,000 in flour and food from a mill he owned in the country.117 The United States also sent vaccines and 4,360 pounds of food.118

In Cuba, sailors on the USS America (CV-66) spent about 1,700 hours to assist Guant<U+00E1>namo Bay in recovery.119 The sole survivor of the sinking boat carrying Cuban exiles was tried in a military tribunal. The man was held responsible for the 44 boat deaths and was quickly executed.120 The United States Coast Guard deployed boats, helicopters, and airplanes to find victims of the boat capsized in the Straits of Florida.121 In the United States, the price for lettuce and tomatoes rose due to Inez's crop damage in Florida and reduced crop output in California.122 The country's military sent helicopters and planes to join the Mexican Air Force,123 as well as trucks by land,124 to deliver tons of supplies, including food, clothing, and medicine to flood-affected areas in Tamaulipas.125 In the weeks after the storm, many residents had minimal food access, and some areas remained flooded. Conditions in Tampico, located near where the storm moved ashore, gradually returned to normal.126 Heavy rainfall in Mexico restored reservoirs, which assisted future crop growing.127

The name Inez was retired and will never again be used for an Atlantic hurricane name.128

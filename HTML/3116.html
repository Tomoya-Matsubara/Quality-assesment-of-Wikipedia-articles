Gian Gastone de' Medici (24 May 1671 – 9 July 1737) was the seventh and last Medicean Grand Duke of Tuscany. He was the second son of Cosimo III de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany, and Marguerite Louise d'Orl<U+00E9>ans, Princess of France.1 His sister, the Electress Palatine, married him to Anna Maria Franziska of Saxe-Lauenburg, a wealthy widow, in 1697. Gian Gastone despised her; and she, him. The union produced no offspring. As Grand Prince Ferdinando, Gian Gastone's elder brother, predeceased Cosimo III, Gian Gastone succeeded his father as Grand Duke in 1723.

His reign was marked by the reversal of his predecessor's ultra-reactionary policy; he abolished taxes for poorer people, repealed the anti-Semitic penal laws and discontinued public executions.2 The Medici were wanting in male heirs; his father, Cosimo III, wanted the Electress Palatine to succeed Gian Gastone. However, Spain, Great Britain, Austria and the Dutch Republic disregarded Cosimo's plan and appointed Don Carlos of Spain<U+2013>whose mother, Elisabeth Farnese, was a great-granddaughter of Margherita de' Medici<U+2013>Gian Gastone's heir.3 Don Carlos later transferred his claim to Francis III of Lorraine pursuant to a preliminary peace that was finalized in 1738.4 Francis duly succeeded at Gian Gastone's demise, on 9 July 1737, ending almost 300 years of Medici rule over Florence. For the latter part of his reign, Gian Gastone chose to remain confined in his bed, tended by his entourage, the Ruspanti.5

Biography

Early years (1671-1697)

On 24 May 1671, the first anniversary of his grandfather Ferdinando II's death, Giovanni Battista Gastone de' Medici was born in Florence to Grand Duke Cosimo III and Marguerite Louise d'Orl<U+00E9>ans. He derived his baptismal name, Giovanni Battista Gastone, from his maternal grandfather, Gaston of France, duc d'Orl<U+00E9>ans.6 Cosimo and Marguerite Louise frequently quarelled. As a result, four years after his birth, Marguerite Louise returned home to France. Therefore, Gian Gastone and his siblings were left in the care of their grandmother Vittoria della Rovere.7

Gian Gastone was tutored by Cardinal Henry Noris, whose company the Prince hardly left.8 The Tuscan Prince was an avid intellectual, being an antiquarian, a botanist and an amateur scientist.9 In addition, he could speak English, among other languages.10 However, it was these traits that earned Gian Gastone the disdain of both his father and his elder brother, Grand Prince Ferdinando; Gian Gastone was thus granted a scant allowance by his father and therefore could not enjoy the "dissipations" of court.11

Cosimo III considered compelling Gian Gastone to become a cardinal. In order to rally Gian Gastone to its cause, Spain, concerned that another Medicean cardinal<U+2014>Gian Gastone's uncle, Francesco Maria de' Medici, already enjoyed that dignity<U+2014>would tip the scales in favour of France at a Papal conclave, offered to create him "General of the Spanish Seas".12 Peter II of Portugal, however, had other things in mind for him: he wanted Gian Gastone to marry his only daughter, Infanta Isabel Lu<U+00ED>sa. To marry her, Gian Gastone would have to convince his father to allot him an allowance of "adequate stature". However, the Grand Duke refused, and both the cardinalate and the Portuguese marriage never materialised.13

Out of sympathy, Gian Gastone befriended his unhappy sister-in-law, Violante Beatrice of Bavaria.14 Her husband, Grand Prince Ferdinando, thought her too ugly and too dull for him.15 At the same time, Gian Gastone sank into a state of melancholy.16 In an attempt to rouse him from this condition, Cardinal Francesco Maria often summoned Gian Gastone to festivities at his villa, Lappeggi.17 However, these soir<U+00E9>es had no effect and Gian Gastone continued to weep unceasingly in his private rooms.18

Marriage and Bohemia (1697-1708)

By 1697, Violante and Ferdinando had been married for eight years and still lacked issue, as did Gian Gastone's sister, the the Electress Palatine. Concerned for the future of the dynasty, Cosimo urged the Electress to find Gian Gastone<U+2014>currently the only one of his siblings unmarried<U+2014>a suitable bride.19 She put forward Anna Maria Franziska, her brother-in-law's widow and potential heiress of the Duchy of Saxe-Lauenburg.20 The bride-to-be was hailed as "more like a Bohemian peasant than a princess" by a contemporary.21 Irrespective, they were married in D<U+00FC>sseldorf, the capital of the Electoral Palatinate, by the Bishop of Osnabr<U+00FC>ck on 2 July 1697.22 As she did not like cities or courts, Anna Maria Franziska demanded they establish themselves in her Bohemian residence, Ploskovice Castle, in Reichstadt, post-haste.23

Gian Gastone found life in the little village intolerable.24 Anna Maria Franziska was unpredictable and prone to outbursts of rage, she held "conversations in the stables [i.e. with the horses]" and would rather have remained a widow than have been married again.25 The lack of intellectual society there and his wife's hostility towards him drove Gian Gastone into the arms of alcohol.26 Gian Gastone, unable to rein in his disgust, abandoned Anna Maria Franziksa for Paris after one year in Reichstadt. Whence he arrived there, an enraged Cosimo, who had explicitly told his son not to leave Anna Maria Franziksa without his prior consent,27 ordered him back to Reichstadt.28 Anna Maria Franziska made an effort to welcome him back; however, her mood turned sour when Gian Gastone brought up the prospect of wintering in Prague.29 Thus, he went to Prague alone but for his suite and favourite attendant, Giuliano Dami.

Gian Gastone's once acclaimed complexion and weight were ruined by the course of dissipation he pursued in Prague, becoming blotchy and bloated respectively.30 Dami acted as a pimp for the Prince, soliciting countless young men for his master's enjoyment.31 The Prince frequently gambled, racking up exorbitant debts, losing, on one occasion, 150,000 crowns. His ruinous behaviour was relayed to Florence by the Electress Palatine. Gian Gastone replied to Cosimo's ensuing admonishes with an account of his married life, blaming Anna Maria Franziska's "capriciousness, peevish faces and sharp words" for his desperate conduct.32 Considering summoning him to Florence, Cosimo sent the Marquis Rinuccini to scrutinise his younger son's debts. Rinnucci was horrified to learn that the Archbishop of Prague was among his creditors.33 Anna Maria Franziska greeted Rinuccini warmly, and implied that Gian Gastone pawned some of her jewels to ameliorate his gambling debts.3435 The Marquis concluded that nothing<U+2014>not even the Holy Roman Empress's reproaches<U+2014>could compel Anna Maria Franziska to Florence, where Gian Gastone longed to be.36 Forced once again to quell his enmity towards his wife, Gian Gastone returned to Reichstadt as per the Holy Roman Emperor and Holy Roman Empress's advice.37 Nevertheless, their reconciliation was brief, and Gian Gastone left for Hamburg in October 1703, only to return to Prague the next February.38

The Grand Duke tired of the Princess of Saxe-Lauenburg's stubborn ways. He requisitioned the help of Pope Clement XI, who sent the Archbishop of Prague to tell her she must go to Florence with Gian Gastone. The Princess was incensed, replying that there was no point going with him because he was "absolutely impotent."39 Cosimo III conceded defeat and recalled Gian Gastone to Florence in 1708; he never saw his wife again.40

Return to Florence (1708-1723)

As Prince Gian Gastone disliked his father's hyper-pious character, he elected to stay away from him and the royal court.41 The closed court that Gian Gastone did keep was dominated by his favourite, Dami.42 Meanwhile, the Grand Prince Ferdinando was slowly dying from syphilis, making Gian Gastone's immediate succession more likely.43 Ferdinando, nonetheless, was not alone in his suffering: Guyot de Merville, a French adventurer, took note of Gian Gastone's mental decline: "He carries [apathy] so far that it is said he never opens a letter, to avoid having to answer."44 Additionally, Gian Gastone's fragile temperament required long periods of isolation; he spent several hours alone each night, drinking and staring up at the moon.45

The Grand Prince finally succumbed to syphilis on 30 October 1713, sparking a succession crisis.46 Cosimo III deposited a bill in the Tuscan senate, the nominal legislature, provisioning for a male line succession failure by making the Electress Palatine Gian Gastone's heiress.47 It passed and was disseminated to chancelleries across Europe.48 Austria refused to sanction it, fearing that Tuscany would fall into the Bourbons' hands.49 However, France and England did.50

The Electress returned to Florence in October 1717, following the death of her husband the previous summer.51 Violante of Bavaria, to whom Gian Gastone had become quite attached, disliked the Electress and therefore left the grand ducal court for the position of governor of the town of Siena.52 The Electress, now the first-lady of Tuscany, and Gian Gastone were not on good terms: he scorned her for marrying him to Anna Maria Franziska, who, for eleven years, made his life unbearable.53

On 4 April 1718, England, France and the Dutch Republic (and later Austria) selected Don Carlos of Spain, the elder child of Elisabeth Farnese (a great-granddaughter of Margherita de' Medici) and Philip V of Spain, as the Tuscan heir, the Electress's rights to throne being completely disregarded in the process.54 All his ambitions in regards to the succession being thwarted, Cosimo III distributed one final proclamation shortly before his death, on 31 October 1723, decreeing that the Electress shall succeed Gian Gastone.55 Unfortunately for Cosimo, his declaration was completely ignored.56

Reign (1723-1737)

The Tuscany Gian Gastone inherited was in a pitiful state: the army numbered less than 3,000, the royal coffer was empty and Florence was full of beggars.5758 The ramshackle condition of the capital was noted by philosopher Montesquieu five years into Gian Gastone's reign: "There is no town where men live in less luxury than Florence". Despite these hindrances, Gian Gastone, aged 52, commenced his reign with a burst of ebullience, releasing prisoners, abolishing exorbitant taxes, "Pensions on the Creed"<U+2014>monies paid to converts to Christianity<U+2014>and public executions.596061

Gian Gastone recalled Governor Violante to the royal court and banished his sister, the Dowager Electress, to the Villa La Quiete.62 The Governor reigned supreme over Tuscan society, and Gian Gastone delegated most of his public duties to her, and literally chose to spend most of his time in bed.63 Here, Gian Gastone was entertained by the Ruspanti, a team of poor, handsome young men assembled by Dami, who performed sexual acts for and with the Grand Duke.64 Loathing the aristocracy, Gian Gastone called the foremost Ruspanti by the names of prominent Tuscan nobles. A contemporary dubbed the head of the Ruspanti, Giuliano Dami, "the despot of Gian Gastone's court"; he exploited his influence with the Grand Duke by offering anybody who was willing to bribe him an audience with their monarch.65

During the period 1723–1731, the powers-that-be busied themselves hammering out a solution to the "Tuscan question". Spain zealously endorsed its candidate, the Infante Don Carlos, who had been made heir by the powers in 1718 but did not enjoy universal recognition; Austria would not to accept another Bourbon monarch in Italy.66 Gian Gastone, knowing the Dowager Electress probably would never succeed, went about ensuring his sister's inheritance of all the House of Medici's private property. Thus, he separated Medici possessions from those of the state<U+2014>hitherto, there was no distinction between the two.67

Governor Violante and the Dowager Electress, following the former's sojourn in Rome, in spite of their mutual dislike of each other, attempted to ameliorate Gian Gastone's decadent public image together. Violante organised banquets to remove him from the sway of the Ruspanti. His behaviour at these literally sent guests running for their carriages: he vomited into his napkin, wiped his mouth with his peri-wig and told rude jokes.68 These parites ceased when Violante's died, in 1731; Gian Gastone was devastated by the loss of his beloved sister-in-law. Rumours abounded that the Grand Duke had died, as the public never saw him. To discredit these, the Dowager Electress induced Gian Gastone to make a public appearance<U+2014>his final one<U+2014>on Saint John the Baptist's day of 1729.69 An alcoholic, Gian Gastone drank too much before the ceremony and vomited repeatedly throughout; he had to be carried unconscious back to the Palazzo Pitti, the royal palace, on a litter.70 A sprained ankle incurred in July 1729 left the Grand Duke bed-ridden for<U+2014>barring two occasions<U+2014>the rest of his life.71

The "Tuscan question" finally seemed resolved by the Treaty of Vienna (1731): in exchange for Spain and her allies recognition of the Pragmatic Sanction, Austria confirmed Don Carlos as Gian Gastone's heir<U+2014>the Grand Duke wasn't consulted.72 He was, however, appointed joint-guardian of the Infante with Dorothea Sophia of the Rhine, Dowager Duchess of Parma and the Electress Palatine's sister-in-law.73 Thirty thousand Spanish troops occupied the Grand Duchy of Tuscany on Don Carlos's behalf in October 1731, and the Duke of Parma, Don Carlos's monarchical title since January, arrived in Florence the next March.7475 Gian Gastone was taken with the Infante-Duke, showering him with gifts.76 He even went so far as to have the Duke created Grand Prince of Tuscany, the title borne by the Tuscan heir-apparent, against the wishes of the Holy Roman Emperor, Charles VI; however, the latter soon had the grant revoked.77

After the War of the Polish Succession broke out in 1733, Don Carlos marched a Spanish army south and defeated the Austrians holding the kingdoms of Naples and Sicily. Pursuant to preliminary peace terms negotiated in 1735 (but not finalized until after Gian Gastone's death with the 1738 Treaty of Vienna), Don Carlos surrendered Parma and the claim to the Tuscan throne to Francis III of Lorraine in exchange for being crowned king of Naples and Sicily. (Francis was forced to surrender Lorraine to Stanis<U+0142>aw Leszczy<U+0144>ski, the losing claimant to the Polish throne.)78 Neither the Grand Duke nor Francis III were consulted. The latter was reluctant to cede his homeland to France, but the Holy Roman Emperor (soon to be his father-in-law) compelled him.79 Gian Gastone was angry; he had become quite attached to the Infante-Duke, and didn't like the prospect of a foreigner ruling Tuscany.80 Spain accordingly withdrew its troops from Tuscany in January 1737; 6,000 Austrians took their place on Francis's behalf.81

Death and legacy

By the time of the Spanish withdrawal, the Grand Duke was dying from "an accumulation of diseases".82 One of his final acts was to order the erection of a statue of Galileo Galilei, who was patronised by his grandfather Grand Duke Ferdinando II, in the Cathedral of Santa Croce.83 Anna Maria Luisa, the Dowager Electress Palatine, rushed to his bedside when it became apparent he would not recover.84 Gian Gastone could not eat anything, and his condition steadily worsened; Anna Maria Luisa subsequently had him moved to a clean bed.85 Worried for her brother's soul, the Dowager Electress had the irreligious Gian Gastone repent for his sins. On 9 July 1737, in the fourteenth year of his reign, the last Medicean Grand Duke expired.86 The Electress Anna Maria Luisa inherited all of the House of Medici's allodial possessions, but as per the great power's wishes Francis of Lorraine succeeded to the title Grand Duke of Tuscany.

Gian Gastone gave his all in reforming his realm, purging corrupt ecclesiasts from government, reversing Cosimo III's ban of teaching "new ideas", i.e. the of philosophy Galileo et al., in the University of Pisa and abolishing the cumbersome income taxes.87 Ergo, Gian Gastone was sincerely mourned by his people.88 Charles de Brosses, a French politician and writer, wrote, in 1739, "The Tuscans would give two-thirds of their property to have the Medici back, and the other third to get rid of the Lorrainers."89 This sentiment did not diminish much in 62 years, when, in 1799, during the French occupation of Tuscany, the people called for the long-dead Gian Gastone to ascend the throne.90 His remains were interred in the Medicean necropolis, the Basilica of San Lorenzo. His corpse was dressed in "black velvet", according to Official Report on the Examination of the Tombs in the Medici Mausoleum of 1857, "with, over this, the great cloak of Grand Master of the Order of Saint Stephen. On the head was the Grand Ducal crown, worn over a cap; and by his side the sceptre. But the crown and sceptre were corroded by the acids which had been used in embalming the body."91

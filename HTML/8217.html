The February 2009 tornado outbreak was a tornado outbreak that primarily affected portions of the South Central United States on February 10, as well as eastern sections of the United States on February 11. During the two-day span, 15 tornadoes touched down in seven different states;Oklahoma was struck by six, the most of any state. The first day of the outbreak proved to be the most active regarding tornadoes, while the second brought mainly high wind damage.

The outbreak occurred after a storm system moved across the four corners region into the central and southern Great Plains during the afternoon of February 10. A moist low-level air mass moved to the north across Texas and Louisiana into eastern Oklahoma and the Ozarks. Daytime heating of the moistening boundary layer increased through the afternoon across Oklahoma and Texas and combined with cooling aloft in conjunction with the approaching storm system, the air mass began to destabilized, which resulted in conditions supportive of thunderstorm development. A strong wind field, increasing with height through the lower troposphere, resulted in favorable wind shear for rotation in the thunderstorms. The risk of tornadoes was down significantly for February 11, as stronger frontal forcing limited discrete supercell activity and lowered the instability in the air mass. However, a squall line developed during the day and produced four tornadoes.

The most devastating tornado of the outbreak was an EF4 tornado that traveled through four counties in Oklahoma during the evening hours of February 10. It killed eight people and injured 46 others, as well as destroy 114 residences in Lone Grove. The Lone Grove tornado is the deadliest to hit Oklahoma since May 3, 1999 and the strongest tornado during the month of February in Oklahoma since 1950.

Synopsis

During the afternoon hours of February 10, A strong middle and upper storm system moved across the four corners region and emerged into the central and southern Great Plains. A moist low-level air mass streamed northward across Texas and Louisiana into eastern Oklahoma and the Ozarks ahead of the system. Daytime heating of the moistening boundary layer increased through the afternoon across Oklahoma and Texas as cloud cover shifted eastward. Along with cooling aloft in conjunction with the approaching storm system, the air mass began destabilizing, resulting in the development of thunderstorms. A very strong wind field that increased with height through the lower troposphere resulted in favorable wind shear for rotation in the thunderstorms.1

As a result, large hail, damaging winds along with the potential for strong tornadoes was expected as thunderstorms developed from central Oklahoma southward into north central Texas in the afternoon hours. During the night, a cold front moved eastward toward the Mississippi Valley and organized the thunderstorms into a squall line. Along with a continued threat for hail and tornadoes, the risk of damaging winds increased.2 As a result, the Storm Prediction Center issued a moderate risk for severe weather for portions of eastern Oklahoma, northeastern Texas, western Arkansas, and northwest Louisiana.3

At around 1 pm CST (1900 UTC), Violent thunderstorms began developing near the Wichita Mountains in Comanche County. The supercell thunderstorm that would produce the first tornado of the outbreak moved northeast through Caddo and Grady counties over the next 30 to 45 minutes, before reaching southeastern Canadian County at around 2 pm CST (2000 UTC).4 By 2:14 pm CST (2014 UTC), it was beginning to develop a hook echo, and the hook echo was fully developed four minutes later. Wall clouds were then reported from the Yukon area into western portions of Oklahoma City during the next 10 to 15 minutes.5 At 2:37 pm CST (2037 UTC), the supercell produced a tornado northwest of Warr Acres, causing damage near the Northwest Expressway.6 It continued showing signs of rotation on Doppler weather radar as it moved northeast across Oklahoma County. At 2:52 pm CST (2052 UTC), the supercell produced its second tornado near Edmond and moved into neighborhoods and subdivisions on the northwestern side of Edmond, produced its most severe damage near the Oklahoma and Logan County line. The tornado dissipated north of the Logan County Line at 3:05 pm CST (2105 UTC).7

The supercell produced three other tornadoes as it continued to move northeast. The first tornado occurred northwest of Meridian in Logan County at 3:26 pm CST (2136 UTC). The fourth tornado produced by the supercell developed near Langston in Logan County and moved to about southwest of Stillwater in Payne County. The fifth tornado produced by this supercell in Oklahoma occurred in Pawnee County.8 There were also three tornadoes that struck northern sections of Texas and one tornado that hit Springfield, Missouri.910

The most significant tornado of the outbreak first touched down in southeast Jefferson County at about 6:50 pm CST (0050 UTC) near the Red River. The tornado crossed through rural areas as it moved to the northeast into Love County, before going into Carter County at 7:10 pm CST (0110 UTC). The tornado continued moving to the northeast for the next 15 minutes, before striking Lone Grove.11 It came close to hitting the Newport Mesonet station as it passed through Lone Grove, before crossing Interstate 35 just north of the Prairie Valley Road exit. The tornado tracked through rural areas of northeastern Carter County to the east of Springer, and eventually crossed into southern Murray County, ending southeast of Dougherty at 8:00 pm CST (0200 UTC).12

The Storm Prediction Center also issued a very large slight risk area for February 11 which encompassed parts of the Deep South, Ohio Valley, Midwest, and Appalachians. The risk for tornadoes was lower on February 11 due to stronger frontal forcing, which limited discrete supercell activity, and lower instability.13 The slight risk was later upgraded to a moderate risk as a powerful squall line developed.14

Lone Grove tornado

At about 6:50 pm CST (0050 UTC), a large wedge tornado touched down near the Red River. It crossed through Jefferson and Love counties before entering Carter County. It then slammed into Lone Grove with devastating results across a swath over a half-mile wide.15 The tornado dissipated at 8:00 pm CST (0230 UTC) near Dougherty after remaining on the ground for over an hour.16 After an assessment by the National Weather Service office in Norman, Oklahoma, the tornado was rated as an EF4 on the Enhanced Fujita Scale with winds estimated at .17 The total distance traveled by the tornado was estimated to be at least .18 The Lone Grove tornado is the deadliest to hit Oklahoma since May 3, 1999,19 and the strongest tornado during the month of February in Oklahoma since 1950.20 The previous strongest were two F3 tornadoes which touched down on February 17, 1961.21

Damage was extensive in the community. Buildings were reported to have been thrown off their slabs, the local chamber of commerce office was flattened, a furniture store was destroyed and two mobile home parks were also destroyed.22 One of which, Bar K, contained 40 homes.23 All 40 of the homes were completely destroyed by the tornado, leaving about 100 people homeless.24 Some mobile homes were completely destroyed, having only the cinder blocks they stood on remaining.25 One mobile home was thrown 100 yards from its foundation before being destroyed by the tornado.26 The UPS building was also damaged27 with its glass lobby being destroyed.28 The area north of Ardmore was also hard hit, where several buildings, including Beaver Academy, were heavily damaged or destroyed.29 Ardmore Adventist Academy was significantly damaged by the tornado.30 Most of the damage was to the school's gymnasium, but most of the campus was considered a total loss.31 Eight to ten homes were destroyed in Ardmore and another 20 sustained damage.32 A total of 114 residences were destroyed by the tornado in Lone Grove.33

Eight people were confirmed dead,34 though early reports suggested that as many as 15 people were killed as a result of the tornado.35 Seven of the fatalities were in the town of Lone Grove, most of which were caused by <U+201C>blunt-force trauma to the head". Three of the victims were found inside their homes, two were found outside their homes, and one was found in an open field. The person who was found in the field was reported to have been picked up by the tornado, thrown by it, and killed on impact. The eighth fatality occurred when a truck driver was pinned under his vehicle on Interstate 35.36 Two of the victims were the parents of a 13-year old girl. They were killed when the tornado picked the three of them out of their home and threw them 400 feet.37 One of the fatalities was the result of a pickup truck being thrown by the tornado and landing on a man.38 Three people were rescued after being trapped in the ruins of their home for almost a day. Officials stated that 10 to 15 people remained unaccounted for on the night of February 12 but they were presumed to have left the area.39 An additional 46 people were injured.40 Total damages from the tornado were estimated at $3 million.41

During the evening of February 10, a long line of severe thunderstorms developed along the tail-end of a cold front in central Texas. Embedded supercell thunderstorms were capable of producing hail up to in diameter and wind gusts up to .65 North of the squall line, bow echo thunderstorms developed in Missouri, causing widespread wind damage.66 By the morning of February 11, the squall line tracked into eastern Louisiana where winds were recorded in excess of along with hail up to in diameter.67 Around 12 pm, the line of thunderstorms significantly weakened and a new, narrow, line developed in eastern Kentucky and Tennessee.68 A strong, deep layer wind field prevented a significant number of tornadoes from forming but allowed for widespread wind damage.69 Throughout the day, the line of low-topped thunderstorms continued eastward, reaching Ohio and West Virginia by 4 pm.70 Several hours later, the line broke apart, with the strongest storms tracking through Pennsylvania.71 By February 12, rain showers, accompanied by high winds up to affected most of the Northeast United States.72

In addition to the strong tornadoes produced across Oklahoma, high winds and large hail impacted the state. Extremely large hail, measuring up to in diameter, caused significant damage in Okesa. Numerous cars were hit with the hail and several houses sustained roof damage. The cost of the hail was estimated at $100,000 in the town.73 Heavy rains produced by the storms also caused isolated flooding, with numerous streets being inundated with several feet of water.74 Lightning also produced damage in the state, with one bolt striking an oil tank, igniting a fire. The fire severely damaged the tank, leaving $13,000 in damages.75 Throughout the state, an estimated 61,000 people lost power; 29,000 lost power due to thunderstorms 76 and 32,000 due to tornadoes.7778 In Mississippi, strong winds on the backside of the cold front caused isolated damages, with a metal roof being blown off a power company and downed several trees and power lines.7980 Damages in the state amounted to $35,000.81

Strong straight-line winds near caused significant damage in Hamilton County, Texas. A barn sustained severe damage and a home lost most of its roof. Debris from a small outbuilding which was destroyed was carried about half a mile north of where the structure once stood by 80 mile per hour (130 km/h) winds. A trailer near Hamilton Municipal Airport was broken off its moorings and moved 20 feet. Several mobile homes near Hamilton sustained damage after tree limbs fell on them. In Huron, several outbuildings were damaged or destroyed and a permanent building was damaged at a youth retreat center.82 An estimated 15,000 residences were without power following the storms in Texas.83 One home in McGregor was destroyed by the winds. The three people who lived in the home, one of which was a child, all sustained injuries. The father had a few broken ribs and several scratches after he tried to find his son during the storm which hurled debris at him.84 In Louisiana, 12,000 residences lost power due to the storm system.85 Two wildfires were sparked in Kenedy County and Kleberg County, burning 300 and 20 acres respectively. The larger of the two fires was along Texas State Highway 77. The second fire burned three homes and threatened 20 others.8687

In Arkansas, high winds produced by the squall line knocked out power to 315,324 residences and caused widespread structural damage.88 Seven people sustained injuries in the state. Trees and power lines were downed in eight counties. Several homes in Independence and Van Buren County either lost their roofs sustained damage.89 Winds gusting up to in Independence County damaged numerous homes, leaving $200,000 in damages.9091 A wind gust in Van Buren blew a barn onto Arkansas Highway 9, destroying it. Hundreds of trees were blown down, many of which fell on homes, causing widespread damage. Damages in the county were estimated at $625,000.9293 In Conway County, a sawmill and two barns were destroyed. Several homes also sustained damage, mainly roof damage, as well as one church. Damages in the county were estimated at $300,000.94 Three people were injured in White County; the cause of the injuries is unknown. Numerous homes sustained wind damage and two mobile homes and a recreational vehicle were overturned. Damages in the county were estimated at $750,000.95 Total damages throughout the state were estimated at $2.4 million.96

In Ohio, wind gusts led to a highway accident which killed a truck driver.97 The high winds also caused significant damage throughout Ohio.98 Heavy rains produced by the same system inundated several streets in flood-prone towns in Ohio.99 In Scioto County, a brick home was destroyed by high winds and numerous power lines were downed.100 The most significant damage resulted from the strong pressure gradient behind the frontal system. Thousands of tree were knocked down or uprooted by winds gusting in excess of ,101 cutting power to 585,775 residences.102 Ten rail cars were knocked off their tracks near Shelby. Several hundred homes lost shingles and gutters due to the winds. Two semi-trucks were overturned; the drivers of both vehicles were unharmed. A vacant school building in Epworth lost it's entire roof.103 Some windows also had windows broken by flying debris or sustained damage from fallen trees. Power outages forced numerous schools to call off classes for at least two days following the storm.104 A large barn was leveled just north of Brighton by winds.105 Throughout the state, damages from the storm system amounted to $4.7 million.106

In Missouri, high winds caused widespread damage and knocked out power to 7,629 residences. Several homes and businesses lost their windows due to wind gusts produced within squall lines.107108 In Huntsville, Alabama, a car carrying four people lost control on rain-slicked roads and crashed into a van, killing one of the occupants and injuring the other three.109 Another fatality occurred in Kentucky when a utility worker was knocked over by high winds and fell to his death while trying to restore power.110 Following the widespread power outages from a severe ice storm, an additional 161,588 residences lost power due to winds.111 Flooding and high winds in Michigan knocked out power to about 57,000 residences.112 In southern Michigan, upwards of of rain fell, leading to faster snowmelt.113 In Tennessee, several structures were damaged and numerous trees were knocked down, closing roads throughout the state as the squall line tracked eastward.114 A total of 74,052 residences lost power due to the winds.115 Winds gusting up to caused substantial damage in Rutherford County. Several commercial buildings had major roof damage, leaving $150,000 in damages.116 Throughout Tennessee, damages from the storm system were estimated at $1.1 million.117

In Iowa, heavy rains triggered rapid snowmelt, causing isolated flooding. Only minor impacts resulted from the flooding; total damages from the event was estimated at $25,000.118 A total of 14,590 residences lost power in Indiana.119 Several large trees were downed by high winds, one of which fell on a home in South Bend, leaving $50,000 in damages.120 In the town of Carmel, a total of of rain fell, the highest in the state. The heavy rains, in combination with unseasonable temperatures, led to significant snowmelt and flooding.121 The highest wind gust recorded in the state was in Crawford County.122 A total of 70,695 residences lost power in Illinois.123 Rainfall up to fell throughout most of the state. Several major roadways were covered in flood waters. The Little Wabash River overflowed its banks and inundated nearby roads.124 Along Illinois Route 1, a large tree blocked a section of the road near Georgetown. The fallen tree resulted in a vehicle accident; the driver was unharmed by the accident.125 One person was killed by high winds in Davy, West Virginia when a gymnasium roof collapsed.126 Throughout the state, power was knocked out to an estimated 225,000 residences.127 In Montgomery County, Virginia, winds knocked down power lines which sparked two brush fires, one of which burned a total of 12 acres.128 Winds up to cut power to 28,059 residences in the state.129 The strong winds also cut power to 50,820 residences in Maryland.130

Hurricane-force wind gusts up to caused significant damage and power losses in Pennsylvania.131 Throughout the state, at least 400,000 residences lost power due to the winds. Allegheny Power stated that the loss of power due to this system was the largest ever experienced by the company. Thousands of trees and power lines were downed by strong winds and numerous homes sustained significant damage.132 Most of the damages to homes consisted of shingle damage; however, several homes lost gutters and had portions of their sidings blown off.133 In Pottstown, a large portion of the roof of a four story building was blown off, forcing residents to evacuate the building. An pillar was knocked down at the First Moravian Church in Easton. In Northampton County, a fire sparked by downed power lines destroyed a barn and partially melted a nearby metal shed. The blaze was fully contained by 40 firefighters. Another fire sparked by down power lines destroyed a garage in Lower Merion Township. In Philadelphia, the roof of a portable classroom began to peel off, forcing the forty students inside to evacuate to a safer structure. A large tree fell through one house and severely damaged the roof of a nearby home. Numerous major highways and local streets were shut down for several hours to allow cleanup crews to clear debris.134 Throughout the state, damages from the storm system amounted to $3.7 million.135

The storm system continued producing gusty winds through February 12, which caused tree damage, power outages, and killed a construction worker in the New York City metropolitan area.136 The construction worker was killed after a cinderblock wall collapsed due to high-winds. Winds in the city gusted to in the Bronx. Throughout Long Island, an estimated 4,000 people lost power.137 In upstate New York, winds in excess of toppled numerous trees, downed power lines, cutting power to more than 50,000 residences and shattered windows.138139 Unseasonably warm temperatures and heavy rains from the storm system increased the snowmelt rate in northern areas of the state. In Genesee County, an estimated of the snowpack melted, triggering flash floods along several rivers. The Tonawanda Creek reached a height of , above flood stage. Several homes were inundated by flood waters throughout the region.140 In all, 84,624 residences lost power in New York State.141

Two people were killed in New Jersey after trees limbs fell on them due to strong winds which gusted up to . Several others were injured due to the storm.142 A total of 93,816 residences were also left without power at the height of the storm.143 High winds on the backside of the storm downed a power line that sparked a small fire. The fire burned for several hours in Galloway Township, burning a total of 40 acres.144 A total of 8,486 residences were left without power in Connecticut and one boy was injured after high winds knocked down numerous power lines and tree limbs.145146 In Massachusetts, gusts up to downed several trees and cut power to residences in Middleborough, Boston, Brookline and Hingham.147 The Western Massachusetts Electric Company reported that at least 360 residences lost power during the storm.148 Moderate snow fell across portions of The Berkshires late on February 12 as the storm system began entering the Atlantic Ocean, with isolated areas received upwards of .149

Aftermath

In the wake of the tornado outbreak, 30 national guard troops and additional law enforcement officers were sent to Lone Grove to provide security.150151 The American Red Cross opened shelters in Ardmore, Edmond, and Oklahoma City. About 100 volunteers from the Sorghum Mill Estates Neighborhood Association traveled to affected areas in Edmond to help with cleanup efforts. A local restaurant provided free meals to the volunteers as they assisted relief workers.152 The Salvation Army had two canteens in Oklahoma City, one in Lone Grove, and one in Perkins.153 About 2,000 meals, drinks, and snacks were provided by the Salvation Army to emergency responders. Feed the Children also sent two truckloads of relief supplies to Carter County.154 They also sent three food trucks containing a total of 619 meals, 900 drinks, and about 500 snacks to Ardmore.155 Baptist Disaster Relief responded to Lone Grove to assist with feeding and emotional care.156 The Oklahoma City Thunder of the National Basketball Association (NBA) gave away two tickets to anyone who made a donation of $25 or an equivalent amount of food to benefit victims of the tornado.157 A concert featuring Chris Cagle and other local musicians at Heritage Hall in Ardmore raised over $18,000 to benefit Carter County tornado victims.158159 Another concert set up by the Salvation Army raised $2,300 more for victims.160 The Department of Homeland Security offered to assist with the reconstruction of homes and businesses and to support those who were left homeless. President Barack Obama passed along his condolences and best wishes to the victims of the tornado.161

The Governor of Oklahoma Brad Henry issued a state of emergency in 17 counties and described the damage in Lone Grove as the worst natural disaster he has seen since becoming governor.162 On February 15, President Obama approved Henry's request for federal assistance in Carter, Logan and Oklahoma counties.163 A disaster recovery center was set up by the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) on February 20. Homeowners were allowed to borrow $200,000 to repair damages to their home or find a temporary or permanent shelter. They were also allowed to borrow up to $40,000 to replaced lost property. Businesses were also allowed to borrow up to $2 million for damage repair, property loss, and economic loss.164 On February 22, the New Life Baptist Church sent drinks and prepackaged snacks, along with items needed for clean-up.165 By February 23, about $781,000 had been given in federal grants.166 On February 25, FEMA approved Governor Henry's request for public assistance in Carter, Coal and Love counties.167 The estimated cost to clear the 7,000 tons of debris in Lone Grove was placed at $500,000, of which $90,000 has been paid for by FEMA.168

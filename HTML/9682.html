The Walden<U+2013>Wallkill Rail Trail, also known as the Jesse McHugh Rail Trail, is a rail trail between the village of Walden in the town of Montgomery and the hamlet of Wallkill in the town of Shawangunk. Montgomery and Shawangunk are located in Orange and Ulster counties, respectively, in upstate New York.

The trail, like the Wallkill Valley Rail Trail to the north, is part of the former Wallkill Valley Railroad's rail corridor. The land was purchased by the towns of Montgomery and Shawangunk in 1985 and converted to a public trail. The portion of the trail in Shawangunk was formally opened in 1993 and named after former town supervisor Jesse McHugh. Plans to pave the trail between Walden and Wallkill were discussed since 2001, and the route was finally paved between 2008 and 2009. The trail also includes an unofficial, unimproved section to the north of Wallkill, and is bounded by NY 52 and NY 208.

History

Stretching from Montgomery to Kingston, the Wallkill Valley Railroad operated from 1866 until its last regular freight run on December 31, 1977. In the 1980s, Conrail, then the owner of the Wallkill Valley line, attempted to sell the former rail corridor. The towns of Montgomery and Shawangunk <U+2013> in Orange and Ulster counties, respectively <U+2013> purchased their sections of the rail line to allow "development of a commercial corridor [as well as] utility easements and access" to a local reservoir.1

The Montgomery section consisted of from the village of Walden to the town line with Shawangunk, and the Shawangunk section ran north from the town line to Birch Road. The purchases were completed in August and October 1985, respectively.2 In November of that year, the New York State Department of Correctional Services bought of the former corridor in Shawangunk's hamlet of Wallkill, near the Wallkill Correctional Facility. This portion extends from Birch Road to the town line with Gardiner.3 The Shawangunk Correctional Facility was built at that location.4 South of Walden, the corridor remains an active rail line operated by the Norfolk Southern Railway.56

North of the prisons, the former corridor continues as the separate Wallkill Valley Rail Trail. Rail trail enthusiasts have been trying to find a way to combine the two rail trails since the 1990s, and in 2004 the town of Shawangunk commissioned an open space study that identified possible ways to accomplish such a connection.7 A 2008 Ulster county transportation plan included projects to connect the trails.8

The portion of the former corridor running through the center of Wallkill was converted to a road, Railroad Avenue.9 The southern part of the route, running from Wallkill to the Montgomery<U+2013>Shawangunk town line, was officially opened as the Jesse McHugh Rail Trail on June 5, 1993. Jesse McHugh was a former Shawangunk town supervisor.10 The northern portion of the Shawangunk section, which stretches to the border of the prison grounds, is maintained by the town but not officially part of the trail.

In 2001, Shawangunk, Montgomery and the Walden began applying for over $600,00011 in TEA-21 grants to create a paved, ADA-accessible trail between Walden and Wallkill.12 The total cost of paving the trail was expected to be $750,000,13 though it eventually ballooned to $1.5 million.14 The decision to pave the trail was vehemently opposed by horseback riders who felt it would endanger them,15 and protested at several public meetings by the Mid-Hudson Horse Trails Association.16 The decision was also opposed by nearby homeowners who believed an increase in trail use would threaten their privacy.17

In October 2003, Walden, Shawangunk and Montgomery acquired the $600,000 grant needed to begin paving the trail.18 Two months later, Bob and Doris Kimball, a couple in Montgomery, donated of their land to create a park by the trail near Lake Osiris Road.19 The park is expected to be developed once funds are available to do so.20 Nearly $200,000 in funding to complete pavement of the trail was lost when the outgoing 109th Congress did not approve a 2006 budget bill.21 In February 2008, Congressman Maurice Hinchey announced the appropriation of $351,000 to complete the project.22 Construction began on September 22, 2008,23 and the paved 24 trail opened on May 2, 2009.25

Route

The trail begins at the Wooster Grove Park in the village of Walden, near NY 52.2627 There is a visitor center for rail trail users at the park. The park also contains Walden's former train station, which has since been renovated as a recreational facility.28

The trail continues north from the trailhead before reaching Lake Osiris Road, continuing another to the Montgomery<U+2013>Shawangunk town line. Once in Shawangunk, the trail passes by the Borden Estate,29 a mansion built in 1906 by the granddaughter of Gail Borden. In 1854, Gail Borden patented the process for creating condensed milk; the Borden family subsequently owned a series of milk companies. The mansion is now used by the School of Practical Philosophy for philosophy classes.30

About from the town line, the trail reaches its Wallkill trailhead bordering NY 208, directly across the street from the Shawangunk police station. The paved section between Walden and Wallkill is flat, with only a 3% grade.31 A portion of the former corridor in central Wallkill has since been converted to a road.

An unimproved northern section in Wallkill extends from the intersection of Railroad Avenue and C. E. Penny Drive to Birch Road. Birch Road marks the border between the former corridor and two state prisons. This section passes through private hunting grounds and is "unmarked [with] no signs" but is "arguably... the most scenic" portion of the former Wallkill Valley rail corridor, featuring "splendid" views of the Shawangunk Ridge to the west.

While the total length of the trail is officially only about ,32 the inclusion of the northern section increases its length to about .33 The trail is used for walking, jogging, bicycling and dog walking.
